Note that your integrand is an _even_ function. That might help.
(∫0∞e-4⁢x2⁢𝑑x)2=∫0∞e-4⁢y2⁢𝑑y⁢∫0∞e-4⁢x2⁢𝑑x=∫0π2𝑑θ⁢∫0∞r⁢e-4⁢r2⁢𝑑r. It's _not_ the right part of the plane--it's only the 1st quadrant.
If you want to carry out the idea without borrowing the answer, let θ go from -π/2 to π/2.

I'm not totally sure what "proof" you're talking about--I'm assuming it's the trick that the square of the integral can be written as a double integral in the familiar 2D x⁢y-plane then converted to an easy integral using polar coordinates:

(∫0∞e-4⁢x2⁢𝑑x)2=∫0∞e-4⁢x2⁢𝑑x⁢∫0∞e-4⁢y2⁢𝑑y=∫0∞∫0∞e-4⁢(x2+y2)⁢𝑑x⁢𝑑y

Before you convert to polar coordinates, you must understand that this region is only the first quadrant--which means the angle runs from 0 to π2 (as opposed to [0,2⁢π)--as is the case when it's -∞ to ∞).

Converting to polar coordinates gives:

∫0∞∫0∞e-4⁢(x2+y2)⁢𝑑x⁢𝑑y=∫0π2𝑑θ⁢∫0∞r⁢e-4⁢r2⁢𝑑r

The r-integral is easy through u-substitution and is 18. The θ-integral is independent of the r-integral so is just π2. Giving:

(∫0∞e-4⁢x2⁢𝑑x)2=π16

And thus:

∫0∞e-4⁢x2⁢𝑑x=π4

(just as can be retrieved from a u-substitution used from the result that ∫-∞∞e-x2⁢𝑑x=π).
