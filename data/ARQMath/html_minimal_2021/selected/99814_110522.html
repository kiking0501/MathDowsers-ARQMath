I'll explain my comment.

General stuff

For a Markov process on a countable state space and for a given state x, let us denote by τ the first return time in x starting from x. I also assume that the Markov process is transitive, so that what I say holds for the whole space (and not only for the single point x), otherwise things become a bit more complicated. There are then three possibilities:

  • τ has finite expectation. The Markov chain is said to be positive recurrent, and there exists an invariant probability measure μ. Asymptotically, the process spend a positive proportion of the time μ⁢(x) at site x. Examples of such Markov chains include transitive Markov chains of finite state spaces, and something I will present later.

  • τ is finite almost surely, but has infinite expectation. The Markov chain is said to be null recurrent. Asymptotically, the process spend a null proportion of the time at site x. Example of such Markov chains include "good" random walks on ℤ and ℤ2, and something I will present later (yes, the same something).

  • τ is infinite with positive probability. The Markov chain is said to be transient. Almost surely, at some time the process will leave x and never come again at x. Examples of such Markov chains include non-degenerate random walks on ℤd for d≥3.

Now, I'll present two classes of examples. The first has more far-reaching applications, while the second is more flexible (and has its fair share of applications).

Random walks

Let us consider a random walk (Sn) on ℤd starting from 0. Let (pi)i∈ℤd be the transition kernel, i.e. ℙ(S1=i)=pi. I'll denote by S its covariance matrix:

(u,S⁢v)=∑i∈ℤd(pi,u)⁢(pi,v).

We shall assume that the random walk has no drift, finite variance and is non-degenerate, i.e. that the matrix S is positive definite.

Then the random walk is transitive of a d-dimensional lattice. Note that the Markov process (Sn) can't be positive recurrent: asymptotically, the process spends roughly the same time at each point of the lattice, and since there are infinitely many of them, it can't spend a positive proportion of the time at any given point. Hence, the process is either null recurrent or transient. We indeed have the following result. If τ denote the first return time at 0, then:

  • If d=1, then:

ℙ⁢(τ>n)∼2⁢det⁡Sπ⁢1n.

  • If d=2, then:

ℙ⁢(τ>n)∼2⁢π⁢det⁡Sln⁡(n).

  • If d≥3, then the random walk is transient.

I am not totally sure I have the constants right, but at least the order of the growth in n is. A good reference is, for instance, Random walk in random and non-random environments by P. Révész. In the case of the simple random walk on ℤ (when p1=p-1=1/2), you can check the formula via Stirling formula and a generous helping of renewal theory. By being cunning, you can deduce the 2-dimensional case for the simple random walk from the 1-dimensional one.

Anyway, since we have the formula:

𝔼⁢(τ)=∑n∈ℕℙ⁢(τ>n),

both the 1-dimensional and the 2-dimensional cases are counter-examples.

Now, I'll give a more general (and more simple) class of examples: the "something I will present later".

Towers

Let μ be a probablity distribution over ℕ. I claim that there exists a Markov process over a countable state space and a state x such that, if τ is the first return in x starting from x, then the law of τ is μ.

Indeed, let us consider a Markov process (Xn) on ℕ such that:

  • From any k>0, if Xn=k then Xn+1=k-1;

  • ℙ(Xn+1=k|Xn=0)=μ({k}).

Then this Markov chain fulfills the conditions of my claim. You can see ℕ as a "tower"; then this process comes down one floor at a time, and after it reaches 0 it goes up at the floor k+1 (damn English labels for the floors...) with probablity μ⁢({k}). This construction is closely related to Rokhlin towers in ergodic theory.

This mean that you can find an example such that τ has any specifies distribution over the nonnegative integers. Crafting examples for which τ is almost surely finite but has finite expectation becomes easy.
