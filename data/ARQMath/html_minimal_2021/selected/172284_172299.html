It is a little bit tedious to establish that the Gaussian Integers form a Euclidean domain, but what could be more elegant than the fact that rational primes p such that p≡3 (mod 4) remain prime in the Gaussian integers,while if p=2 or p≡1 mod 4, there are unique positive integers a≤b such that p=a2+b2, and a+b⁢i is a prime element of the Gaussian integers. Furthermore, up to multiplication by a unit, these two cases determine all the primes in the Gaussian integers.

Lattice theorem for rings: For any ring homomorphism ψ:A→B and any ideal I of B, the set ψ-1⁢(I) is an ideal of A. This map of ideals is inclusion-preserving, i.e. if I⊆J are ideals of B, then ψ-1⁢(I)⊆ψ-1⁢(J). Furthermore, for any prime ideal P⊂B, we have that ψ-1⁢(P) is a prime ideal of A.

We have an inclusion homomorphism j:ℤ→ℤ⁢[i]. Our strategy is to classify the (non-zero) prime ideals P of ℤ⁢[i] according to the "value" of j-1⁢(P)=P∩ℤ.

Consider the ring homomorphism ev:ℤ⁢[x]→ℤ⁢[i] defined by ϕ⁢(f)=f⁢(i). This function is surjective, and the kernel of ev is the ideal (x2+1) of ℤ⁢[x]. Therefore, by the first isomorphism theorem, ev descends to an isomorphism ϕ:R→ℤ⁢[i], where R=ℤ⁢[x]/(x2+1).

For the remainder of this post, we will now be interested in classifying the prime ideals of R. The lattice theorem guarantees that ϕ sets up a bijective, order-preserving correspondence between the prime ideals of the two rings. I can explain more about why this is okay if you'd like.

Consider the inclusion homomorphism k:ℤ→R (of course k=ϕ-1∘j); we will pretend for the sake of expediency that ℤ is actually contained as a subset of R.

For each (non-zero) prime ideal (q) of ℤ, we want to classify the prime ideals Q of R such that k-1⁢(Q)=(q), i.e. the prime ideals Q of R that contain (q). Note that a prime ideal Q of R will contain the ℤ-ideal (q) if and only if Q contains the element q, which is the case if and only if Q contains the R-ideal q⁢R. By the lattice theorem, the prime ideals of R containing q⁢R are in order-preserving bijection with the prime ideals of R/q⁢R=(ℤ⁢[x]/(x2+1))/q⁢(ℤ⁢[x]/(x2+1))≅ℤ⁢[x]/(q,x2+1)≅𝔽q⁢[x]/(x2+1). If q=2, then in 𝔽2 we have x2+1=(x+1)2, so that R/2⁢R≅𝔽2⁢[x]/(x+1)2 has one prime ideal, namely (x+1)⁢𝔽2⁢[x]/(x+1)2. Unwinding our various isomorphisms, this corresponds to i+1 in ℤ⁢[i].

If q≡1mod4, then x2+1 is reducible (by quadratic reciprocity there is some a∈𝔽q such that a2=-1 in 𝔽q), so that R/q⁢R≅𝔽q⁢[x]/(x-a)⁢(x+a) which has two prime ideals, (x-a)⁢𝔽q⁢[x]/(x-a)⁢(x+a) and (x+a)⁢𝔽q⁢[x]/(x-a)⁢(x+a). Unwinding our various isomorphisms, this corresponds to a conjugate pair of Gaussian primes π, π¯ with norm q≡1mod4 in ℤ⁢[i].

Lastly, if q≡3mod4, then x2+1 is irreducible (by quadratic reciprocity there is no a∈𝔽q such that a2=-1 in 𝔽q), so that R/q⁢R≅𝔽q⁢[x]/(x2+1)≅𝔽q2 is a field and therefore has one prime ideal, namely the zero ideal. Unwinding our various isomorphisms, this corresponds to a Gaussian prime q≡3mod4 that lives in ℤ.

Needless to say, this method is well-known and not original to me; for example I'm pretty sure that Neukirch does precisely this at the outset of his book.
