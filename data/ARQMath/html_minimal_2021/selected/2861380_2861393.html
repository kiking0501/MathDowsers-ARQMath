@b00nheT The derivative in your example will be zero almost everywhere, but will not exist at the points of discontinuity. A function is always continuous where it is differentiable.

The following statement holds: f : \mathbb ⁢ R → \mathbb ⁢ R ℝ Moreover, it may be that the parts of the piecewise are not differentiable at the boundary of the sets on which they are defined but the piecewise function itself is.
