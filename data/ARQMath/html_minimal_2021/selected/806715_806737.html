Why does manipulating the expression help?

Consider the limit limx→1⁡x2-1x-1 Plugging in 1 for x will yield 00, which is no good. However, if we simplify the expression, we get: limx→1⁡x2-1x-1=limx→1⁡(x+1) Now, plugging in 1 yields the answer 2. My question is: why on earth should simplifying the expression help us find the limit? The two expressions above are absolutely equivalent. I.e., if I plug in, say 10 in (x+3)2 and x2+6⁢x+9, I get the same answer - 169.

real-analysis limits
The two expressions are not completely equivalent. The first expression has a singularity at x=1, the second does not. The clever thing is that both expressions are equivalent on all points *except* x=1, but by the definition of limit, the actual value at x=1 is irrelevant. That's why the trick works.
To be clear, limx→1⁡x2-1x-1 does indeed evaluate to 2. It is x2-1x-1 which is undefined at x=1.
As @becko said, the first equation is not defined for x=1. When you are taking the limit, you look at numbers that are arbitrarily close to 1, so therefore you are justified in cancelling out the terms in the numerator and denominator.

First, you need to ask yourself why plugging a number in the expression works? Precisely speaking, you need to figure out the condition under which the following expression holds limx→a⁡f⁢(x)=f⁢(a)

The answer is the continuity. In other words, for a continuous function f:ℝ→ℝ, we have limx→a⁡f⁢(x)=f⁢(a) ∀a∈ℝ

Now, is g⁢(x)=x2-1x-1 continuous in ℝ? Obviously not, because g is not even defined on x=1. Therefore, plugging a number will never works for g.

Second, why simplifying a expression works? The answer is very simple: you can always simplify a expression as long as you can. Besides, limit of a function at x=1 is only a behavior of a function in the neighborhood of x=1

Therefore, to calculate the limit limx→1⁡x2-1x-1 we first note that (x2-1)/(x-1) is not continuous at x=1. Therefore, plugging x=1 in the expression will not work. Then, in the neighborhood of x=1, g is equivalent to (x+1), we simplify the expression and get limx→1⁡(x+1) Now, is (x+1) continuous at x=1? Of course, it is! Now, you can plug x=1 in (x+1) to get the final answer.
