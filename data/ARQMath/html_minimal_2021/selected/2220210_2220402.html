Yes, differentiability of h implies continuity of h. But you're asked to prove the continuity of h′ at c, not of h.
|x-c|<δ⟹ |h'(x)-h'(c)|<ϵ
Since you're assuming that limx→c⁡h′⁢(x) exists, why don't you give it a name? Then assume it's different from h′⁢(c) and try to derive a contradiction. You should start by drawing a picture to try to see why this situation isn't possible. Bear in mind the mean-value theorem.

This is a standard property of derivatives: derivatives can't have jump discontinuity. In other words if both f′⁢(c) and limx→c⁡f′⁢(x) exist then they are equal and hence f′ is continuous at c.

To prove the above property we can use either Mean Value Theorem or Darboux Theorem. First let's use the mean value theorem. We have \begin{align} f'(c) &= \lim_{h \to 0}\frac{f(c + h) - f(c)}{h}\notag\\ &= \lim_{h \to 0}f'(c_h)\text{ (for some }c_h\text{ between }c, c + h)\notag\\ &= \lim_{x \to c}f'(x)\notag\\ \end{align}

Next we use Darboux theorem which says that derivatives possess intermediate value property. Let f′⁢(c)=A≠B=limx→c⁡f′⁢(x). Let's take the case when A<B. By choosing ϵ=(B-A)/2 and using limit limx→c⁡f′⁢(x)=B we can ensure that there is an h>0 such that f′⁢(x)>B-ϵ=A+ϵ for all x∈(c-h,c+h). Thus in the interval (c-h,c+h) there is a value of f′ less than A+ϵ namely f′⁢(c) and also as noted above there are values of f′ greater than A+ϵ. Hence by intermediate value property f′ must take the value A+ϵ in this interval. But this is not possible because of equation (1) and hence we reach a contradiction. If A>B then we can just interchange the roles of A,B in above argument. Hence we must have A=B and then f′ is continuous at c.

Note: I have used letter f for the function instead of h as given in question. When dealing with just one function f appears to be a natural choice and I keep the answer that way.
