First: the fact that the identity a⁢b=a⁢b is only valid for nonnegative reals a,b does not mean that it is always false for other numbers.

But let's see the proof of the identity to see where it's essencial that a and b are nonnegative:

(a⁢b)2=(a)2⁢(b)2=a⁢b Therefore, a⁢b=a⁢b.

Now, let's do this for a=b=-1:

(-1⁢-1)2=(-1)2⁢(-1)2=(-1)⁢(-1) Therefore, (-1)⁢(-1)=-1⁢-1.

The problem arises in the conclusion. The definition of says: x=y⇔y2=x⁢ and ⁢y≥0

The last condition is essential. Many books for high school student say that 4 has "two solutions" but this is totally wrong. What has two solutions is the equation x2=4, but 4 is 2 and not, ever, -2.

In the fallacy we are saying in essence that (-1)⁢(-1)=-1⁢-1, but this is wrong because -1⁢-1<0.
