Unique decimal representation using only 0s and 1s (base 10)

Say x∈[0,1) and x=∑i=1∞ai10i for ai∈{0,1}. Is it true if x=∑i=1∞bi10i for bi∈{0,1} that ai=bi for all i?

It seems to me that yes this is true, since the only times I have seen uniqueness of decimal representation break down is when we introduce repeating 9s. Is there a simple proof of my statement above?

EDIT: I added base 10 to the subject to clarify I don't want to consider ∑i=1∞ai2i. I am not sure if that is the correct terminology.

I want to establish f:{0,1}∞→ℝ by (a1,a2,a3,⋯)↦0.a1⁢a2⁢a3⁢⋯ is an injection.

Yes, this is true. It's enough to look at the first digit they do not have in common and the next digit after that to show that they cannot be equal.

Note that we have 0=x-x=∑i=1∞ai10i-∑i=1∞bi10i=∑i=1∞ai-bi10i Note that you are allowed to rearrange terms since |ai|+|bi|≤2 and the sequence ∑i|ai|+|bi|10i is absolutely convergent bounded above by 2⁢x. Hence, we have 0=∑i=1∞ci10i where ci∈{-1,0,1}. Our goal now is to show that all the ci's are 0. If not, look at the first non-zero ck. Note that ∑i=k+1∞|ci|10i≤∑i=k+1∞110i=110k+1⁢(11-1/10)=19⋅10k<110k. Hence, |ck10k|=110k>∑i=k+1∞|ci|10i. Hence, |∑i=1∞ci10i|=|ck10k-(-∑i=k+1∞|ci|10i)|≥|ck10k|-|(∑i=k+1∞|ci|10i)|&gt;0 Hence, if 0=∑i=1∞ci10i⟹ci=0, which in-turn gives us that ai=bi.
