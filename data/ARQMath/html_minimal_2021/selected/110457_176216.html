Closed form for ∫0∞xn1+xm⁢𝑑x

I've been looking at

∫0∞xn1+xm⁢𝑑x

It seems that it always evaluates in terms of sin⁡X and π, where X is to be determined. For example:

∫0∞x11+x3⁢𝑑x=π3⁢1sin⁡π3=2⁢π3⁢3

∫0∞x11+x4⁢𝑑x=π4

∫0∞x21+x5⁢𝑑x=π5⁢1sin⁡2⁢π5

So I guess there must be a closed form - the use of Γ⁢(x)⁢Γ⁢(1-x) first comess to my mind because of the π⁢xsin⁡π⁢x appearing. Note that the arguments are always the ratio of the exponents, like 14, 13 and 25. Is there any way of finding it? I'll work on it and update with any ideas.

B R Pointed out that the integral is in Gradshteyn/Ryshik. I'd like to add that Mathematica finds for the integral in almost no time the result πm⁢csc⁡(π⁢(n+1)m).

Contour Integration Approach

Assuming only that m>0 and -1<n<m-1, let z=xm with the exaggerated contour γ:

γ is actually tight above and below the positive real axis and around the origin and circles back at an arbitrarily large distance from the origin.

The part just above the positive real axis captures the integral. The part just below the positive real axis gets -e2⁢π⁢i⁢n-m+1m times the integral. The residue at z=-1 of zn-m+1m1+z is eπ⁢i⁢n-m+1m. Putting this all together yields

1m⁢∫γzn-m+1m1+z⁢dz=(1-e2⁢π⁢i⁢n-m+1m)⁢∫0∞xn1+xm⁢dx 2⁢π⁢im⁢eπ⁢i⁢n-m+1m=(1-e2⁢π⁢i⁢n-m+1m)⁢∫0∞xn1+xm⁢dx πm=sin⁡(π⁢n+1m)⁢∫0∞xn1+xm⁢dx πm⁢csc⁡(π⁢n+1m)=∫0∞xn1+xm⁢dx

Relation to 𝚪⁢(α)⁢𝚪⁢(𝟏-α)

Setting m=1, n=α-1, and using the substitution s=t⁢u yields Euler's Reflection Formula: Γ⁢(α)⁢Γ⁢(1-α)amp;=∫0∞sα-1e-sds∫0∞t-αe-tdtamp;=∫0∞∫0∞uα-1e-(t⁢u+t)dudtamp;=∫0∞uα-11+uduamp;=πcsc(πα)

Another Proof of (𝟏) ∫0∞xα-11+x⁢dxamp;=∫01x-α+xα-11+xdxamp;=∑k=0∞(-1)k∫01(xk-α+xk+α-1)dxamp;=∑k=0∞(-1)k(1k-α+1+1k+α)amp;=∑k∈ℤ(-1)kk+αamp;=πcsc(πα) where the last step of (3) is proven in (3) of this answer. Then apply (3) to get ∫0∞xn1+xm⁢dxamp;=1m∫0∞xn-m+1m1+xdxamp;=πmcsc(πn+1m)
