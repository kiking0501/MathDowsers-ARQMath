If (cn) is any sequence with period k (that is, cn+k=cn) then it's possible to evaluate ∑cn/n! using tricks involving k-th roots of unity.

Let ω=e2⁢π⁢i/k. Consider the k sequences

s0:1,1,1⁢…

s1:1,ω,ω2,ω3,…

s2:1,ω2,ω4,ω6,…

s3:1,ω3,ω6,ω9,…

...

sk-1:1,ωk-1,ω2⁢(k-1),….

Using tricks analogous to finding Fourier coefficients you can find a0,…⁢ak-1 so that (cn)=a0⁢(s0)+⋯+ak⁢(sk-1).

Hence ∑cnn!=∑j=0k-1aj⁢∑nωj⁢nn!=∑j=0k-1aj⁢eωj.

If you do that for your sequence you get ∑n=0∞1(k⁢n)!=1k⁢∑j=0k-1eωj.

Edit: Thomas Andrews makes a comment that I should have included: Since the original sum is real, it follows that ∑n=0∞1(k⁢n)!=1k⁢∑j=0k-1ecos⁡2⁢π⁢j/k⁢cos⁡(sin⁡2⁢π⁢j/k).

Edit: If one is familiar with "abstract harmonic analysis", here just harmonic analysis on compact abelian groups, one sees that those "tricks analogous to finding Fourier coefficients" are in fact finding Fourier coefficients for a certain function on the group ℤ/k⁢ℤ.

And, since the imaginary part is necessarily zero, this also gives the formula:1k⁢∑j=0k-1ecos⁡2⁢π⁢j/k⁢cos⁡(sin⁡2⁢π⁢j/k)
