Rough Sketch: Since we are given that G is a finite commutative group, then this means that G is abelian. Suppose that I have a commutative group G such that G=a,b,c⁢…,, where Id ∈G. Here, I⁢d stands for the identity element, since this is what is required of a commutative group. So the order of the elements of G is the smallest positive integer, say k, such that for an element a, ak=I⁢d. That is as far as I got.

Here is an argument that reduces the problem to when the exponent is a prime power.

Let e be the exponent of G. If e=a⁢b with gcd⁡(a,b)=1 and a,b>1, let A={g∈G:ga=1} and B={g∈G:gb=1}. Then by induction you have elements α∈A of order a and β∈B of order b. Then α⁢β has order a⁢b=e.
