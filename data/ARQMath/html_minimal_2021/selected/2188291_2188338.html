I think it is best to reserve ∥A∥2 for the usual 2-norm ∥A∥2=sup∥x∥2=1⁡∥A⁢x∥2.

You can use the singular value decomposition: there exist unitary matrices U and V and a matrix S with singular values of A along the diagonal and zeros elsewhere satisfying A=U⁢S⁢V.

Suppose that x is a vector with ∥x∥2=1. Then you have ∥A⁢x∥2=∥U⁢S⁢V⁢x∥2=∥S⁢V⁢x∥2≤∥S∥2⁢∥V⁢x∥2=∥S∥2. Because of its particular form, the 2-norm of S is its largest entry. Since its nonzero entries are the singular values of A, this means ∥S∥2=σ1⁢(A) so that ∥A∥2≤σ1⁢(A).

The fact that ∥A∥∞≤n⁢∥A∥2 is easy to prove, giving you 1n⁢∥A∥∞≤σ1⁢(A).
