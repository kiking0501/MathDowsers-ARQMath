The Carmichael function allows to generalize the Euler-theorem. It is a bit more complicated because we cannot always reduce modulo λ⁢(n). If the modulus is coprime to the base, this can be done. In this case, the numbers 1,a,a2,⋯,aλ⁢(n) actually form a subgroup of the group consisting of 1,a,a2,⋯,aϕ⁢(n)

The Carmichael-function cannot exceed the totient-function because it determines the smallest exponent with the desired congruence, hence it must be a divisor of the totient-function with the same argument. In short, we have λ⁢(n)|ϕ⁢(n) for all n. Equality is possible.

Euler's function ϕ⁢(n) gives the order of the multiplicative group (\Z/n⁢\Z)×, whereas Carmichael's function gives the exponent of this group, see fifth and sixth sentence of https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Torsion_group .

Let U⁢(n) be the group of units mod n.

Euler's totient function gives the order of U⁢(n). Euler's theorem is Lagrange's theorem for U⁢(n).

Carmichael's function gives the exponent of U⁢(n), by definition.

Every finite abelian group A contains a cyclic subgroup whose order is the exponent of A. This subgroup correspond to the largest invariant factor of A.
