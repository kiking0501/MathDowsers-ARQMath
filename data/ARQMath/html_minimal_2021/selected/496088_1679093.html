A proof by Fourier transform

This won't be the shortest nor the most elementary proof here, but it has a nice background which I'll explain later. The main drawback is that it assumes that we know the Fourier transform of the Gaussian, which is about as hard to compute rigorously than the initial integral.

Fix A=(A+B)-B. Then, for any ξ∈ℝ,

∫ℝei⁢ξ⁢b⁢I⁢(a,b2)⁢𝑑bamp;=∫ℝei⁢ξ⁢b∫0+∞e-a⁢x2-b2x2dxdbamp;=∫0+∞e-a⁢x2∫ℝei⁢ξ⁢be-b2x2dbdx  \color#00fFubini-Tonelliamp;=π∫0+∞xe-(a+ξ24)⁢x2dx  \color#00fFourier transform of the Gaussianamp;=π2∫0+∞e-(a+ξ24)⁢udu  \color#00fChange of variables u=x2amp;=π2⁢(a+ξ24)  \color#00fEvaluation of the integralamp;=π2⁢a(12⁢a+i⁢ξ+12⁢a-i⁢ξ)  \color#00fPartial fraction decompositionamp;=π2⁢a(∫0+∞e-(i⁢ξ+2⁢a)⁢bdb+∫0+∞e-(-i⁢ξ+2⁢a)⁢bdb)amp;=π2⁢a∫ℝei⁢ξ⁢be-2⁢a⁢|b|db.

Hence, I⁢(a,b2) and π2⁢a⁢e-2⁢a⁢|b| have the same Fourier transform. Then you can check that both are continuous and decay exponentially fast at infinity, so they are pointwise the inverse Fourier transform of their Fourier transform, and thus coincide for all b. Finally, we get:

I⁢(a,b)=π2⁢a⁢e-2⁢a⁢b.

Motivation: a probabilistic proof

I stumbled upon this integral during my research (or rather, a family of such integrals). Let X and Y be two independent random variables, with Y being exponential ℰ⁢(λ) and Z being standard normal 𝒩⁢(0,1). Let X:=Z⁢Y.

A first computation yields the density of the distribution of X. Let f be continuous and bounded on ℝ. Then:

𝔼⁢(f⁢(X))amp;=λσ⁢2⁢π∫0+∞∫ℝf(zy)e-z22e-λ⁢ydzdyamp;=λ2⁢π∫0+∞e-λ⁢y∫ℝ1yf(b)e-b22⁢ydbdy  \color#00fChange of variables b=zyamp;=λ2⁢π∫ℝf(b)∫0+∞e-λ⁢yye-b22⁢ydydb  \color#00fFubini-Tonelliamp;=λ⁢2π∫ℝf(b)∫0+∞e-λ⁢x2e-b22⁢x2dxdb  \color#00fChange of variables x=y

The density of X is thus given by the function:

b↦λ⁢2π⁢∫0+∞e-λ⁢x2-b22⁢x2⁢𝑑x.

And then, if we compute this integral, we can get a simpler expression for the density. However, it turns out that there is an easier way to compute the distribution of X, with the Fourier transform. Indeed, for all real ξ,

𝔼⁢(ei⁢ξ⁢X)amp;=𝔼(ei⁢ξ⁢Z⁢Y)amp;=𝔼(𝔼(ei⁢(ξ⁢Y)⁢Z|Y))amp;=𝔼(e-ξ2⁢Y2)amp;=11+ξ22⁢λ,

which is the Fourier transform of a Laplace distribution of parameter 1/2⁢λ. With the argument of continuity given in the first paragraph, we get:

λ⁢2π⁢∫0+∞e-λ⁢x2-b22⁢x2⁢𝑑x=λ2⁢e-2⁢λ⁢|b|,

so that:

∫0+∞e-λ⁢x2-b22⁢x2⁢𝑑x=π2⁢λ⁢e-2⁢λ⁢|b|.

The proof in the first paragraph is the streamlined version of this reasoning. All in all, an interpretation of this integral is that the product of a centered Gaussian and of the square root of an independent exponential random variable is a Laplace random variable.
