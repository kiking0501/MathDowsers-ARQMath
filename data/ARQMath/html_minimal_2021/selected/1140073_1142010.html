The matrix p-norm is the operator norm: ∥A∥p=maxx≠0⁡∥A⁢x∥p∥x∥p. The fact that ∥A∥2=ρ⁢(A*⁢A) and ∥A∥∞=maxi⁢∑j|ai⁢j| is just the consequence of (1). The 2-norm and ∞-norm satisfy [2a⁢m⁢p;1-1a⁢m⁢p;0], for any n-vector x and the fact that ∥A∥2≤n⁢∥A∥∞ follows by plugging (3) to (1): ∥A∥2=maxx≠0⁡∥A⁢x∥2∥x∥2≤maxx≠0⁡n⁢∥A⁢x∥∞∥x∥2≤maxx≠0⁡n⁢∥A⁢x∥∞∥x∥∞=n⁢∥A∥∞.

Assuming that you know nothing about (1) and take (2) as the definitions (such an approach in a class is a bit odd because it makes showing a lot of things quite complicated), you can proceed as follows:

  1. You show that the matrix ∞-norm is consistent with the vector ∞-norm, that is, ∥A⁢x∥∞≤∥A∥∞⁢∥x∥∞ holds for all vectors x. You can see that this is a trivial consequence of (1) but since you do not know about (1) you need to do it "by hand".
  2. From (4), you have that ∥A∥22=ρ⁢(AT⁢A)≤∥AT⁢A∥∞. Since there is an eigenvector x and an eigenvalue λ of AT⁢A such that λ=ρ⁢(AT⁢A) (yes, all eigenvalues of AT⁢A are nonnegative but that is not important here), AT⁢A⁢x=λ⁢x gives us that ∥λ⁢x∥∞=∥AT⁢A⁢x∥∞ and hence λ⁢∥x∥∞=∥AT⁢A⁢x∥∞≤(4)∥AT⁢A∥∞⁢∥x∥∞, which by cancelling the ∥x∥∞ gives ρ⁢(AT⁢A)=λ≤∥AT⁢A∥∞.
  3. Finally, one must show that ∥AT⁢A∥∞≤n⁢∥A∥∞2. This is easy: ∥AT⁢A∥∞amp;=maxi∑j|(ATA)i⁢j|=maxi∑j|∑kai⁢kak⁢j|≤maxi∑j∑k|ai⁢kak⁢j|amp;≤\colorredmaxi,j|ai⁢j|\colorbluemaxi∑j∑k|ai⁢k|. In the last inequality, we bounded |ak⁢j| by the maximum over the absolute values of all entries in A (which is clearly OK). Now the red term can be bounded from above by ∥A∥∞, while the blue term equals n⁢∥A∥∞ (notice the terms in the sum over j no longer depend on j). Hence, indeed, ∥AT⁢A∥∞≤n⁢∥A∥∞2 which together with the result of Item 2 gives ∥A∥22=ρ⁢(AT⁢A)≤∥AT⁢A∥∞≤n⁢∥A∥∞2.
