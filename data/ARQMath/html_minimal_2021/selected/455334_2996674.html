A question about odd perfect numbers

Edit [in response to a comment from anon]: Hereinafter, N is a positive integer, σ⁢(N) is the sum-of-divisors of N, ω⁢(N) is the number of distinct prime factors of N, and Ω⁢(N) is the number of prime factors of N (counting multiplicities).

Thus, N is a perfect number if σ⁢(N)=2⁢N.

A 2013 preprint by Nielsen claims to have proved that ω⁢(N)≥10 if N is an odd perfect number. A paper by Ochem and Rao containing inequalities relating Ω⁢(N) and ω⁢(N) for N an odd perfect number has been recently accepted in the Mathematics of Computation. The state-of-the-art result for Ω⁢(N) remains to be Hare's Ω⁢(N)≥75.

[Edit - August 29] The state-of-the-art result for Ω⁢(N) (where N is an odd perfect number) is now Ochem and Rao's Ω⁢(N)≥101. [End edit]

(This is only a partial answer. This approach considers the largest prime factor of N (instead of the smallest) when computing a bound for Ω⁢(N).)

If N=∏i=1ω⁢(N)piαi 𝑖𝑠 an odd perfect number (where the pi's are primes ordered in increasing magnitude and the αi's are all positive), then

N≥32⁢piαi⁢σ⁢(piαi).

(See Theorem 4.2.5, page 112 in this M.Sc. thesis.)

(Let r=ω⁢(N).) In particular, suppose i=r. (That is, let pr be the largest prime factor of N.) Then we have

prαr⁢∏i=1r-1piαi=N=∏i=1ω⁢(N)piαi≥32⁢prαr⁢σ⁢(prαr)>32⁢pr2⁢αr,

from which it follows that

∏i=1r-1pr-1αi≥∏i=1r-1piαi>32⁢prαr.

But we also have

prΩ⁢(N)-αr>pr-1Ω⁢(N)-αr=∏i=1r-1pr-1αi≥∏i=1r-1piαi>32⁢prαr>prαr,

from which we obtain

Ω⁢(N)>2⁢αr.
