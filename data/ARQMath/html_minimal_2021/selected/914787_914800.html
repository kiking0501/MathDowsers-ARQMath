Exotic proofs of ∑j=0n-1(p+jp)=(p+np+1)

Let p,n be positive integers.

The following identity ∑j=0n-1(p+jp)=(p+np+1) may be proved by induction or by successive uses of Pascal's rule (both techniques are demonstrated here).

Question

Is there a combinatorial proof for this identity ? Do you know other proofs ?

combinatorics summation

This proof can be viewed as the combinatorial explanation of "successive Pascal's rule".

Suppose we want to choose p+1 numbers a1<a2<⋯<ap+1 out of {1,…,p+n}. It is clear that ap+1 can only be in {p+1,p+2,⋯,p+n}. For each value ap+1=p+j in {p+1,p+2,…,p+n}, there are (p+j-1p) choices of a1<a2<⋯<ap. Add all the cases one get the required identity.
