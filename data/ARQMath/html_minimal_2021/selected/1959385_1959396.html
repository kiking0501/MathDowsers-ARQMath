In classical logic, the logical connectives are truth-functional, i.e. defined by their truth-table.

With them, we may easily verify that the two formulae :

P→Q and ¬⁢P∨Q

are equivalent.

If you are not "at ease" with this result, you are not alone: in Intuitionsitic Logic this result is not provable.

In effect, this logic does not "agree" on the truth-functional definition of the connectives.

In the context of "classical logic" the best "intuition I can suggest you is to reflect how the → connective is used in mathematical reasoning by way of modus ponens rule of inference.

This rule licenses us to infer B from A and A→B.

We "apply" it asserting the premises :A→B and A.

The first assertion excludes the case when A is true and B false, while the second assertion excludes the two cases where A is false.

Thus, the truth-table fo → leaves us only one possibility : B true, and this is what we need in order to conclude the proof.

The same happens with the "disjunction version" of the same rule :

that licenses us to infer B from A and ¬⁢A∨B.

In words : if we know that at least one between ¬⁢A and B holds and if we know that A holds (i.e. ¬⁢A does not), we have to conclude that B must hold.
