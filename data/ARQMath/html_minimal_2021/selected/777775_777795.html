This becomes a special case of the [Vandermonde identity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vandermonde%27s_identity) if you replace one of the factors (nk) by (nn-k). Also, it is a duplicate of at least [this question](http://math.stackexchange.com/q/677307), if not of many others.

[EDIT]

The idea behind double counting arguments is to provide verbal proofs of combinatorial identities that give an intuitive understanding of why they should be so. By simply showing that two expressions measure different ways to count the same thing it becomes clear to even a novice mathematician that they must be equivalent.

On the left hand side: (2⁢nn) is the count of ways to divide a set of distinct objects into two equal sized subsets.

On the right hand side: ∑k=0n(nk)2 is the count of ways to redistribute objects between two equal sized sets as follows:

(nk) counts the ways to select k objects form a set of n distinct objects. Thus (nk)2 counts the ways to do this for two sets; which makes it the count of ways to transfer k object from one set to the other in exchange for k objects originally in the second set. The summation is thus the count of ways to exchange equal numbers of distinct objects between two equal sized sets, for all possible size of the exchanges.

It should be clear that one gets the same result by simply combining the two sets and counting all the ways to split them into equal sized sets.

Hence it follows that: (2⁢nn)=∑k=0n(nk)2

Counting two ways, ∑(nk)⁢(mn-k)=(n+mn)
