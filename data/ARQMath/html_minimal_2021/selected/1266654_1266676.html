Why isn't Euler's theorem working to find the smallest k such that 10k≡1(mod9)?

10k≡1(mod9)

According to Euler's theorem and the Carmichael function, smallest k is ϕ⁢(9)=6, but clearly the smallest k is k=1.

What am I doing wrong?

Both λ⁢(n) (Carmichael function) and ϕ⁢(n) work like f here: (a,n)=1⇒af⁢(n)≡1(modn), except that λ⁢(n) may be smaller (it is defined as the smallest f⁢(n) that satisfies this statement). The smallest k in your question is called the _order_, denoted ord9⁢10, and λ⁢(n),ϕ⁢(n) don't talk about it. It is easy to prove that the order divides any l such that al≡1(modn), i.e. ord9⁢10∣l (assume not-then l=h⁢(ordn⁢a)+r with 0<r<ordn⁢a and al≡ar≡1(modn), contr).
So in particular ordn⁢a∣ϕ⁢(n),λ⁢(n).

The smallest solution is a divisor of φ⁢(n).

Note that the Carmichael Function gives the smallest solution of ak≡1(modn) that works for all a coprime with n.

So it stands to reason that the Carmichael function for n=9 won't be 1 since, for instance, taking a=2 we get 21≢1(mod9).

The equation you have is a special case. Note that 10=9+1 so

10k=(9+1)k=9k+(k1)⁢9k-1+…+(kk-1)⁢9+1≡1(mod9)

So any k is a solution. Generally the smallest solution for (a+1)k≡1(moda) is k=1 for any positive integer a.
