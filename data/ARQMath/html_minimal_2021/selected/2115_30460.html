There are no functions continuous on precisely the rationals, since the irrationals are not an Fσ set.

I think that there can be no such function.

Suppose that f is discontinuous at some point q. There must be constants A,B so that f⁢(s)<A<B<f⁢(t) holds for points s,t arbitrarily near to q.

Suppose that x0<q satisfies q-x0<B-A. Choose sequences sn,tn>x0 converging to q and satisfying f⁢(sn)<A<B<f⁢(tn). Then we have

f⁢(tn)-f⁢(x0)tn-x0-f⁢(sn)-f⁢(x0)sn-x0>B-f⁢(x0)tn-x0-A-f⁢(x0)sn-x0→B-Aq-x0>1

and may conclude that there exist s,t as close to q as we like satisfying

f⁢(t)-f⁢(x0)t-x0-f⁢(s)-f⁢(x0)s-x0>1

Arguing similarly when x0>q and trivially (since the Newton quotients will be unbounded as a result of the discontinuity) when x0=q we get the same conclusion with only |x0-q|<B-A.

For n a positive integer let Xn denote all of the points x0 in 𝐑 for which there exist s,t at distance less than 1/n from x0 satisfying the preceding inequality. Our argument implies that, for every n, Xn is a neighbourhood of every point q at which f is discontinuous (consider the points x0 whose distance from q is less than 1/n and B-A=Bq-Aq). If it happens that the points of discontinuity are dense (as in the case of 𝐐) then this implies that every Xn contains an open dense set and thus ⋂nXn is second category in 𝐑.

The point is of course that no point at which f is differentiable can be in all the Xn so if f is discontinuous on the rationals then f is differentiable on at most a set of 1st category in 𝐑 (which the irrationals are not).
