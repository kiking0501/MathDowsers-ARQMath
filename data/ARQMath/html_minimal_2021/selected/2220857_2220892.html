With regard to your third question, if I understand what you're asking, consider the following. It is always true that ¬⁢p∨p. Now, since if p is true, then q is true, we can rewrite the first assertion as ¬⁢p∨q.
For your third point, I would submit that what is counterintuitive is not how the → connective works; it is simply pragmatic that we often need a connective with that truth table, especially for putting below a universal quantifier, and → is a perfectly good mnemonic symbol for that; p→q is "the thing that converts knowledge of p (if/when we acquire that knowledge) to knowledge of q". What you find counterintuitive is _explaining_ that particular truth table as "If p then q".
I'm puzzled by what you mean by "sequent form, with its heavy use of implication rules" -- on the contrary, using sequent calculus as your proof system _downplays_ the importance of the → connective considerably relative to Hilbert systems (where you can't do _anything_ without a truckload of implications) or even natural deduction.

Anyway, here are some further equivalence rules for the conditional:

¬⁡(p→q)≡p∧¬⁢q (Negation Implication)

p→q≡¬⁢q→¬⁢p (Contraposition)

p→(q→r)≡(p∧q)→r (Exportation)

p→(q∧r)≡(p→q)∧(p→r) (left Distribution → over ∧)

(p∨q)→r≡(p→r)∧(q→r) (right 'distribution' ... But not really since the ∨ becomes a ∧)
