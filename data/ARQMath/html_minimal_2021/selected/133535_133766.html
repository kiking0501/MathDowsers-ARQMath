Lemma Let 𝔭 be a prime ideal of ℚ⁢[x1,…,xn]. Let F/ℚ be a finite Galois extension. Let G⁢a⁢l⁢(F/ℚ)={σ1,…,σf}. Let S={𝔭i} be the set of prime ideals of F⁢[x1,…,xn] which are lying over 𝔭. Then G=G⁢a⁢l⁢(F/ℚ) may act transitively on S. In particular, S is finite.

Proof First, G acts on S. Suppose σ∈G. Then σ⁢(𝔭i)∩ℚ⁢[x1,…,xn]⊃𝔭(=σ⁢(𝔭)) and σ-1⁢(σ⁢(𝔭i)∩ℚ⁢[x1,…,xn])⊂𝔭i∩ℚ⁢[x1,…,xn]=𝔭, hence G acts on S.

Second, G acts transitively on S. Let 𝔭1,𝔭2∈S and x∈𝔭1. Then σ1⁢(x)⁢σ2⁢(x)⁢⋯⁢σf⁢(x)∈𝔭⊂𝔭2. Hence there exists some σ such that σ⁢(x)∈𝔭2, in other word, x∈σ-1⁢(𝔭2). So 𝔭1⊂⋃iσi⁢(𝔭2). By prime avoidance theorem, 𝔭1=σ⁢(𝔭2) for some σ∈G.

Now, come back to our problem:

Let I⊂ℚ⁢[x1,…,xn,y]:=ℚ⁢[X,Y] be an ideal such that ℚ⁢[X,Y]/I is of dimension zero. Suppose L is a field finite over ℚ such that the maximal ideals 𝔪i,i=1,2,…,l containing I are L-coefficients. Write 𝔪i=(X-Ai,Y-bi), and P⁢(Y)=(Y-b1)⁢(Y-b2)⁢⋯⁢(Y-bl). We ask if P⁢(Y)∈ℚ⁢[Y]?

The answer is Yes.

Let F be a finite Galois extension of ℚ, which contains L. Then it is clear to see that (X-Ai,Y-bi) are already maximal in F⁢[X,Y]. So all maximal ideals in F⁢[X,Y] containing I are exactly 𝔪i⁢F⁢[X,Y]. Thus we may assume L is a finite Galois extension of ℚ.

Let I=⋂𝔫i in ℚ⁢[X,Y]. Let Si be the set of maximal ideals which lying over 𝔫i in L⁢[X,Y]. Denoted S the set of the maximal ideals 𝔪i. Then S is a disjoint union of Si. And G may act on S having orbits Si, P⁢(Y)=∏iPi⁢(Y) each Pi⁢(Y)∈ℚ⁢[Y].

Example Let us consider a concrete example. Consider (x2-2,y) in ℚ⁢[x,y] is maximal, and (x-2,y),(x+2,y) in ℚ⁢[2]⁢[x,y]. Now our P⁢(y)=y2 which is not irreducible. However, this example suggests that P⁢(y) is a power of an irreducible polyonomial.

This is true!

lemma Let 𝔫 be a maximal ideal of ℚ⁢[X,y] and L a finite Galois extension of ℚ such that the fiber of 𝔫 in L⁢[X,y] are all L-points. We construct P⁢(Y) as in the way above. Let S be the set of maximal ideals 𝔪i=(X-ai,Y-bi) in L⁢[X,Y] which lying over 𝔫. Let 𝔫∩ℚ⁢[y]=(f⁢(y)) where f⁢(y) is a monic polynomial thus a monic irreducible polynomial. Then P⁢(Y) is a power of f⁢(y).

Proof Observing that G acts transitively on S, thus the group Hi={σ∣σ⁢(bi)=bi} are all conjugate. It follows that P⁢(y)=f⁢(y)|H1|. In particular, if all bi are different, then P⁢(y)=f⁢(y).

Practice In practice, let α1,…,αs be algebraic numbers and β=g⁢(α1,…,αs) for g∈ℚ⁢[x1,…,xs]. We let I=(f1⁢(x1),…,fs⁢(xs),y-g⁢(x1,…,xs)) where fi are minimal polynomials of αi over ℚ. If certain good conditions holds, for example, the extension ℚ⁢[α1,…,αi]/ℚ⁢[α1,…,αi-1] has degree d⁢e⁢g⁢(αi) for i=1,…,s, then our I is maximal. More, if we require g⁢(x1,…,xs) are all distinct for substituting the roots of fi. Then our P⁢(y) is irreducible polynomial of β over ℚ.

No Galois theory, no Nullstellensatz, but use ``The fundamental theorem of symmetric polynomials''.

Let α1,α2,…,αs be algebraic numbers. Let β=g⁢(α1,…,αs) for some g∈ℚ⁢[x]. Let fi be the minimal polynomial of αi. Let P⁢(y)=∏(y-g⁢(θ1,…,θs)) where θi runs through the roots of fi. Then P⁢(y) is a polynomial of ℚ⁢[y].

Proof Let γi be the roots of f1. Then ∏i(y-g⁢(γi,x2,…,xs)) is a symmetric polynoimal of γi in ℚ⁢[x2,…,xs,y]⁢[γ1,…,γt] (View γi being indeterminate). Since the element symmetric polynomials of γ1,…,γt have vaule in ℚ. Hence ∏i(y-g⁢(γi,x2,…,xs)) is a polyonomial in ℚ⁢[x2,…,xs,y]. Finally P⁢(y)∈ℚ⁢[y].
