Snags when discovering the asymptotic behavior of an integral

I have trouble in discovering the asymptotic behavior (i.e, the asymptotic expansion) of the following integral:

∫0π/2d⁢x1+(n⁢π+x)⁢sin⁡x

The motivation is about the convergence of such improper integral:

I=∫0∞xβ⁢d⁢x1+xα⁢\abs⁢sin⁡x  α,β>0

The preceding integral is due to G.Hardy, which could be solved in such a way:

In=∫n⁢π(n+1)⁢πxβ⁢d⁢x1+xα⁢\abs⁢sin⁡x=∫0π(n⁢π+x)β⁢d⁢x1+(n⁢π+x)α⁢sin⁡x

Therefore I=∑nIn and we only need to estimate the asymptotic behavior of In.

In fact, it could be estimate roughly, which is sufficient to determine the convergence of I:

(n⁢π)β1+((n+1)⁢π)α⁢sin⁡x≤(n⁢π+x)β1+(n⁢π+x)α⁢sin⁡x≤((n+1)⁢π)β1+(n⁢π)α⁢sin⁡x  0≤x≤π

Both the leftest side and the rightest side could be integrated over the closed interval [0,π] elementarily and they are asymptotically equivalent (approximately C⁢n-1⁢log⁡n where C is a constant).

I want to see closer to the preceding integral. Can we obtain the asymptotic expansion of In? Out of simplicity, take α=1 and β=0, and with a slight modification, we'll deal with the simpler version (1).

I have tried Laplace's method but failed after some efforts. It seems that Laplace method could estimate ∫0π/n well, but ∫π/nπ/2 is not a negligible tail.

asymptotics definite-integrals

We write the integrand as

11+n⁢π⁢sin⁡x+x⁢sin⁡xamp;=11+n⁢π⁢sin⁡x⋅11+x⁢sin⁡x1+n⁢π⁢sin⁡xamp;=11+n⁢π⁢sin⁡x[1+O(x⁢sin⁡x1+n⁢π⁢sin⁡x)],

where the O⁢(⋯) holds uniformly for Form⁢(x) and x∈[0,π/2], so that

a⁢m⁢p;∫0π/2d⁢x1+n⁢π⁢sin⁡x+x⁢sin⁡xamp;=∫0π/2d⁢x1+n⁢π⁢sin⁡x+O(∫0π/2x⁢sin⁡x(1+n⁢π⁢sin⁡x)2dx).

We can estimate the integral in the O⁢(⋯) by observing that

∫0π/2x⁢sin⁡x(1+n⁢π⁢sin⁡x)2⁢𝑑xamp;=1n2⁢π2∫0π/2x⁢sin⁡x(1n⁢π+sin⁡x)2dxamp;∼1n2⁢π2∫0π/2xsin⁡xdx

by the dominated convergence theorem.

In light of this, (1) becomes

∫0π/2d⁢x1+n⁢π⁢sin⁡x+x⁢sin⁡x=∫0π/2d⁢x1+n⁢π⁢sin⁡x+O⁢(1n2).

Now courtesy of Mathematica we have

∫0π/2d⁢x1+n⁢π⁢sin⁡x=log⁡(n⁢π+n2⁢π2-1)n2⁢π2-1,

which allows us to conclude from (2) that

∫0π/2d⁢x1+n⁢π⁢sin⁡x+x⁢sin⁡x=log⁡(n⁢π+n2⁢π2-1)n2⁢π2-1+O⁢(1n2).

Here's a plot showing the numerical value of the integral in blue and the asymptotic in purple for 1≤n≤10.

As discussed in the comments, we can estimate the error term precisely by noting that the integrand of the error is

11+n⁢π⁢sin⁡x+x⁢sin⁡x-11+n⁢π⁢sin⁡xamp;=1+n⁢π⁢sin⁡x-1-n⁢π⁢sin⁡x-x⁢sin⁡x(1+n⁢π⁢sin⁡x+x⁢sin⁡x)⁢(1+n⁢π⁢sin⁡x)amp;=1n2⁢π2⋅-x⁢sin⁡x(sin⁡x+1+x⁢sin⁡xn⁢π)⁢(sin⁡x+1n⁢π),

so that, by the dominated convergence theorem, the error is asymptotic to

-1n2⁢π2⁢∫0π/2xsin⁡x⁢𝑑x=-2⁢Gn2⁢π2,

where G is Catalan's constant. Thus

a⁢m⁢p;∫0π/2d⁢x1+n⁢π⁢sin⁡x+x⁢sin⁡xamp;=log⁡(n⁢π+n2⁢π2-1)n2⁢π2-1-2⁢Gn2⁢π2+o(1n2).
