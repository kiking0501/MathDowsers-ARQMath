The answers say it all now. A last thing to add is that all this relies on the distinction between two-sided and one-sided ideals in the noncommutative setting. A ring which has neither proper left- nor proper right-ideals would indeed be a division ring. In particular, "artinian" in Qiaochu Yuan's answer means "left-artinian", and it is fascinating to see that, e.g., matrix algebras over an infinite field have infinitely many distinct left ideals (although they fall into finitely many isoclasses), and e.g. the Weyl algebra even has an infinite descending chain of left ideals.

Matrix is a very important tool non-commutative ring. Following theorem can help to us for your question.
Theorem: Let R be a ring, Mn⁢(R) be the ring of all n x n matrices over R.
I⊴Mn⁢(R)⇔ I=Mn⁢(U) for a uniquely determined ideal U of R.
In particular, R is simple ⇔ Mn⁢(R) is simple.
Consequently, Mn⁢(D) is a simple for any division ring D.
Hence, you can find a lot of examples via above statements.
