X is normal if and only if A⊆U implies there exists V such that A⊆V⊆V¯⊆U

I want to prove this: X is normal if and only if for every closed A and open U such that A⊆U, there exists V such that A⊆V⊆V¯⊆U.

Suppose X is normal. Let A⊆U where A is closed and U is open. Then X∖U is closed and (X∖U)∩A=∅. But since X is normal, there exist open sets V and V′ such that A⊆V, X∖U⊆V′, and V∩V′=∅. Clearly V⊆V¯, and also V⊆U because V∩V′=∅.

But how does that guarantee the closure of V is also contained in U?

Conversely, suppose for each A⊆U with A closed and U open, there exists an open set V such that A⊆V⊆V¯⊆U. Let C1,C2 be closed and C1∩C2=∅. Then X∖C2 is open and C1⊆X∖C2. By assumption, there exists an open V such that C1⊆V⊆V¯⊆U, hence V∩C2=∅. Similarly there exists open V′ such that C2⊆V′⊆V′¯⊆U with V′∩C1=∅.

But how do we get that V∩V′=∅?

For the forward direction, we let A⊆U for some closed A and open U. Then, Uc is closed, and A∩Uc, as you pointed out. Therefore, we have some disjoint open V1 and V2 such that A⊆V1 and Uc⊆V2. As orangeskid points out, V1∩V2=∅ implies V1⊆V2c⊆U, and V2c is closed, so V1¯⊆V2c⊆U. Therefore, A⊆V1⊆V1¯⊆U.

You might also consider an application of Urysohn's lemma: let f:X→[0,1] be a continuous function such that f⁢(A)={1} and f⁢(Uc)={0}. Then, consider that S=f-1⁢((1/2,1]) (or S=f-1⁢((a,1]) for any 0<a<1) is open in X and satisfies A⊆S⊆U. The fact that S¯⊆U follows from f⁢(S¯)⊆[1/2,1] (which implies that S¯∩Uc=∅).

For the backward direction, we let C1 and C2 be two disjoint closed subsets of X as you did. Then, we consider some open V such that C1⊆V⊆V¯⊆C2c. As (V¯)c is open, is disjoint from V, and contains C2, we have disjoint open neighborhoods V⊇C1 and (V¯)c⊇C2, and therefore X is normal.
