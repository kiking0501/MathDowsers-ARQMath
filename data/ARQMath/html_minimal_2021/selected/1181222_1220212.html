Really late to the party, but here is a very short and sweet approach:

Claim: For each integer n≥8, let S⁢(n) be the statement that n is expressible as a sum of 3's and 5's.

Base step: Since 8=3+5,9=3+3+3,10=5+5,S⁢(8),S⁢(9), and S⁢(10) are true.

Inductive step (S⁢(k)→S⁢(k+3)): Let k≥8 and assume that S⁢(k) is true, where for some non-negative integers, a,b,k=a⋅3+b⋅5. Then k+3=(a+1)⋅3+b⋅5, a sum of 3's and 5's, so S⁢(k+3) is true.

By mathematical induction, three cases of induction actually, for all n≥8,S⁢(n) is true. \blacksquare
