arrow here just means f maps D, which is defined as a subset of the reals, to a real. Congrats on educating yourself and moving above religious whackery :)
The arrow means D which is a subset of ℝ is the domain and ℝ is the co-domain of f, and *f maps D into ℝ*.
https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Function_%28mathematics%29#Notation may help with the function arrow while https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_mathematical_symbols may help more generally
other synonyms for the symbol are: map, mapping, transformation, function, application, arrow (itself).

If we write y=f⁢(x), then f:A→B indicates that x∈A and y∈B. It is required that f⁢(x) be defined for every x in A and that every value of f⁢(x) should be in B. Some times the relationship between x and f⁢(x) is written as x↦y=f⁢(x), or more commonly, as x↦f⁢(x).

A is called the domain and B is called the codomain. {y∈B:∃x∈A,y=f⁢(x)}, or, less formally, {f⁢(x):x∈A} is called the image of A. It is OK for the image of A to be a proper subset of the codomain of f. That is, B needs to contain every possible value of f⁢(x), but it is allowed to be even bigger.

For example, the sine function can be thought of as a function sin:ℝ→ℝ or as a function sin:ℝ→[-1,1]. Both functions have the same image but they have different codomains; so, technically, they are different functions. When the difference is important, you must explicitely state which one you mean.

The notation f:D⊆ℝ→ℝ is a contraction of f:D→ℝ where D⊆ℝ.

I'd stay away from calling B the range for f:A→B; a much better term is "codomain" in my opinion. The codomain is what set you are mapping to whereas f⁢(A)=Rng⁡(f).
