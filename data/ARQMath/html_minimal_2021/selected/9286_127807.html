Another proof, from G.M. Fichtengoltz, Calculus Course, page 612.

K=∫0∞e-x2⁢𝑑x

It easy to see (and prove) that, max⁡{(1+t)⁢e-t}=1 at t=0, hence for all t∈ℝ:

(1+t)⁢e-t<1

Substitution of t=±x2, leads us to:

(1-x2)⁢ex2<1  and    (1+x2)⁢e-x2<1

So,

ℓi⁢\lt⁢ui

Now, at the left inequality we restrict our x to be in (0,1) (so that, 1-x2>0), and in the right inequality let x>0. Raising all the inequalities with natural number n, we get,

(1-x2)n<e-n⁢x2x∈(0,1)  and    e-n⁢x2<1(1+x2)nx>0

Integrating the first inequality from 0 to 1, and the second inequality from 0 to +∞ we'll get:

∫01(1-x2)n⁢𝑑x<∫01e-n⁢x2⁢𝑑x<∫0∞e-n⁢x2⁢𝑑x<∫0∞d⁢x(1+x2)n

But,

∫0∞e-n⁢x2dx=1nK   (substitution u=n⁢x),

∫01(1-x2)ndx=∫0π2sin2⁢n+1(v)dv=(2⁢n)!!(2⁢n+1)!!   (substitution x=cos(v))

and, finally,

∫0∞d⁢x(1+x2)n=∫0π2sin2⁢n-2(v)dv=(2⁢n-3)!!(2⁢n-2)!!π2   (substitution x=ctg(v))

Hence, our unknown, K is bound:

n⁢(2⁢n)!!(2⁢n+1)!!<K<n⁢(2⁢n-3)!!(2⁢n-2)!!⁢π2

or,

n2⁢n+1⁢((2⁢n)!!)2((2⁢n-1)!!)2⁢(2⁢n+1)<K2<n2⁢n-1⁢((2⁢n-3)!!)2⁢(2⁢n-1)((2⁢n-2)!!)2⁢(π2)2

Now, the final step - Wallis Formula :

limn→∞⁡((2⁢n)!!)2((2⁢n-1)!!)2⁢(2⁢n+1)=π2

Then, when n tends to ∞ in our last inequality, we get:

K2=π4

and,

K=π2    as⁢K>0
