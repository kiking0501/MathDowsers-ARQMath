Cool way of finding cos⁡(π5)

while trying to solve a problem, I stumbled upon a way of finding cos⁡(π5) using identities and the cubic formula. Is it possible to find other values of sine or cosine in a similar way ?

Consider cos⁡(π5)-cos⁡(2⁢π5). Using the difference of cosines identity, we have cos⁡(π5)-cos⁡(2⁢π5)=-2⁢sin⁡(3⁢π10)⁢sin⁡(-π10).

Now we change the RHS using the identity sin⁡(x)=cos⁡(π2-x) and the fact that sin⁡(x) is odd.

-2⁢sin⁡(3⁢π10)⁢sin⁡(-π10)=2⁢cos⁡(π5)⁢cos⁡(2⁢π5) So, cos⁡(π5)-cos⁡(2⁢π5)=2⁢cos⁡(π5)⁢cos⁡(2⁢π5)

Now we make use of the identity cos⁡(2⁢x)=2⁢cos2⁡(x)-1. cos⁡(π5)-2⁢cos2⁡(π5)+1=2⁢cos⁡(π5)⁢(2⁢cos2⁡(π5)-1) Let y=cos⁡(π5) and we have y-y2+1=2⁢y⁢(2⁢y2-1) 4⁢y3+2⁢y2-3⁢y-1=0 which has the correct solution y=5+14=cos⁡(π5) One of the roots is also sin⁡(π10) which I'm guessing is because you end up with the same cubic if you apply the above to sin too.

There is a slightly shorter way:

sin⁡π5=sin⁡4⁢π5=2⁢sin⁡2⁢π5⁢cos⁡2⁢π5=4⁢sin⁡π5⁢cos⁡π5⁢cos⁡2⁢π5

Hence

cos⁡π5⁢cos⁡2⁢π5=14

You can also write this

cos⁡π5⁢cos⁡3⁢π5=-14

Then, using the formula 2⁢cos⁡a⁢cos⁡b=cos⁡(a+b)+cos⁡(a-b), you have:

12=2⁢cos⁡π5⁢cos⁡2⁢π5=cos⁡π5+cos⁡3⁢π5

Hence cos⁡π5 and cos⁡3⁢π5 are the roots of t2-12⁢t-14. The rest is easy, and the positive root is cos⁡π5.

To answer your question: in general, it's not possible to find values of cos⁡πn only by radicals, even though they are algebraic numbers. Even in the case you obtain an irreducible cubic equation (which can be solved by radicals), it's the "trigonometric case" here, which has no expression with real radicals (and a complex cubic root needs the trigonometric functions anyway). For instance, cos⁡1∘ can't be computed with real radicals. But cos⁡3∘ can.

Even if in general it's not possible, there are a few tricks you can apply, for instance

cos⁡π12=cos⁡(π3-π4)

Also, if you can compute cos⁡x by radicals, then you can compute Q=[0a⁢m⁢p;1a⁢m⁢p;2a⁢m⁢p;1a⁢m⁢p;20a⁢m⁢p;1a⁢m⁢p;-1a⁢m⁢p;-1a⁢m⁢p;10a⁢m⁢p;2a⁢m⁢p;-5a⁢m⁢p;-4a⁢m⁢p;10a⁢m⁢p;4a⁢m⁢p;2a⁢m⁢p;0a⁢m⁢p;6] too.

So, which angles of the form πn leads to trigonometric functions computable by radicals? The answer is given by the Gauss-Wantzel theorem. For instance, one can compute cos⁡π17. However, the computation is not obvious, see http://mathworld.wolfram.com/TrigonometryAnglesPi17.html
