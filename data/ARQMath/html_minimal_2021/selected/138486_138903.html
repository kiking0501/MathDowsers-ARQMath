(If you've done things with π1⁢(S1) yet...) The map you have is the concatentation of a degree 2 map and a degree -1 map, and so has degree 1.
As z goes half-way around the circle, f⁢(z) goes around the whole circle twice. Then as z traverses the second half, f⁢(z) goes backwards around the circle once. Then z and f⁢(z) have each gone around once.

The idea was to consider f:S1→S1 as a path f:I→S1, and then to lift that path to ℝ via the covering map ℝ→S1, t↦e2⁢π⁢i⁢t. This lift, f~:I→ℝ is given by f~⁢(t)=4⁢t for 0≤t≤1/2, and f~⁢(t)=3-2⁢t for 1/2≤t≤1. We see that f~⁢(0)=0, and f~⁢(1)=1, so using covexity of ℝ we can construct a basepoint preserving homotopy between f~ and the usual inclusion I⊂R. Composing this homotopy with the projection ℝ→S1 gives H.

We can identify your map f as f⁢(e2⁢π⁢i⁢t)=e2⁢π⁢i⁢(4⁢t) for 0≤t≤1/2 and f⁢(e2⁢π⁢i⁢t)=e2⁢π⁢i⁢(3-2⁢t) for 1/2≤t≤1. Then, we can use a linear homotopy H:S1×I→S1 given by H⁢(e2⁢π⁢i⁢t,s)=e2⁢π⁢i⁢((4⁢t)⁢(1-s)+t⁢s) for 0≤t≤1/2 and H⁢(e2⁢π⁢i⁢t,s)=e2⁢π⁢i⁢((3-2⁢t)⁢(1-s)+t⁢s) for 1/2≤t≤1. It is easy to check that this map is well defined, and that it satisfies H⁢(1,s)=1. This provides a basepoint preserving homotopy f≃1, and thus the mapping cone of f is contractible.
