This is a very elementary doubt.

Is it true that, ϕ⁢(n) the smallest number for which aϕ⁢(n)≡1(modn), where gcd⁡(a,n)=1.

No; it's not the smallest for specific values of a; it's not even the smallest that works for all a.

For example, ϕ⁢(8)=4, but for every odd integer n, n2≡1(mod8).

The number you are looking for is called the "reduced totient function", "least universal exponent function", or Carmichael function λ⁢(n). This is the smallest positive integer such that for all a with gcd⁡(a,n)=1, we have aλ⁢(n)≡1(modn). It always divides ϕ⁢(n).

Maybe it's also worth mentioning that λ⁢(p)=ϕ⁢(p) for prime p..
