The polynomials 1,…,xn *are* vectors in K⁢[x]≤n. They are certainly linearly independent, as a0+a1⁢x+⋯⁢an⁢xn=0 implies that a0=⋯=an=0.
The monomials 1,x,x2,…,xn are linearly independent over K more or less by the definition of the polynomial ring K⁢[x].
Anyway, looks like you are confused about what polynomials are. They are finite sums of the form ∑i=0mci⁢xi, ci∈K,m∈ℕ. Here x is an unknown, and it is not to be viewed as an element of K. Also, two polynomials are (by definition!!) equal if and only if all their corresponding coefficients are equal. In particular the above polynomial is the zero element of K⁢[x] if and only if all the coefficients ci=0. But, this is exactly what is required for the monomials to be linearly independent over K.
Another possible source for confusion is the linearly independent **OVER** K -part. Up to this point you should have covered abstract vector spaces over an arbitrary field.
And, the relation x⋅x-1⋅x2=0 shows that x and x2 are linearly dependent OVER THE RING K⁢[x]. In this relation the coefficients, x and -1 are elements of the ring K⁢[x], but they are not both elements of the field of constants, so they are linearly independent over K.

This is wrong. The element xk of the polynomial ring K⁢[x] is most emphatically NOT a function from K to itself. If that were the case, then the polynomials x and x2 would be equal in the case when K is the field of two elements {0,1} (arithmetic modulo two). Elements of K⁢[x] are formal polynomials. Two such beasts are equal (by definition) if and only if the coefficients of all the terms are equal.
