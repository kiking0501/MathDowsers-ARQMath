Perhaps modifying your notation just a bit? T:V→W where d⁢i⁢m⁢(V)=n and d⁢i⁢m⁢(W)=m our goal is to prove that d⁢i⁢m⁢(V)=d⁢i⁢m⁢(N⁢u⁢l⁢l⁢(T))+d⁢i⁢m⁢(r⁢a⁢n⁢g⁢e⁢(T)) where d⁢i⁢m⁢(N⁢u⁢l⁢l⁢(T))=r and d⁢i⁢m⁢(r⁢a⁢n⁢g⁢e⁢(T))=r⁢a⁢n⁢k⁢(T)=s. To prove this dimension theorem we need to exhibit bases (yes, that's it) which serve to form minimal spanning sets for the null-space and range of T.

One approach, pick a basis for V, study the matrix for T and steal this theorem from the corresponding theorem for rank and nullity of a matrix. That theorem comes from the nuts and bolts of Gaussian elimination. I don't think that is what your professor intends, so back to the linear algebraic argument.

Note k⁢e⁢r⁢(T)≤V hence k⁢e⁢r⁢(T) is a vector space and as it is a subspace of a finite-dimensional vector space it has a finite dimension as well, let's say r. Moreover, following your notation, βo={x1,x2,…,xr}. I assume at this point you have already proved in your class that if a vector space has a basis with finitely many elements then any such basis has the same number of vectors. We call this number the dimension of the vector space(or subspace).

It is also a simple exercise to show T⁢(V)≤W hence there exists a basis βz={z1,z2,…⁢zs} for the range. To prove the theorem, we must show r+s=n.

If zj∈T⁢(V) then there exists yj∈V such that T⁢(yj)=zj. We show {y1,y2,…,ys} is linearly independent by supposing otherwise towards a contradiction: suppose c1⁢y1+c2⁢y2+⋯+cs⁢ys=0 with at least one cj≠0 then since T⁢(0)=0 and the image of a linear combination is the linear combination of the images: c1⁢T⁢(y1)+c2⁢T⁢(y2)+⋯+cs⁢T⁢(ys)=0 Hence c1⁢z1+c2⁢z2+⋯+cs⁢zs=0 with at least one cj≠0 hence βz is linearly dependent. This contradicts our assumption that βz serves as a basis for the image of T. Therefore, {y1,y2,…,ys}=T-1⁢(βz) is a linearly independent subset of V.

At this point, I would like to claim β=βo∪T-1⁢(βz)={x1,x2,…,xr}∪{y1,y2,…,ys} forms a LI subset of V. Notice βo∩T-1⁢(βz)=∅ (can you prove this?) Suppose c1⁢x1+⋯+cr⁢xr+b1⁢y1+⋯⁢bs⁢ys=0. We know β contains nonzero vectors hence any nontrivial solution must stem from at least two nonzero coefficients in the above sum. If those two or more coefficients appear in the cj-terms then that cannot happen by LI of βo. Likewise, within the bj-coefficients we cannot find a linear dependence by LI of T-1⁢(βz). The only remaining possibility is that both cj and bj combine to give linear dependence, however, this is impossible since it contradicts the fact that the bases βo and T-1⁢(βz) are disjoint.

Next, ignoring the fact you may have other theorems to use, we must show β spans V. Let v∈V and suppose T⁢(v)=w. There exist α1,α2,…,αs such that w=α1⁢z1+⋯⁢αs⁢zs since βz forms basis of T⁢(V). Furthermore, T⁢(v-α1⁢y1-⋯-αs⁢ys)=T⁢(v)-w=0 hence v-α1⁢y1-⋯-αs⁢ys∈k⁢e⁢r⁢(T). Thus, there exist β1,…,βr such that v-α1⁢y1-⋯-αs⁢ys=β1⁢x1+⋯+βr⁢xr. Consequently, v=α1⁢y1+⋯⁢αs⁢ys+β1⁢x1+⋯+βr⁢xr which shows v∈s⁢p⁢a⁢n⁢(β) and as v was arbitrary we find s⁢p⁢a⁢n⁢(β)=V.

Therefore, β forms a basis for V and so (by that other theorem I'm not proving here) it has n-elements. But, β also clearly has r+s elements by its construction. Hence r+s=n and the proof is complete.
