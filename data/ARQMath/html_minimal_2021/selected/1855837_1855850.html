Your proof seems to be OK, as every number that divides two numbers, also divides their greatest common divisor. And it's well known that c∣d⟹c≤d, when working in positive integers.

For your second requirement here's how one can generate the cases when the GCDs are not equal. By Euclid's Algorithm we have (a+b,a-b)=(a+b,2⁢b) Now obviously c∣(a+b,2⁢b) and let a=c⁢a′,b=c⁢b′ where (a′,b′)=1. Then we have that: (a+b),2b)=c⋅(a′+b′,2b′) If a factor divides b′ then it can't divide a′+b′, as (a′,b′)=1. So (a′+b′,2⁢b′)=1 or 2. Hence we have that d=c or 2⁢c. The second case is obtained when a=c⁢a′,b=c⁢b′, where a′,b′ are odd coprime integers. While in every other case d=c.

$\gcd(a,b,c)=\gcd(\gcd(a,b),c)\,$ [GCD Associative Law]
