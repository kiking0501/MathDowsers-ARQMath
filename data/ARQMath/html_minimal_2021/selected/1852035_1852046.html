Is this how eigenvalues of some matrix A are related to the inverse of A?

Let A be an invertible n×n matrix. If A⁢v=λ⁢v  (1) for some v and λ then λ is an eigenvalue of A and v a corresponding eigenvector. Equation (1) may be written as A⁢v=λ⁢v=λ⁢I⁢v ⇔ A⁢v-λ⁢I⁢v=0 ⇔ (A-λ⁢I)⁢v=0 where I is the identity matrix. The last equation can be used to find correct λ's via the fundamental theorem of algebra (first take determinant).

On the other hand, it is also possible to write A⁢v=λ⁢v ⇔ A-1⁢A⁢v=A-1⁢λ⁢v ⇔ I⁢v-A-1⁢λ⁢v=0 ⇔ (I-A-1⁢λ)⁢v=0 since it was assumed A is invertible.

Now, since (A-λ⁢I)⁢v=0 and (I-A-1⁢λ)⁢v=0 it must be that A-λ⁢I=I-A-1⁢λ which connects the matrix A to its inverse via the eigenvalues of A.

Your last step is wrong. If you have two matrices A,B and a non-zero vector for which A⁢v=B⁢v=0 it doesn't mean that A=B. What is true is that if v is an eigenvector of A associated to the eigenvalue λ and A is invertible then v is also an eigenvector of A associated to the eigenvalue λ-1 as follows from:

A⁢v=λ⁢v⟹v=A-1⁢A⁢v=λ⁢A-1⁢v⟹A-1⁢v=λ-1⁢v.
