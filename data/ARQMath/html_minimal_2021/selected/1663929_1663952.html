The latter is defined as the cross product Nσ⁢(u,v)=∂⁡σ∂⁡u×∂⁡σ∂⁡v However, this only holds for ℝ3, because the cross product is defined only there. What about higher dimensions? I'm asking because I also have a theorem involving the normal vector and the dimension isn't 3.

EDIT: Here is the theorem I'm talking about.

Def Let σ:K→ℝn e η:K′→ℝn be two regular surfaces. We say that σ and η differ by a change of variables if:

  1. there exist two open sets A,A′⊆ℝ2 such that K⊆A e K′⊆A′;
  2. there exist a change of variables α:A′→A of class C1 on A′ such that α⁢(K′)=K;
  3. η⁢(s,t)=σ⁢(α⁢(s,t)) for every (s,t)∈A′.

Theorem If two surfaces differ by a change of variables, then:

  1. σ⁢(K)=η⁢(K′);
  2. The normal vector of σ is Nσ⁢(α⁢(s,t))=Nη⁢(α⁢(s,t))⁢det⁡Jα⁢(s,t) The proof is left to the reader.

A k-dimensional, smooth hypersurface in ℝn, n>k is defined as a smooth map ϕ:G⊂ℝk→ℝn, G open in ℝk and rk⁢(D⁢ϕ)=k throughout G. If the surface is parametrized, the normal vector is defined to be the k-wedge, N⁢(v1,v2,⋯,vk)=∂⁡ϕ∂⁡v1∧∂⁡ϕ∂⁡v2∧⋯∧∂⁡ϕ∂⁡vk The above is simply the 'oriented volume' of the k - dimensional parallelopiped given by the vectors (∂⁡ϕ∂⁡vi)i=1k. The norm of this vector, ||N⁢(v1,v2,⋯,vk)|| is given by the determinant of the Grammian matrix of (∂⁡ϕ∂⁡vi)i=1k, and this is used in calculating the surface area. For the case k=2,n=3 you can check that this is indeed the usual cross product in ℝ3.
