What's "simplest" is highly context dependent - context which you have not supplied. However, one important aspect of simplicity is computational effectivity. In order to have any hope of effectively computing with such radicals it is essential to normalize them so that any algebraic relationships are made explicit. For example, suppose that you are computing with expressions involving  5 raised to the powers  1/6, 1/10, and  1/15. Then such expressions can all be expressed as polynomials in the single radical  51/30, e.g.  51/6=(51/30)5. Said in the language of field theory ℚ⁢(51/6, 51/10, 51/15)⊂ℚ⁢(51/30). Therefore arithmetic operations on such radical expressions reduce to simple efficient polynomial operations, viz. operations in the ring Q⁢[x]⁢(mod⁢x30-5).

Such normalization is even more crucial when working with multiple radicands. For example 6⁢10⁢15= 30, so this algebraic dependence needs to be eliminated by choosing two of the three radicands as a basis, say 6,10, then eliminating 15 using 15=6⁢10/2. Developing effective algorithms for computing with such radicands (e.g. denesting) is a highly nontrivial task, based on the Galois theory of radical extensions (Kummer theory). This is the theory at the basis of such effective algorithms implemented in computer algebra systems.

@Bill -- but please address "simplest form" in the context of rational exponents. OK, I accept the equivalent of radical "simplest" form as ~one~ important canonical form even for rational exponents. But seems to me there is another, more natural form that is in a palpable sense truly "simplest". And that's: factored into primes (including variables) with exponents, one such prime factor per prime, and exponents reduced. That is a true normal form which can facilitate comparison and other results. And, it's truly "simplest" in an elegant sense, a natural generalization of prime factorization.
@David: Yes, one could completely factor everything as you suggest. But this could lead to combinatorial explosion, turning what might be a polynomial time/space algorithm into an exponential one. In number theory and algebra often it suffices to work with gcd-free bases or similar partially factored objects. Sometimes one can design algorithms so that the factorizations are computed "lazily", only when need be, e.g. assume q is prime, but if something breaks down, e.g. a zero-divisor is discovered mod q, then split q and continue appropriately.
