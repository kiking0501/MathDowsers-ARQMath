Any induced norm satisfies ∥A∥≥ρ⁢(A), where ρ⁢(A) is the spectral radius of A, i.e. the largest absolute value of its eigenvalues. This fact is stated without proof in Wikipedia; here is a proof.

Any induced norm is of the form ∥A∥=maxx⁡∥A⁢x∥∥x∥. Let x* be an eigenvector corresponding to the eigenvalue λ* with the largest absolute value. Then ∥A∥≥∥A⁢x*∥∥x*∥=∥λ*⁢x*∥∥x*∥=|λ*|⁢∥x*∥∥x*∥=|λ*|.

Let A=[1000]. Then your norm is ∥A∥=12⁢∥A∥F=12 while ρ⁢(A)=1, contradicting the above inequality.

I didn't expect this answer to turn into "Please prove as many properties of induced norms as possible", but here you go: From the definition of induced norm, one can prove submultiplicativity as follows: ∥A⁢B∥=max⁡∥A⁢B⁢x∥∥x∥≤max⁡∥A∥⁢∥B⁢x∥∥x∥=∥A∥⁢max⁡∥B⁢x∥∥x∥=∥A∥⁢∥B∥. First equality is the definition of ∥A⁢B∥, inequality is because ∥A∥≥∥A⁢y∥∥y∥ for all y and so ∥A⁢y∥≤∥A∥⁢∥y∥, last equality follows from the definition of ∥B∥.
