If s=∑i=1mai⁢χAi=∑j=1nbj⁢χBj then ∑i=1mai⁢μ⁢(Ai)=∑j=1nbj⁢μ⁢(Bj)

Background

Let χA be the characteristic function of the set A. A simple function s is a function of the form s⁢(x)=∑i=1nai⁢χEi⁢(x), where ai∈ℝ and Ei are measurable sets. A simple function may have more than one representation (consider splitting Ej into two disjoint sets).

If (X,ℳ,μ) is a measure space and s is as above, then the Lebesgue integral of s is defined as ∫s⁢𝑑μ=∑i=1nai⁢μ⁢(Ei).

We are to show that the above definition of ∫s⁢𝑑μ is consistent in light of the fact that s may have multiple representations.

Attempt

Assume s=∑i=1mai⁢χAi=∑j=1nbj⁢χBj and further assume that Ai are disjoint and Bi are disjoint. Then \begin{align*} \sum_{i=1}^{m}a_i\chi_{A_i}&=\sum_{i=1}^{m}a_i\sum_{j=1}^n\chi_{A_i \cap B_j}\\ &=\sum_{j=1}^{n}b_j\sum_{i=1}^m\chi_{A_i \cap B_j}\\ &=\sum_{j=1}^{n}b_j\chi_{B_j}, \end{align*} since ai=bj on Ai⋂Bj (*).

Since Ai⁢⋂Bj form a partition of ⋃Ai=⋃Bi, we have \begin{align*} \sum_{i=1}^{m}a_i\mu(A_i)&=\sum_{i=1}^{m}a_i\mu\left(\sum_{j=1}^n A_i \cap B_j\right)\\ &=\sum_{j=1}^{n}b_j\mu\left(\sum_{i=1}^m A_i \cap B_j\right)\\ &=\sum_{j=1}^{n}b_j\mu(B_j), \end{align*}

Question

Is this right? I am not too sure about (*).

measure-theory

The (*) formula is not correct. Precisely, it should be described as: If for some i,j, Ai∩Bj≠∅, then a_i=b_j. The proof is as follows. Because ∑i=1mai⁢χAi=∑j=1nbj⁢χBj, if we take x∈Ai∩Bj for some i,j, then ai=bj.

Is it wrong to assign a value to Ai∩Bj if the intersection is empty?..other than that, is the rest of my proof all right?
Yes, it is wrong. Other than that, the definition of the Lebesgue is not proper. We usually define the Lebesgue integral as ∫s⁢𝑑μ=∑i=1nai⁢χAi with Ai are disjoint partition of X, that is, ∪iAi=X. You miss this condition.
