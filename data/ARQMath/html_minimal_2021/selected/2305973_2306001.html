When you're converting an optimisation problem from general form to standard form, this is how you deal with variables which have no sign constraints.
If x is a variable without a sign constraint, that means that x can take on both positive and negative values. So both x≥0 and x<0 are possible. But in standard form, we want all our variables to be non-negative. To deal with this problem, we introduce two variables in place of x, both of which are positive such that x could take the whole spectrum of positive and negative values.
If we write x=x+-x- where both x+,x-≥0 are non negative, then if x+≥x-,x≥0 and if x-<x+,x<0. This way we meet the criteria for converting our variable (variables) into standard form.

You're removing valid possibilities from your feasible set. When there is no sign constraint on x, there could be feasible points with x<0 and maybe one of them is optimal. In that case, if you try to solve the linear program with just x≥0 then you will not find the best solution.
