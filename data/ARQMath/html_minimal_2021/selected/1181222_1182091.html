This seems to me a good formulation, even though it is not the usual one. (Maybe it is better!) It requires only the proofs of the last three statements, for which you can get a good hint by looking at how you change m and n in the sum x=3⁢m+5⁢n when you transition from x=a1 to b1, from b1 to c1, and from c1 to a2.

You are almost done, actually you can prove it even without induction:

If ∀x≥8 and you must prove that x∈ℕ∧x=5⁢a+3⁢b

Let x1 be 8 and consider your base cases:

x1=8=3⁢⋅⁢1+5⁢⋅⁢1

x2=9=3⁢⋅⁢3+5⁢⋅⁢0

x3=10=3⁢⋅⁢0+5⁢⋅⁢2

This is true for the first 3 n (1, 2 and 3) of xn.

Now, consider the following:

x4=11=x1+3

x5=12=x2+3

x6=13=x3+3

x7=14=x4+3

x8=15=x5+3

…

xn=xn-3+3 ∀n⁢\gt⁢3

As you can see from the pattern, it's obvious that you are summing only multiples of 3 basing on your previous results (which were also sums of multiples of threes and multiples of fives).

And you may also note that as soon as you sum 5 threes (3+3+3+3+3=15=3⁢⋅⁢5) you can safely replace them with 3 fives (5+5+5=15=3⁢⋅⁢5)

The previous equation leads to a recurrence relation and you can say with certainty that your statement is true ∀x≥8
