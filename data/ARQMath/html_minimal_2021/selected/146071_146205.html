Another way of seeing this (yes, there are many ways!) is to notice that two isomorphic groups have the same quotients. That is, if G≅H and G⁢\twoheadrightarrow⁢K then H⁢\twoheadrightarrow⁢K.

Now, by this question (which was only asked the other day, which is why I am posting this answer!), we know that every proper quotient of ℚ is torsion (that is, every element has finite order). On the other hand, ℤ×ℤ has a torsion-free proper quotient, ℤ×ℤ⁢\twoheadrightarrow⁢ℤ. Thus, they cannot be isomorphic.

(Indeed, this actually proves that there cannot be a homomorphism from ℚ to ℤ×ℤ, as lhf has already shown - the result we use tells us that the map cannot have non-trivial kernel, while this proves that the kernel cannot be trivial either.)
