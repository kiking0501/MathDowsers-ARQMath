Algebraic proof of non-trivial solution to the Pell's equation

Let d be a square-free positive integer, and consider the pairs (x,y)∈ℤ2 that satisfy:

x2-d⁢y2=1

The existence of a non-trivial solution to this equation (i.e. distinct from (±1,0)) is equivalent to showing existence of a torsion-free unit in ℤ⁢[d]. All proofs I've seen of this fact uses the Dirichlet Approximation Theorem, and then follows by consecutive applications of the Pigeonhole Principle. But the group of units in ℤ⁢[d] is a purely algebraic construction, so I wonder if it's possible to prove this fact without resorting to the approximation theorem. Is there a known "algebraic" proof of this?

Assume that d=p≡1mod4 is a prime number. The field of p-th roots of unity contains the real quadratic number field k=ℚ⁢(p). The norm of the cyclotomic unit 1+ζ is a unit in k, and you will have an algebraic proof of the solvability of the Pell equation if you can show that this unit is not ±1. This must be east to prove using the sine function (this proof is due to Dirichlet).

If you want to avoid transcendental function, you will have to invoke congruences, but I haven't checked how far this will get you. Things will get more technical in general; for primes p≡3mod4 you already have to look at the field of 4⁢p-th roots of unity.
