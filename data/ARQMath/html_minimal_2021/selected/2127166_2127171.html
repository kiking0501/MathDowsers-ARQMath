Diophantine equation a⁢b=a+b+n, find (a,b) for particular n

It's easy to see that this equation always has a solution:

a=n+2,b=2

Because it can be rewritten:

a=b+nb-1

Moreover, for even n there are no more nontrivial solutions, obviously, because we would have a ratio of odd to even or even to odd. (Never mind, that's not true, of course, even numbers can divide odd. However, there are other reasons for trouble with even n.)

I need to find solutions with minimal a-b, but a≠b for odd values of n. Is there a general formula or at least an algorithm?

Since a and b are interchangeable, assume a>b.

elementary-number-theory diophantine-equations
The equation can be written as (a-1)⁢(b-1)=n+1.

We can rewrite this as a⁢b-a-b+1=n+1, or (a-1)⁢(b-1)=n+1. Hence the smallest a-b is achieved then a-1 is the smallest divisor of n+1 greater than n+1 (or equal if you were to allow a=b).

By the way, the ratio of even to odd can be integer - just look at 63. For example, for n=14, you can take a=6,b=4, so this is another nontrivial solution for an even n.
