From your question it is not clear what kind of proof you are looking for, so I will give a couple of different proofs.

First, a formal semantical one. That is, using the basic truth-functional definition of ∨:

p∨(q∨r) is false iff

both p and q∨r are false iff

p, q, and r are all false iff

both p∨q and q are false iff

(p∨q)∨r is false

This of course also means that p∨(q∨r) is true iff (p∨q)∨r is true. Hence, they are equivalent.

Finally, given your comment not to use DeMorgan, I suspect that you are looking for an algebraic proof. Now again, that means that you will have to tell us what equivalences we are allowed to use, but let's assume the following fairly standard equivalences:

Commutation

p∧q⇔q∧p

p∨q⇔q∨p

Double Negation

¬⁢¬⁢p⇔p

Implication

p→q⇔¬⁢p∨q

Exportation

p→(q→r)⇔(p∧q)→r

With these, you can do the following:

p∨(q∨r)⇔ (Commutation)

p∨(r∨q)⇔ (Double Negation x 2)

¬⁢¬⁢p∨(¬⁢¬⁢r∨q)⇔ (Implication x 2)

¬p→(¬r→q)⇔ (Exportation)

(¬p∧¬r)→q)⇔ (Commutation)
