You can hide the usage of the linear representation lemma, if you want. However, division with remainder is a must with this definition. Otherwise, you'll prove a statement that is too general to be true.

Now, let's prove that if (a,b)=1 and a∣b⁢c, then a∣c by induction in a.

a=1 is straightforward (1 divides everything). Assume that A≥2 and we know the result for a<A. Let (A,b)=1 and A∣b⁢c. Dividing with remainder, we can assume without loss of generality that b<A (this is nothing but one step in the Euclidean algorithm, so, as I said, we still have it in disguise). Also, since (A,b)=1 and A≥2, the case b=0 is impossible. Write A⁢k=b⁢c. Since (b,A)=1, b∣A⁢k, b<A, we have b∣k by the induction assumption. Hence k=m⁢b and A⁢m=c, i.e., A∣c.

This uses the absence of divisors of 0, division with remainder, and the induction axiom (which can be relaxed to the condition that no infinite sequence with strictly decreasing norms exists). Removing any of those makes the proof impossible because you can create a ring in which the statement is false then.
