The -π⁢i is not ∫γR+f⁢(z)⁢𝑑z, it is the limiting value of the integral along the small semicircular indent as the radius tends to 0. The integral over γR+ is indeed 0 in the limit as R→∞.

∫γR+ei⁢z-1z⁢𝑑z=i⁢∫0π(exp⁡(i⁢R⁢cos⁡t-R⁢sin⁡t)-1)⁢𝑑t=-π⁢i+∫0πe-R⁢sin⁡t⁢exp⁡(i⁢R⁢cos⁡t)⁢𝑑t This shows where the -π⁢i comes from. The remaining integral is bounded in absolute value by IR=∫0πe-R⁢sin⁡t⁢𝑑t. Now IR→0 as R→∞. This is immediate from the Dominated Convergence Theorem. There is a tradition in complex analysis textbooks to assume the reader is unfamiliar with the DCT and to use Jordan's inequality to get an explicit upper bound on IR instead. One can avoid both of these by using a rectangular contour instead of a semicircular contour.

The reason for using (ei⁢z-1)/z instead of ei⁢z/z is to avoid having a pole at the origin (actually on this given contour). Indeed most texts use this function but have to indent the contour at the origin to avoid the pole. In this approach, that is where the -π⁢i comes from.

@AnmolBhullar Your error is to assume that because the integrand "goes to 0" then so does the integral. You are neglecting the fact that the length of the path of integration is increasing. This is analogous to asserting that ∫0R(1/R)⁢𝑑t→0 as R→∞ as 1/R→0.
