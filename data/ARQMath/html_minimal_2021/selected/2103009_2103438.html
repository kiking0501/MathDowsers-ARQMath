OP already found an answer, but I will formalize the reasoning here.

Let f:ℚ→ℚ such that |f⁢(x)-f⁢(y)|≤12⁢|x-y|,∀x,y∈ℚ.

Since ℝ is metric space completion of ℚ and f is uniformly continuous, there is unique uniformly continuous function f¯:ℝ→ℝ such that f¯⁢(x)=f⁢(x),∀x∈ℚ. From continuity it directly follows that for any x∈ℝ and any sequence (xn) in ℚ converging to x in ℝ, we have f¯⁢(x)=limn⁡f⁢(xn) and thus the relation (1) is true for f¯ on whole ℝ as well. Hence, it is not loss of generality to assume that f:ℝ→ℝ in the first place, with f⁢(ℚ)⊆ℚ.

By Banach fixed point theorem, there exists unique fixed point a of f.

Let g⁢(x)=x-f⁢(x). Note that a is a fixed point of f if and only if g⁢(a)=0, and that f⁢(ℚ)⊆ℚ if and only if g⁢(ℚ)⊆ℚ. Since you are looking for f such that f⁢(x)≠x,∀x∈ℚ, the unique fixed point of f must be irrational, i.e., g has unique irrational zero.

We can now rewrite (1) as follows:

\begin{align} |f(x)-f(y)|\leq \frac 12|x-y| &\iff |g(x)-g(y)-(x-y)|\leq\frac 12|x-y|\\ &\iff \left|\frac{g(x)-g(y)}{x-y}-1\right|\leq\frac 12\\ &\iff \frac 12\leq \frac{g(x)-g(y)}{x-y}\leq \frac 32\tag{2} \end{align}

Relation (2) can be used to derive simple sufficient condition on g to get f with desired properties:

Let g:ℝ→ℝ be differentiable such that 12≤g′⁢(x)≤32,∀x∈ℝ, and let g have irrational zero. Then f⁢(x)=x-g⁢(x) (restricted on ℚ) satisfies condition (1) and has no rational fixed point. If g⁢(ℚ)⊆ℚ, then f⁢(ℚ)⊆ℚ as well.

Proof. From the above discussion, all that remains to be proven is that 12≤g′⁢(x)≤32 implies (2). Assume that there exist some x,y∈ℝ such that g⁢(x)-g⁢(y)x-y∉[12,32]. By the mean value theorem, there exists some c∈ℝ such that g′⁢(c)∉[12,32]. Contradiction. \tag * \square}
