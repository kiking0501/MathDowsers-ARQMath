I've got an idea how to solve this problem. The proof is not complete, but I am rather confident that one can complete it.

Let's show more generally that k! divides (pn-1)⋅…⋅(pn-pk-1). I believe that this is true for every integer p. Recall that the multiplicity of a prime number q in k! equals vq⁢(k!)=∑ℓ≥1⌊kqℓ⌋. Thus, it suffices to prove that the multiplicity of q in the product P=(pn-1)⋅…⋅(pn-pk-1) is at least that large. Now the idea is to decompose P in partial products Pℓ such that the multiplicity of q in Pℓ is at least ⌊kqℓ⌋, so that in the end the multiplicity of P is at least ∑ℓ⌊kqℓ⌋.

I can show at least that the multiplicity is at least ⌊kq⌋: Consider Pi=(pn-pi⁢q)⁢…⁢(pn-pi⁢q+q-1) for i≤⌊kq⌋-1, so that ∏iPi divides P. Then Pi≡0modq, since each power of p is one the powers of 1,…,pq-1 modulo q. Hence, q⌊kq⌋ divides P.
