For your first question, one nice property of the quotient topology: if f is a continuous map from X into Y and respects the equivalence ∼ on X (i.e. x∼y⇒f⁢(x)=f⁢(y)), then f induces a continous map from X/∼ into Y. This comes quite easily from the definition. In addition, you may want to start with a continuous map from Sn into ℝn∖0. Since Sn is compact, Sn/∼ is compact as well, so you only need to show that your map is continuous and bijective to be sure it is an homeomorphism.

These spaces are not homeomorphic: One is compact, the other is not. The correct claim is that Sn is homeomorphic to the quotient of Rn+1∖0 by the equivalence relation x∼t⁢x,t>0.

Edit: Now I see that you are considering a different equivalence relation x∼t⁢x,x∈Rn+1∖0,t∈R×=R∖0. Then the quotient is the real-projective space R⁢Pn, it is not homeomorphic to Sn unless n=1. Take a look at, say, Hatcher's "Algebraic Topology" to see why these spaces are not homeomorphic (R⁢Pn is not simply-connected while Sn is, for n≥2).

Edit 2. Now that the question is about two descriptions of R⁢Pn, I will give an answer as a sequence of steps you can do in order to solve this problem yourself:

  1. Show that X=Rn+1∖0 is homeomorphic to Sn×R+. Hint: the first component of the map from X to the product is f⁢(x)=x/||x||. Wha should be the second component of this map?

  2. Suppose that X is a topological space homeomorphic to the product Y×Z. Consider the projection p of X to the first factor. Show that the quotient topology induced on Y via the map p is the same as the original topology of Y. Now apply this to the case when X is as in Part 1 and Y=Sn.

  3. Suppose that X is a topological space and you have subjective maps p:X→Y,q:Y→V,r=q∘p. Consider the quotient topology on Y induced by p And then the quotient topology t induced on V by q. Consider also the quotient topology t′ on V induced by the map r. Show that the topologies t,t′ are the same.

  4. Now, apply step 3 to the maps p:X=Rn+1∖0→Y=Sn,q:Y→V=Sn/∼, where x∼y if and only if y=±x and r:X→V which is the quotient map for the equivalence relation x∼t⁢x,t∈R×, the group of nonzero real numbers.
