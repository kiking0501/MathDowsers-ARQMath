Sard's Lemma in one dimmension

Sard's lemma in one dimmension: Let f:ℝ→ℝ be of class C1. Let C={x∈ℝ:f′⁢(x)=0} be the set of critical points of f. Then f⁢(C) has measure 0.

I believe I have found a proof but I'm not sure if its correct. I would appreciate if someone could point out any flaws.

The basic idea is this:

If a is a critical point of f, then if x is close to a, f′⁢(x) is close to f′⁢(a)=0, so f⁢(x) varies by a small amount if x stays close to a. Our goal is to cover ℝ by countably many intervals Ik and make the Ik small enough so that if Ik contains a critical point a, then f⁢(Ik) will be an interval whose size is small. Then f⁢(C) will be contained in the union of countably many small intervals, which is a rough way of saying f⁢(C) has measure zero.

Now more rigorously:

Step I:

Since a ℝ is a countable union of intervals of the type [n,n+1] for n∈ℤ, and a countable union of measure-zero sets is measure-zero, it suffices to show that f⁢(C)∩[n,n+1] is measure-zero for all n∈ℤ. It suffices to take the case n=0.

Step II:

Let S={x∈[0,1]:f′⁢(x)=0}. Let a∈S. Let's fix δ>0. Since f′ is continuous the closed, bounded interval [0,1] it is uniformly continuous on [0,1] by Heine-Cantor, and thus there exists ϵ0>0 such that ∀x,y∈[0,1], |x-y|<ϵ0⟹|f′⁢(x)-f′⁢(y)|<δ.

Hence ∀x∈[0,1], |x-a|<ϵ0⟹|f′⁢(x)|<δ

By Cauchy's mean value theorem, if ϵ>0 then for all x≠a such that |x-a|<ϵ, there exists c such that |c-a|<ϵ and

[f⁢(x)-f⁢(a)]/[x-a]=f′⁢(c).

Choosing ϵ<ϵ0, we have |f′⁢(c)|<δ and thus |f⁢(x)-f⁢(a)|<δ⁢|x-a|<δ⁢ϵ.

In summary, for all x∈[0,1], for all ϵ<ϵ0, we have: |x-a|<ϵ⟹|f⁢(x)-f⁢(a)|<δ⁢ϵ. (***)

Step III:

Now, lets choose N∈ℕ such that 1/N<ϵ0. Then since [0,1]=⋃0≤k<N[kN,k+1N],

f⁢(S)=⋃0≤k<Nf⁢(S∩[kN,k+1N])

If S∩[kN,k+1N]=∅, then its image is empty. Otherwise [kN,k+1N] contains a critical point a and since ∀x∈[kN,k+1N], |x-a|≤1/N, applying (***) we get ∀x∈[kN,k+1N], |f⁢(x)-f⁢(a)|<δ/N. Thus f⁢([kN,k+1N]) is a closed interval of length at most 2⁢δ/N. Finally, since

f⁢(S∩[kN,k+1N])⊂f⁢([kN,k+1N])

we have f⁢(S) is contained in the union of at most N closed intervals each of whose length is at most 2⁢δ/N.Thus the sum of their lengths is at most 2⁢δ.

In conclusion, for any δ>0, we can cover f⁢(S) by finitely many closed intervals whose total length is at most 2⁢δ. Thus f⁢(S) has measure zero, and we're done.
