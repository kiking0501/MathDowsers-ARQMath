The power law applies when the limit is being taken with respect to a variable which is distinct from the variable in the exponent. That is, if it were limx→a⁡(1+1/x)n then it would work.
On the other hand, doing:\begin{align}\lim_{n\to\infty}\left(1+\frac1n\right)^n&=\left(\lim_{n\to\infty}\left(1+\frac1n\right)\right)^{\lim_{n\to\infty}n}\\&=1^\infty\end{align}doesn't help because 1∞ is indeterminate.

The first step is meaningless. What does the n outside the limit mean?

limn→∞⁡(1+1n)n=(limn→∞⁡(1+1n))n

This is wrong. The quantity on the left is independent of n while the one on the right is not.

What the power law tells you is for some positive integer m, limn→∞⁡anm=(limn→∞⁡an)m if an>0 for all n and limn→∞⁡an exists.

[Added later:] Despite the incorrect reasoning mentioned above, it is worth to note that the limit limn→∞⁡(1+1/n)n is sometimes used as a definition of e. Therefore, unless you know why the limit is not 1, it would be "unfair" to say that "e=1" is "obviously not true".

Also, you originally put the Euler-constant tag to your question, which is also incorrect. Since the constant e, sometimes called the "Euler number" is not the same as the Euler's constant.
