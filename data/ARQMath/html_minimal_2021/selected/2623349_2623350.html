Is the inverse of bijective continuous functions always continuous?

Let X,Y be two metric spaces, E⊆X, f:E→Y. I have a question that is lingering in my mind long. If f is continuous and one-to-one, then does f-1:f⁢(E)→E continuous? I think the answer is true, is it? Is there counterexample? However, I read several books, some of them just prove that if E is compact, then it is so. Why don't they more generally discuss it?

PS: The definition of continuity is: f:E→Y is called continuous iff the preimage f-1⁢(U) is open in E for all open set U in Y.

It is not true in general. Just take the identity from ℝ endowed with the discrete metric into ℝ endowed with the usual metric. It is continuous, but its inverse isn't.
