ℤ/n⁢ℤ is always a principal ideal ring, because it is a homomorphic image of the principal ideal ring ℤ.

It is never a principal ideal domain when n is composite, however, because a nontrivial factorization of n leads directly to zero divisors in the ring.

@danr.greenfield: Even a field has ideals, namely {0} and the entire field. Since these are principal ideals -- namely ⟨0⟩ and ⟨1⟩ -- every field is a principal ideal ring. (And so a PID because a field is also a domain).
