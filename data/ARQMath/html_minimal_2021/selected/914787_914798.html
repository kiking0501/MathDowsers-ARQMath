Exotic proofs of ∑j=0n-1(p+jp)=(p+np+1)

Let p,n be positive integers.

The following identity ∑j=0n-1(p+jp)=(p+np+1) may be proved by induction or by successive uses of Pascal's rule (both techniques are demonstrated here).

Question

Is there a combinatorial proof for this identity ? Do you know other proofs ?

combinatorics summation

We want to show a bijection from p+1-element subsets of {0,…,p+n-1} to pairs (j,A), where 0≤j≤n-1 and A is a p-element subset of {0,1,…,p+j-1}. Note that there are exactly ∑j=0n-1(p+jp) such pairs and (p+np+1) such subsets, so if we prove this bijection, we will also prove the identity.

Take any p+1-element subset B of {0,…,p+n-1}. Let m be its biggest element. Then note that it uniquely defines a pair (m,B∖{m}). On the other side, if we have a pair (j,A), then it uniquely defines A∪{j}, which is a p+1-element subset of {0,1,…,p+n-1}. This proves the identity.
