Is my proof that the union of countably many countable sets is countable correct?

In an effort to find a proof that builds intuition for students in my proof writing course, I devised the following. Its seems too easy to me, so I am worried something is wrong. The class is very Naive Naive Set Theory so I realize some axioms are needed but that is way too hard for my students. I guess I would just like to have this result and I used to just state it, but then I thought about this proof.

We assume that ℕ×ℕ is countable.

Suppose A1, A2, A3,... is a sequence of countably infinite pairwise disjoint sets.

Write A1={a11,a12,a13,…}, A2={a21,a22,a23,…},...,An={an⁢1,an⁢2,an⁢3,…}.

Let A=⋃i∈ℕAi. Define f:A→ℕ×ℕ by the rule f⁢(ai⁢j)=(i,j).

Since the Ai's are disjoint f is a function. 1-1 and onto are easily proved. Thus, A is countable.

If we take off the pairwise dijoint condition then for every Ai and Aj with Ai⁢⋂Aj≠∅, replace Aj with Aj-(Ai⁢⋂Aj). I guess this is where I think I may be a little to hand wavey.

elementary-set-theory
Take the elements from the Ai in a round-robin fashion, taking one more set at every round.
Yeah, students never seem to believe the round robin proof so that is why I thought of this.

A proof independent on the fact that ℕ×ℕ is countable can be crafted using diagonal counting: Write the Aj in rows to get an infinite table, now label the finite diagonals consisting of ai⁢j,i+j=m+1 as Dm.
Dm is a finite set of cardinality at most (duplicates are to be ignored) m. Now start the enumeration with a11, the only element of D1, append the elements of D2 wich are not yet enumerated and continue...
If all elements are unique (i.e. the Aj are disjoint), the enumeration will be defined by the sequence a11⁢a12⁢a21⁢a13⁢a22⁢a31⁢a14⁢a23⁢…

This is the round-robin stuff.
