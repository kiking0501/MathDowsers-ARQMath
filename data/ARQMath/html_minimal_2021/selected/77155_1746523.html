Irreducible polynomial which is reducible modulo every prime

How to show that x4+1 is irreducible in ℤ⁢[x] but it is reducible modulo every prime p?

For example I know that x4+1=(x+1)4mod2. Also mod3 we have that 0,1,2 are not solutions of x4+1=0 then if it is reducible the factors are of degree 2. This gives that x4+1=(x2+a⁢x+b)⁢(x2+c⁢x+d) and solving this system of equations mod3 gives that x4+1=(x2+x+2)⁢(x2+2⁢x+2)(mod3). But is there a simpler method to factor x4+1 modulo a prime p?

The answers show how to prove that f=x4+1 is reducible modulo every prime p. If you want to show that f is irreducible in ℤ⁢[x], one way is to apply Eisenstein's criterion with p=2 to f⁢(x+1)=(x+1)4+1=x4+4⁢x3+6⁢x2+4⁢x+2.
What happens is that the splitting field of x4+1 is ℚ⁢(ζ8)/ℚ, which is not a cyclic extension, so there are no inert primes in that extension.

If -1 is a square in 𝔽p (which includes the case p=2), say a2=-1, then we have X4+1=X4-a2=(X2+a)⁢(X2-a). If p is odd and 2 is a square in 𝔽p, say 2=b2, then we have X4+1=(X2+1)2-(b⁢X)2=(X2+b⁢X+1)⁢(X2-b⁢X+1) If p is odd and neither -1 nor 2 is a square, then their product -2 is a square, say -2=c2. (Without using anything even remotely as deep as quadratic reciprocity, this follows immediately from the fact that 𝔽p× is a cyclic group of even order). Then we have X4+1=(X2-1)2-(c⁢X)2=(X2-c⁢X-1)⁢(X2+c⁢X-1)
