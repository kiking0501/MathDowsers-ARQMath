Ross' handwaving can be justified.

We use Weyl's theorem on the equidistribution of polynomials (mod 1) with at least one irrational coefficient (see here: How do you prove that p⁢(n⁢ξ) for ξ irrational and p a polynomial is uniformly distributed modulo 1?)

Choose p⁢(x)=2⁢π⁢x+π.

Let k∈(0,2). Let δ=k if k∈(0,1) and δ=k-1 otherwise.

By Weyl's theorem, density of n such that the fractional part of p⁢(n)<1-δ is non zero.

Thus the density (among the set of natural numbers) of the odd numbers 2⁢n+1 for which the fractional part of (2⁢n+1)⁢π<1-δ is non-zero. Thus the density of [(2⁢n+1)⁢π] is non-zero too.

For such an n,

Let N=[(2⁢n+1)⁢π].

We have that (2⁢n+1)⁢π-N<1-δ and so cos⁡(N)-cos⁡((2⁢n+1)⁢π)<1-δ

And thus cos⁡(N)<-δ and so cos⁡(N)+k<k-δ≤1

Thus for we have a positive density subset S of ℕ for which cos⁡(N)+k<1.

We have that

∑N∈S1Nk+cos⁡(N)>∑N∈S1N

Since S is of positive density, the sum of reciprocals is divergent, for a proof see here: Theorem on natural density
