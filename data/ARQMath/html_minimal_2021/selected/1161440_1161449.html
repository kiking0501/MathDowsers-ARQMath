Since your actual question concerns the legality of a substitution, my answer is a simple no. The reason is because this is a good example of a backwards proof. You are actually assuming what you are trying to prove. You want to show a statement of the form p→q, but what you are doing is trying to obtain something of the form (q∧p)→r, where r is some true equality. This does not really constitute a valid proof. You need to work toward the statement you are trying to prove true, not from it.
