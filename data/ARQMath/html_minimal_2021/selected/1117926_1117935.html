Have you tried doing the same with the ∑n=1∞1n2, like what you did with ∑n=1∞1n? It's the [Cauchy condensation test](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cauchy_condensation_test), you can show ∑n=1∞1n2 converges.

Think about Zeno's paradox in reverse.

12+122+123+…

You want to walk a mile.

First you walk half of it. Then take a bit of rest. Then walk half of the remaining, then take a bit of rest. And so on.

Will you ever go beyond the mile?

You have an infinite number of numbers (of the form 12n), but you can never go beyond the mile. Basically, the numbers are decreasing too fast, that no matter how many you take, you still are bounded.

In this case, you can prove that (using 1n2⁢\lt⁢1(n-1)⁢(n+1))

1+122+132+⋯+1n2⁢\lt⁢2-1n

So it is in fact bounded, and you cannot go beyond 2, no matter how many terms you take.

(And any bounded monotonic sequence converges).
