Series converges implies lim⁡n⁢an=0

I'm studying for qualifying exams and ran into this problem.

Show that if {an} is a nonincreasing sequence of positive real numbers such that ∑nan converges, then limn→∞⁡n⁢an=0.

Using the definition of the limit, this is equivalent to showing

\begin{equation} \forall \varepsilon > 0 \; \exists n_0 \text{ such that } |n a_n| < \varepsilon \; \forall n > n_0 \end{equation}

or

\begin{equation} \forall \varepsilon > 0 \; \exists n_0 \text{ such that } a_n < \frac{\varepsilon}{n} \; \forall n > n_0 \end{equation}

Basically, the terms must be bounded by the harmonic series. Thanks, I'm really stuck on this seemingly simple problem!

Because the series converges the sequence of partial sums forms a Cauchy sequence so we have for m,n>N

∥∑mn(ai)∥<ϵ

And by a similar argument to those above: ∥(n-m)⁢an∥≤∥∑mn(ai)∥<ϵ Distributing out the left hand side and spliting the inequality gives us the chain:

∥n⁢an∥-∥m⁢an∥≤∥(n-m)⁢an∥≤∥∑mn(ai)∥<ϵ

Then in the limit as n goes to infinity and fixing m we have the desired result since m⁢an will go to zero.
