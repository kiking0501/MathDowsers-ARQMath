Eratosthenes could have used his friend Archimedes's work on the quadrature of parabolic segments to obtain upper and lower bounds on the value of α-π2, or indeed any acute angle η, given its sine:

8⁢sin⁡12⁢η-sin⁡η3<η<4⁢tan⁡12⁢η+sin⁡η3 (0<η⁢\leqslant⁢π2).

A modern proof is short, so we get it out of the way first. The result has the form f⁢(η)<3⁢η<g⁢(η), where f⁢(0)=g⁢(0)=0, so it is enough to prove f′⁢(η)<3<g′⁢(η), for all η such that 0<η<π2. Taking the upper bound first, and writing cos⁡η=y, we have a2+b2+2⁢t2-2⁢a2+t2⁢b2+t2⁢cos⁡f=c2, and: g′⁢(η)-3=cos⁡η+2⁢sec2⁡12⁢η-3=y+41+y-3=1-2⁢y+y21+y=(1-y)21+y>0. As for the lower bound: 3-f′⁢(η)=3-4⁢cos⁡12⁢η+cos⁡η=3-4⁢cos⁡12⁢η+2⁢cos2⁡12⁢η-1=2⁢(1-cos⁡12⁢η)2>0, and as this is true for all η≠4⁢n⁢π, we even have f⁢(η)<3⁢η for all η>0, needing no upper bound. □

Recalling the trigonometric identities \begin{align*} \tan\left(\tfrac{1}{2}\sin^{-1}x\right) & = \left(1 - \sqrt{1 - x^2}\right)/x && (|x| \leqslant 1, \ x \ne 0), \\ \sin\left(\tfrac{1}{2}\sin^{-1}x\right) & = \tfrac{1}{2}\left(\sqrt{1 + x} - \sqrt{1 - x}\right) && (|x| \leqslant 1), \end{align*} we can rewrite the theorem just proved as:

4⁢(1+x-1-x)-x3<sin-1⁡x<4⁢(1-1-x2)+x23⁢x (0<x⁢\leqslant⁢1).

In the case of present interest:

8⁢5-8⁢3-112<sin-1⁡(14)<64-16⁢15+112.

In radians, this is 0.2526781133<η<0.2526888717, or η=0.2526835±.0000054.

Converting to degrees, and adding 90∘, we get 104.477383947∘<α<104.47800588∘, or:

The conversion factor 180/π needed here is obtained by long division by an estimate of π, which can be obtained by exactly the same method. Rather than perform the laborious computation needed to obtain once and for all a sufficiently accurate value of π, we shall just do enough to give a general idea.

Even the crudest estimate using the theorem, given by substitution in the equation π=2⁢sin-1⁡1, isn't bad: (8⁢2-2)/3<π<10/3, or π=3.22±0.11, but we do significantly better than this by substituting in π=6⁢sin-1⁡12, using the half-angle identities recalled above, as well as this one: \begin{gather*} \cos\left(\tfrac{1}{2}\sin^{-1}x\right) = \tfrac{1}{2}\left(\sqrt{1 + x} + \sqrt{1 - x}\right) \quad (|x| \leqslant 1). \\ \tan\frac{\pi}{12} = 2 - \sqrt{3}, \quad \sin\frac{\pi}{12} = \frac{\sqrt{6} - \sqrt{2}}{4}, \quad \cos\frac{\pi}{12} = \frac{\sqrt{6} + \sqrt{2}}{4}, \\ 4\sqrt{6} - 4\sqrt{2} - 1 < \pi < 17 - 8\sqrt{3}, \\ \text{i.e. } 3.141104722 < \pi < 3.143593539, \\ \pi = 3.1423 \pm 0.0012. \end{gather*} Explicit algebraic Euclidean-constructible bounds remain tractable for hand computation even when we apply the half-angle formulae again, substituting in π=12⁢sin-1⁡6-24: \begin{gather*} \tan\frac{\pi}{24} = \frac{1 - \cos\frac{\pi}{12}}{\sin\frac{\pi}{12}} = \frac{4\cos\frac{\pi}{12} - 2\cos\frac{\pi}{6} - 2} {2\sin\frac{\pi}{6}} = \sqrt{6} + \sqrt{2} - \sqrt{3} - 2, \\ \sin\frac{\pi}{24} = \frac{\tan\frac{\pi}{24}}{\sqrt{1 + \tan^2\frac{\pi}{24}}}, \\ \frac{\sqrt{6} + \sqrt{2} - \sqrt{3} - 2} {\sqrt{\sqrt{16} + 8\sqrt{3} - 10\sqrt{2} - 6\sqrt{6}}} - \sqrt{6} + \sqrt{4} < \pi < 17\sqrt{6} + 15\sqrt{2} - 16\sqrt{3} - 32, \\ \text{i.e. } 3.141561971 < \pi < 3.141716142, \\ \pi = 3.141639 \pm 0.000077. \end{gather*} This is the estimate of π obtained by inscribing a regular icositetragon in a circle, and erecting major and minor parabolic segments on each of its 24 sides. It considerably improves the estimate made by Archimedes using inscribed and circumscribed regular enneacontahexagons (96-sided polygons), while involving less computation; but it is still not as good as the approximation π⁢\bumpeq⁢355113.

In this accelerated variant of Archimedes's construction (and also of Viète's formula, except that if we follow Archimedes we start with a hexagon instead of a square - although a square is also acceptable), we start with a regular m-gon inscribed in the unit circle in ℂ, with one vertex being 1, and the next in anticlockwise order being z0=x0+i⁢y0, where the value of x0=cos⁡(2⁢π/m) is supposed to be already known. We end by giving a proof that Archimedes or Eratosthenes could have given (see Addendum 2):

We wish to obtain upper and lower bounds for the area θ of the sector O⁢P⁢R⁢Q of a circle, centre O, whose radius is taken as the unit of length. The angle at the vertex of the sector is η=2⁢θ, and we are given the length of the straight line segment Q⁢V=sin⁡η.

It is shown below (simply, using modern methods) that: (i) there exists a unique parabola passing through P and Q and touching the circle at the midpoint R of the circular arc P⁢R⁢Q, and the parabolic arc P⁢R⁢Q lies inside the circular segment P⁢R⁢Q except for the three points mentioned; and (ii) there exists a unique parabola touching the circle at P and Q, and it lies outside the circle except at these two points. It is a well-known theorem (but it is also proved below) that if the tangents meet the axis O⁢R at the point Z, then the vertex of the parabola is the midpoint of S⁢Z.

By Archimedes's famous quadrature result, the area of the minor parabolic segment (as I shall call it, while I call the other the "major" parabolic segment) is 43 the area of the triangle △⁢Q⁢P⁢R, which is also 43 the area of the rectangle Q⁢S⁢R⁢X (or equivalently, R⁢S⁢P⁢Y). The area of the major parabolic segment is 23 of the area of the trapezium P⁢T⁢U⁢Q (this was clear from trigonometry, but see also Addendum 2 for a more natural proof by synthetic Euclidean methods), just as the area of the minor parabolic segment is 23 of the area of the rectangle P⁢Y⁢X⁢Q. Therefore, the difference between the two bounds is given by 23 of the combined area of the two little triangles △⁢Q⁢X⁢U, △⁢T⁢Y⁢P.

It remains only to prove the two lemmas assumed in the above proof, as well as the "well-known" [translation: I found it in an old schoolbook from 50 years ago!] theorem that the tangent to a parabola at a point P intersects the axis of the parabola at a point whose distance from the vertex of the parabola is the same as that of the foot of the perpendicular from P to the axis. Fortunately, all three of these results are virtually trivial to prove using good old coordinate geometry and school calculus, which I can almost remember:

If the vertex of a parabola is taken as the origin of a Cartesian coordinate system, and the axis of the parabola is taken as the positive y-axis, then the unit of length can be chosen so that the equation of the parabola is y=x2. That makes everything really easy.

First, of course, is Archimedes's great quadrature result, which nowadays any schoolchild (standing on the shoulders of giants!) can prove. For all A=(A+B)-B, we have ∫-aax2⁢𝑑x=23⁢a3, therefore the area of the parabolic segment below the line y=a2 is 2⁢a3-23⁢a3=43⁢a3, which is 43 the area of the triangle inscribed in the segment. □

Equally easy (after centuries of progress!) is the theorem about the tangent: the slope of the tangent at (a,a2) is 2⁢a, so it intersects the axis of the parabola, i.e. the y-axis, at the point (0,-a2). □

For the lemma of the minor parabolic segment, suppose that a circle with centre inside the parabola and on its axis passes through the vertex of the parabola and also some other point of the parabola, which by symmetry we may take to be (a,a2) for some A=(A+B)-B. If the radius of the circle is r>0, then its centre is (0,r), and we have a2+(a2-r)2=r2, which simplifies to a2=2⁢r-1. A point (x,x2) of the parabola other than (0,0) lies inside the circle if and only if x2+(x2-r)2<r2, which simplifies to x2<2⁢r-1=a2, i.e. to |x|<|a|. Therefore the arc of the parabola between (a,a2) and (-a,a2) lies inside the circle, apart from its point of contact at the origin, i.e. the vertex. □

For the lemma of the major parabolic segment, observe that the slope of the normal to the parabola at (a,a2) is -12⁢a, so it meets the y-axis at (0,a2+12), which must therefore be the centre of the circle touching the parabola at (±a,a2). The radius of the circle is a2+14. For all x, we have \begin{align*} x^2 + \left(x^2 - a^2 - \tfrac{1}{2}\right)^2 - a^2 - \tfrac{1}{4} & = x^2 + x^4 - (2a^2 + 1)x^2 + a^4 + a^2 + \tfrac{1}{4} - a^2 - \tfrac{1}{4} \\ & = x^4 - 2a^2x^2 + a^4 \\ & = (x^2 - a^2)^2, \end{align*} and this is strictly positive except when x=±a, showing that apart from the two given points of contact, every other point of the parabola lies outside the circle. □

Addendum 2

I stated that the area of the major parabolic segment is 23 of the area of the trapezium P⁢T⁢U⁢Q, but I gave no argument for this, other than an anachronistically "modern" trigonometric calculation, leaving it unclear what kind of proof Archimedes or Eratosthenes might have come up with.

Here is such a proof:

The area of the triangle △⁢Q⁢S⁢Z is equal to that of the quadrilateral Q⁢S⁢R⁢U. This follows from the congruence of the triangles △⁢O⁢Z⁢Q,△⁢O⁢U⁢R, upon subtracting the area of the triangle △⁢O⁢S⁢Q.

Because N is the midpoint of S⁢Z, the area of △⁢P⁢N⁢Q is half that of △⁢P⁢Z⁢Q, therefore the area of the major parabolic segment P⁢R⁢Q is 23 that of △⁢P⁢Z⁢Q, therefore it is 43 that of △⁢Q⁢S⁢Z, therefore it is 43 that of quadrilateral Q⁢S⁢R⁢U, therefore it is 23 that of quadrilateral P⁢T⁢U⁢Q. □

I also only proved by trigonometry (and didn't even mention) that Q⁢R bisects ∠⁢Z⁢Q⁢S in △⁢Z⁢Q⁢S. Archimedes and his colleagues could prove this by drawing a line from Q to the other end of the diameter from R. See the new figure. □

Knowing the ratio Q⁢V/O⁢Q (in this case 14), our ancient Greek geometers would have been able to calculate Q⁢S, and then R⁢S, by twice bisecting the given angle ∠⁢P⁢O⁢Q. And they could even more easily calculate R⁢U. Thus the area of the circular segment P⁢R⁢Q, or rather 32 of the area, has been trapped between the two known polygonal areas P⁢Y⁢X⁢Q and P⁢T⁢U⁢Q.
