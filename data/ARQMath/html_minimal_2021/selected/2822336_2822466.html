If you were to do this in your head, the first thing to note is that it's not the euler function, but rather the period that matters. For 100, this is 20. That is 100∣x20-1.

The way to find this out, is to take the lowest common multiple of the euler-function of each prime-power, so lcm(ϕ(4),ϕ(25). The reason for this can be seen in the period for 91 in decimal. Although ϕ⁢(91)=72, the cycles of 7 (ie ϕ⁢(7)=6 and 13 (ϕ⁢(13)=12) run side by side.

Useful to know is that 7 has 5 as a sevenite, that is, 52∣75-1-1.

This means we are looking first for 11x⁢ for ⁢x(mod20), and then 7x⁢ for ⁢x(mod4).

So 5x=1(mod4) always

Then 7x=7(mod20) when x=1, mod 4

Then 117=71(mod100), is easy to find on pascal's triangle.

Adding 13 to the mix presents no problem. 71 reduces to 11, modulo 20.

We then seek 1311. Note here that 132=1,7,-1 base 10. Its tenth power is then 1310=,,,5×7,-1 or 49. We find then that 13*49=13*50-13, or 50-13, or 37.

Because 100 is divisible by its period-length, one can continue to use the tower of primes without further adjustments.
