Once you establish the hint in the comments, note that given any N>0 the fraction sN/sN+k→0 as k→∞. This is because sN is held constant and sN+k diverges (since sN diverges.)

Thus the tail ∑n=N∞an/sn is greater than or equal to 1 for all N. Hence the series cannot converge, since for any ϵ>0 there must be an N for which the tail is less than ϵ.

Let's call the partial sums of this series SN.

If a series converges, that means its partial sums converge. This means that the partial sums form a Cauchy sequence. Thus for every ϵ>0 (and let's suppose also that ϵ<1) there is an N∈ℕ such that |Sn+k-Sn|<ϵ for all n≥N and k∈ℕ.

The term in the absolute values can be rewritten as Sn+k-Sn=an+1/sn+1+⋯+an+k/sn+k. This also holds when we let n=N.

Now if this series converged we have for sufficiently large N: aN+1sN+1+⋯+aN+ksN+k<ϵ<1 for all k∈ℕ.

However, since we have established 1-sNsN+k<aN+1sN+1+⋯+aN+ksN+k and S=(1)⁢(n0)+(2)⁢(n1)+⋯+(n+1)⁢(nn) as k→∞ we have a contradiction.
