to show that (p-1)!≡-1⁢(p) without explicitly use group theory, maybe the simplest path is: (the following assumes p is odd, but if p=2 then the result is immediate)

  1. given n≠0, all values n,2⁢n,…⁢(p-1) n are different mod p. Otherwise, if h⁢n≡k⁢n⁢(p) then (h-k)⁢n≡0⁢(p) against the hypothesis that p is prime.

  2. this means that each n has an inverse mod p, that is for each n there is a m such that m⁢n≡1⁢(p).

  3. the equation x2≡1⁢(p) may be written as (x+1)⁢(x-1)≡0⁢(p); therefore its only solutions are x≡1⁢(p) and x≡-1⁢(p). For each other number n, an inverse m must exist (because of the pigeonhole principle) but m≠n.

  4. we are nearly done. Let's couple every number from 2 to p-2 with its own inverse. Their product is 1⁢(p), so they don't count in the overall total. 1 does not count either; it remains just p-1, that is -1⁢(p) as requested.

For 2., it is vital that the inverse is unique. Otherwise, this is a very nice summary
if the inverse were not unique, we would have h and k such that nh ≡ 1 and nk ≡ 1, and we fall again in case 1.
