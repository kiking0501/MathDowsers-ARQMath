Question about the proof of Ramsey's Theorem

I was reading up on a proof of Ramsey's Theorem and I can't understand this part of the proof:

Pick a vertex v from the graph, and partition the remaining vertices into two sets M and N, such that for every vertex w, w is in M if (v,w) is blue, and w is in N if (v,w) is red. Because the graph has R⁢(r-1,s)+R⁢(r,s-1)=|M|+|N|+1 vertices, it follows that either |M|≥R⁢(r⁢−⁢1,s) or |N|≥R⁢(r,s⁢−⁢1).

Why is that |M|≥R⁢(r⁢−⁢1,s) or |N|≥R⁢(r,s⁢−⁢1)? I know from the proof of R⁢(3,3)=6 that given a vertex, at least 3 are guaranteed to be either red or blue, so what is the pigeonhole argument for the general case?

combinatorics graph-theory

If |M|⁢\ngeq⁢R⁢(r-1,s) and |N|⁢\ngeq⁢R⁢(r,s-1), then |M|≤R⁢(r-1,s)-1 and |N|≤R⁢(r,s-1)-1. Therefore, number of vertices =|M|+|N|+1≤R⁢(r-1,s)-1+R⁢(r,s-1)-1+1. That is,|M|+|N|+1≤R⁢(r-1,s)+R⁢(r,s-1)-1 which is a contradiction to |M|+|N|+1=R⁢(r-1,s)+R⁢(r,s-1)
