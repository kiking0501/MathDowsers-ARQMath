Assume all the sets are infinite and disjoint. Convince yourself that this is the worst case; if their union is countable we are done.

I'll go beyond set theory using just very elementary facts about positive integers: (i) there are infinitely many prime numbers (ii) a number that is a power of one prime number can not be the power of another prime number (unique factorization theorem).

Let p1,p2,p3⁢… be the list of prime numbers in the increasing order.

Let Pj={pjk∣k⁢ a positive integer} consist of all powers of the jth prime. Clearly the sets Pj are disjoint and are all countably infinite. And ⋃j=1∞Pj is a (proper) subset of positive integers and hence is countable.

Now take your sets A1,A2,…,Am.

By countability hypothesis we can list the elements of Aj as {aj⁢1,aj⁢2,…} Now we can set up bijections this way: fj:Aj→Pj by f⁢(aj⁢k)=pjk for each j=1,2⁢…,m.

So the union of Aj has the same cardinality as the union of Pj's and the latter is countable.

This proof works even in the case of countably many countable sets.
