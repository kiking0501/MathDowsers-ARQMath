@MooS thanks - I've found a proof now by searching "closed immersions are stable under base change" rather than my more prosaic question title. I guess one needs to ask questions in the right language with algebraic geometry to get the standard answers :)

Let φ,ψ:X→Y be morphisms of S-schemes; by definition the following diagrams commute: \begin{equation} \require{AMScd} \begin{CD} X @>\varphi>> Y\\ @V\psi VV & @VV\upsilon V\\ Y @>>\upsilon> S\\ Y\times_SY @>pr_1>> Y\\ @Vpr_2VV & @VV\upsilon V\\ Y @>>\upsilon> S \end{CD}; \end{equation} by universal property of Y×SY, there exists a unique S-morphism of schemes (φ,ψ):X→Y×SY such that: \begin{equation} pr_1\circ(\varphi,\psi)=\varphi,\,pr_2\circ(\varphi,\psi)=\psi. \end{equation} Let ΔY/S=Δ be the diagonal S-morphism of Y; considering the diagram: \begin{equation} \require{AMScd} \begin{CD} Z @>j>> Y\\ @V i VV & @VV\Delta V\\ X @>>(\varphi,\psi)> Y\times_SY \end{CD} \end{equation} where Z is the fibre product of the previous diagram.

By construction, (Z,i) is a locally closed subscheme of X, because Δ is a locally closed morphism of S-schemes; if Δ is a closed morphism of S-schemes, that is Y is a separated S-scheme, then (Z,i) is a closed subscheme of X.
