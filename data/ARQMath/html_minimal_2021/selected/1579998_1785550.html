Fractals using just modulo operation

Let us calculate the remainder after division of 27 by 10.

27≡7(mod10)

We have 7. So let's calculate the remainder after divison of 27 by 7.

27≡6(mod7)

Ok, so let us continue with 6 as the divisor...

27≡3(mod6)

Getting closer...

27≡0(mod3)

Good! We have finally reached 0 which means we are unable to continue the procedure.

Let's make a function that counts the modulo operations we need to perform until we finally arrive at 0.

So we find some remainder r1 after division of some a by some b, then we find some remainder r2 after division of a by r1 and we repeat the procedure until we find such index i that ri=0.

Therefore, let M⁢(a,b)=i-1

for a,b∈ℕ,b≠0 (I like to call it "modulity of a by b", thence M)

For our example: M⁢(27,10)=3.

Notice that M⁢(a,b)=0⇔b|a (this is why i-1 feels nicer to me than just i)

What we see here is the common divisor plot that's already been studied extensively by prime number researchers.

Here are some rediscovered (but fairly old) connections between the analysis of Euclid's GCD algorithm, the Farey series dissection of the continuum, and continued fractions. Some of these topics are all treated in chs. 3 and 10 in (1) by Hardy and Wright. Long time ago the author of this response asked this question in the newsgroup sci.math and this is a collected summary with some new findings, after the main responder's analysis, that of Gerry Myerson. Additional contributions and thanks to Dave L. Renfro, James Waldby, Paris Pamfilos, Robert Israel, Herman Rubin and Joe Riel. References may be a little mangled.

Introduction

When studying the asymptotic density distribution of ϕ⁢(n)/n or of ϕ⁢(n) versus n, both graphs display certain trend lines around which many positive integers accumulate. On the ϕ⁢(n)/n graph they are horizontal, while on the ϕ⁢(n) versus n graph they have varying slopes r, with 0≤r≤1. This density distribution has been studied extensively. Schoenberg, for example in [9, pp. 193-194] showed that ϕ⁢(n)/n has a continuous distribution function D⁢(r) (also in [4, p.96]). Later in [10, p.237] he proved that under fairly general conditions D⁢(r) exists for a multiplicative function, leading to necessary and sufficient conditions for the existence and continuity of such a D for an additive arithmetical function. Erdos showed ([3, p. 96]) that D⁢(r) for ϕ⁢(n)/n is purely singular, hence trend lines r⁢x exist for almost any r∈[0,1]. Weingartner in [11, p. 2680] and Erdos in [2, p. 527] derived explicit bounds for the number of integers such that ϕ⁢(n)/n≤r. Here we first briefly try to explain those trend lines and then we present a theorem which suggests that they follow certain fractal patterns related to the Farey series 𝔉n of order n, which exist in a graph which times the asymptotic performance of the Euclidian GCD algorithm. Because the timing of the Euclidian GCD algorithm is involved, this theorem can ultimately be used to speed-up factorization by trial and error searches. Additionally, these trend lines are also connected with a certain function which plays a role in tetration.

Notation

To avoid complicated notation, we always notate primes with p, q and pi, qi, with i∈I=\set⁢1,2,…,t, t∈\Natural an appropriate finite index set of naturals. Naturals will be notated with m, n, k, etc. Reals will be notated with x, y, r, a, ϵ, etc. \floor⁢x denotes the familiar floor function. Functions will be notated with f, g, etc. The Greatest Common Divisor of m and n will be denoted with \GCD⁢(m,n). When we talk about the \GCD algorithm, we explicitly mean the Euclidean algorithm for \GCD⁢(m,n).

Theorem 3.1: The non-prime trend lines on the graph of ϕ⁢(n) versus n follow the functions f⁢(r,s,x)=r⁢x/s, with r=∏p|n(p-1)/(q-1), s=∏p|np/q, where q is the largest prime q|n.

For n=3k⁢q, q>3, ϕ⁢(n)=n⁢(1-1/3)⁢(1-1/q)=(2⁢n/3)⁢(q-1)/q. For large q, again t=(q-1)/q∼1, hence also for large n, ϕ⁢(n)∼2⁢n/3, consequently these numbers follow the trend f⁢(2,3,x)=2⁢x/3.

Generalizing, for n=pk⁢q, q>p, ϕ⁢(n)=n⁢(1-1/p)⁢(1-1/q)=(p-1)/p⁢(q-1)/q. For large q, again t=(q-1)/q∼1, hence also for large n, ϕ⁢(n)∼(p-1)⁢n/p, consequently these numbers follow the trend f⁢(p-1,p,x)=(p-1)⁢x/p.

For n=2k⁢3l⁢q, q>3, ϕ⁢(n)=n⁢(1-1/2)⁢(1-1/3)⁢(1-1/q)=(n/2)⁢(2/3)⁢(q-1)/q=(n/3)⁢(q-1)/q, hence again for large n, ϕ⁢(n)∼n/3, consequently these numbers follow the trend f⁢(1,3,x)=x/3.

For n=2k⁢5l⁢q, q>5, ϕ⁢(n)=n⁢(1-1/2)⁢(1-1/5)⁢(1-1/q)=(2⁢n/5)⁢(q-1)/q, hence the trend is f⁢(2,5,x)=2⁢x/5.

The Farey series 𝔉n of order n≥2 ([5, p.23]), is the ascending series of irreducible fractions between 0 and 1 whose denominators do not exceed n. Thus, h/k∈𝔉n, if 0≤h≤k≤n, \GCD⁢(h,k)=1. Individual terms of a specific Farey series of order n≥2 are indexed by m≥1, with the first term being 0 and the last 1. Maple code for creating the Farey series of order n is given in the Appendix.

Theorem 3.2: The Farey series 𝔉n of order n satisfies the following identities:

⁢\absFnamp;=\absF-n1+ϕ(n)⁢\absFnamp;=1+∑=m1nϕ(m)

Proof: By induction on n. 𝔉2=\set⁢0,1/2,1, hence \abs⁢𝔉2=3, since there are 3 irreducible fractions of order n=2. Note that the irreducible fractions of order n are necessarily equal to the irreducible fractions of order n-1 plus \abs⁢\set⁢k/n:k≤n,\GCD⁢(k,n)=1=ϕ⁢(n), and the first identity follows. The second identity follows as an immediate consequence of the first identity and induction, and the theorem follows.

In [5, p.23], we find the following theorem:

Theorem 3.3: If 0<ξ<1 is any real number, and n a positive integer, then there is an irreducible fraction h/k such that 0<k≤n, \abs⁢ξ-h/k≤1/k/(n+1).

We can now reformulate Theorem 3.1, which follows as a consequence of Theorem 3.3.

Corollary 3.4: The trend lines on the graph of ϕ⁢(n) versus n follow the functions g⁢(n,m,x)=𝔉n,m⋅x.

Proof: Note that for large n=pk, ϕ⁢(n)/n→1. For large n=∏ipiki, ϕ⁢(n)/n→1/ζ⁢(1)=0. Putting ξ=ϕ⁢(n)/n, Theorem 3.3 guarantees the existence of an irreducible fraction h/k, and some n, such that ϕ⁢(n)/n is close to a member h/k of 𝔉n and the result follows.

The trend lines on the graph of ϕ⁢(n) versus n are completely (and uniquely) characterized by either description. For example, consider the factorizations 2k⁢3l⁢5m⁢q, with q>5 and k,l,m≥0. Then if n=2k⁢q, ϕ⁢(n)/n∼1/2, if n=3l⁢q, ϕ⁢(n)/n∼2/3, if n=5m⁢q, ϕ⁢(n)/n∼4/5, if n=2k⁢3l⁢q, ϕ⁢(n)/n∼1/3, if n=2k⁢5m⁢q, ϕ⁢(n)/n∼2/5, if n=3l⁢5m⁢q, ϕ⁢(n)/n∼8/15∼2/3, if n=2k⁢3l⁢5m⁢q, ϕ⁢(n)/n∼4/15∼1/3, all of which are close to members of 𝔉5=\set⁢0,1/5,1/4,1/3,2/5,1/2,3/5,2/3,3/4,4/5,1.

We will need the following counting theorem.

Theorem 4.1: If i∈\Natural, L=\set⁢a1,a2,…,ai a set of distinct numbers ai∈\Natural and N∈\Natural, then the number of numbers n≤N of the form n=∏iaiki for some ki∈\Natural, is given by S⁢(L,N), where,

=⁢S(L,N){⁢\floorloga⁢\absL(N)⁢amp;, if =⁢\absL1∖∑=k1⁢\floorloga⁢\absL(N)⁢S\parenL⁢\seta⁢\absL,⁢\floorNa⁢\absLk⁢amp;, otherwise

Proof: We use induction on i=\abs⁢L. When i=1, the number of numbers n≤N of the form n=ak is exactly \floor⁢loga⁡(N). The Timing of the Euclidian GCD Algorithm

The Euclidean GCD algorithm has been analyzed extensively (see 6 for example). For two numbers with m and n digits respectively, it is known to be O⁢((m+n)2) in the worst case if one uses the crude algorithm. This can be shortened to O⁢((m+n)⋅log⁡(m+n)⋅log⁡(log⁡(m+n))), and if one uses the procedure which gets the smallest absolute remainder, trivially the length of the series is logarithmic in m+n. So the worst time, using the crude algorithm, is O⁢((m+n)2⋅log⁡(m+n)), with the corresponding bound for the asymptotically better cases. It has been proved by Gabriel Lame that the worst case occurs when m and n are successive Fibonacci numbers.

Using the Maple code on the Appendix, in figure 6 we show the timing performance graph of the Euclidean GCD algorithm as a function of how many steps it takes to terminate for integers m and n, relative to the maximum number of steps. Darker lines correspond to faster calculations. The time performance of \GCD⁢(m,n) is exactly equal to the time performance of \GCD⁢(n,m), hence the graph of figure 6 is symmetric with respect to the line m=n.

Practical Considerations of Theorem 6.1

What is the practical use (if any) of theorem 6.1? The first obvious use is that one can make a fairly accurate probabilistic statement about the speed of \GCD⁢(m,n) for specific m and n, by `inspecting' the ϵ-density of the line r⁢x, where r=m/n (or T1=T0⁢ek⁢L+A⁢(L-ek⁢L-1k)+B⁢(1-ek⁢L).). The real practical use of this theorem however, lies not so much in determining the actual speed of a specific GCD calculation (that's pretty much impossible by the probabilistic nature of the theorem), rather, in avoiding trying to factorize a large number m, when the ratio r=p/m for various known primes p determines lines r⁢x of relatively low ϵ-density on figure 6. The latter can effectively be used to speed up factorization searches by trial and error, acting as an additional sieve which avoids such timely unfavorable primes and picks primes for which \GCD⁢(m,p) runs to completion relatively fast.
