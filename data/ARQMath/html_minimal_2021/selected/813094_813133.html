Imaginary unit i is not a limit of a real Cauchy sequence

I saw this in some book once and it has been bugging me. The book had, I think as the first exercise it mentioned, to prove that the imaginary unit i=-1 is not a limit of any real valued Cauchy sequence.

Given all I know of complex numbers, this shouldn't seem so hard to show. Like, I know any Cauchy sequence converges in ℝ and elements of ℝ have total ordering and elements of ℂ cannot and so on, but still I have great doubts of how this is supposed to be done.

I think you should use the algebraic property that i2=-1 and that multiplication is continuous. In other words, without knowing about the complex numbers beforehand, you are supposed to show that there exists no Cauchy sequence in ℝ that converges to a number whose square is -1.

Hint: If an is real, then the distance from an to i is an2+1, which is always ≥1.

For an ϵ-N argument that the limit of the an is not i, choose ϵ=12.
