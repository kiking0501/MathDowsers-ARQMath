@JakeWickham It is good that you have asked this question. The answer is : there is a difference between *having an infinite decimal expansion* and *being infinite*. The reason is very simple : The sum of infinitely many numbers can possibly be finite, and that is what is happening. 1.414213=1+410+1100+41000+…. You are adding infinitely many numbers, but the sum *remains finite*. To be *infinite* is , in some sense, to be *larger in absolute value than any natural number*, you could say.
For example, the cardinality of the natural numbers is infinite, because it is larger than any natural number you can think of. In our case, it is clear that the irrational number in question is smaller than 2, hence is finite. If you still do not see the difference, I can explain again, so make sure you are clear.

All irrational numbers have an infinite expansion (when expressed in a rational base). Suppose one had a finite decimal expansion then you could express it as an integer divided by a sufficiently large power of 10 - contradicting its irrationality.
You forgot the ellipses. Did you believe that your calculator was telling you that 2=1.41421356237 (full stop) or 2=1.41421356237⁢…. Your calculator meant the latter: more digits follow. Equivalently, do you believe 1/3=0.33333333333 (full stop) or =0.33333333333⁢…?
Don't take calculator output literally. Calculator outputs are approximate; your calculator might give a terminating decimal result for 2, but that's not the actual square root.

The square root of two cannot exactly be written out on a computer screen in decimal notation – the digits extend forever. You're looking at an approximation accurate to a lot of digits. The approximation itself, however, is rational, as you have demonstrated. If you expanded it out to more digits, however, you'd realize that your quantity has zeros extending out to infinity, while 2 has more digits. Therefore your rational number does not contradict the proof given by the mathematical community that 2 is irrational.

Note: just because a number is irrational doesn't mean it can't be arbitrarily well approximated by rational numbers – every number, rational and irrational can, and that property is known as "the rational numbers are dense in the reals." For instance, for 2: \begin{align} \frac{1}{1}, \frac{14}{10}, \frac{141}{100}, \frac{1414}{1000}, \frac{14142}{10000}, ... \end{align} get arbitrarily close to 2, but none of them are equal to it.

Related topic: if you're interested in rational numbers which quickly converge to irrational numbers, check out continued fractions. They're really cool. Interesting numbers like 2 often have continued fraction expansions with closed-form coefficients – 2 can be represented by a continued fraction with first coefficient 1 and all following coefficients 2. You can do all sorts of weird things with this, like show that the Golden Ratio (which has a continued fraction expansion of all 1's) is (one of) the irrational number(s) which takes the longest to converge to, or in more plain concepts, the rational numbers used to approximate it require a really large denominator to do a good job.
