One one hand, 10=∞; on the other, ∞⁢∉⁢ℝ.

The usual meaning of a/b=c is that a=b⋅c. Since for b=0 we have 0⋅x=0 for any x, there simply isn't any c such that 1=0⋅c, unless we throw the properties of arithmetic to the garbage (i.e. adding new elements which do not respect laws like a⁢(x+y)=a⁢x+a⁢y).

So "undefined" or "not a number" is the most correct answer possible.

However:

It is sometimes useful to break the laws of arithmetic by adding new elements such as "∞" and even defining 1/0=∞. It is very context-dependent and assumes everyone understands what's going on. This is certainly not something to be stated to kids as some general law of Mathematics.

Also:

I believe that the common misconception of "1/0=∞" comes from elementary Calculus, where the following equality holds: limx→0+⁡1x=∞. This cannot be simplified to a statement like 10=∞ because of two problems:

  1. limx→0-⁡1x=-∞, so the "direction" of the limit matters; moreover, because of this, P⁢(A∪B)≥P⁢(A). is undefined.
  2. By writing lim⁡f⁢(x)=∞ we don't really mean that something gets the value "∞" - in Calculus ∞ is what we call "potential infinity" - it describes a property of a function (namely, that for every N>0 we can find xN such that f⁢(xN)>N and xN is in some specific neighborhood).
