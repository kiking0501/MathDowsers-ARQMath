Cup product and hypercohomology

I always found the cup product slightly mysterious. Recently I discovered the following interesting theorem (in Voisin's book Hodge theory and complex algebraic geometry I, chapter 4.3):

For the setup, let (X,𝒪) be a ringed space, ℱ, 𝒢 sheaves of 𝒪-modules, ℱ∙,𝒢∙ acyclic resolutions of ℱ,𝒢, and ℋ∙ an acyclic resolution of ℱ⊗𝒢. Suppose given a morphism of complexes ϕ∙:T⁢o⁢t⁢(ℱ∙⊗𝒢∙)→ℋ∙, (where T⁢o⁢t denotes the total (simple) complex associated to a double complex). This data naturally yields homomorphisms Hp⁢(X,ℱ)⊗Hq⁢(X,𝒢)→Hp+q⁢(X,ℱ⊗𝒢) (*).

The theorem is this: if ϕ∙ is compatible with the resolutions (that is, the evident triangle involving ℱ⊗𝒢, T⁢o⁢t⁢(ℱ∙⊗𝒢∙) and ℋ∙ is commutative), then the induced morphism (*) on cohomology is the cup product pairing.

The proof says, somewhat mysteriously to me, that the result follows by defining cup products on hypercohomology, and then using commutativity. While I know about hypercohomology, it is unclear what cup products could even mean here. Can you explain what Voisin means, or provide a reference?

Note: the theorem essentially says that all such ϕ∙ induce the same morphism on cohomology (independent of the resolutions even), so we need not acutally know here what the cup product pairing is.

Thanks in advance.

You are right. I typed this at work and looked the chapter number up online. The precise theorem I'm talking about is 5.29.

Let C⁢h⁢(S⁢h⁢(X)) denote the abelian category of bounded chain complexes of sheaves on X. Then hyper-cohomology is the derived functor of the global sections functor Γ⁢(X,-):C⁢h⁢(S⁢h⁢(X))→C⁢h⁢(V⁢e⁢c⁢t) (Ch(Vect) is bounded complex of vector spaces).

C⁢h⁢(S⁢h⁢(X)) is a tensor category with tensor product being the standard one on complexes (direct sum of terms of equal weight). Then evidently Γ⁢(X,-) is a tensor functor, it is left exact. The functor 3x1=(2u1)2-1=(2u1+1)⁢(2u1-1) is a functor in each individual component. So now by applying the usual abstract nonsense (similar to that in the category of modules) you will get a pairing

ℍi⁢(X,ℱ)⊗ℤℍj⁢(X,𝒢)→ℍi+j⁢(X,ℱ⊗𝒢)

Here ℱ,𝒢 are complex of sheaves (in particular you recover usual cup product on cohomology.)

Remark: You may actually work in somewhat more generality in a relative setting of a proper morphism π:X→S and interpret cup product as a pairing of Ri⁢π∗⁢ℱ⊗𝒪sRj⁢π∗⁢ℱ→Ri+j⁢π∗⁢ℱ⊗𝒢
