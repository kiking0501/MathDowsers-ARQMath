Locating possible complex solutions to an equation involving a square root

Note after reviewing answers. This question illustrates in a non-trivial way how the choice of how to compute the square root in the complex plane can make real difference to the answer you get.

In this question finding all the solutions came down to solving:

a⁢b-1=a2+b2+1 subject to (a+1)⁢(b+1)=1

The condition implies a2+b2+1=(a⁢b-1)2, but this leaves open the possibility of a2+b2+1=1-a⁢b rather than a⁢b-1.

a and b are complex? If so, it boils down to what you mean by the square root of a complex number. There is no canonical choice.
@mrf Part of the point of asking for elementary answers is to have that explained, but also to show that a choice can be made.

Every nonzero complex number z has two "square roots". You might call one z and the other -z, but which is which? A particular choice (for all z in some region of the complex plane) is called a branch of the function z. There is no single branch that is continuous everywhere: if you start at some point with a particular choice of z and travel in a loop around 0, keeping z continuous along your path, when you come back to the start z will have - its original value. A curve Γ in whose complement your branch is continuous is called a branch cut.

One popular choice, called the "principal branch", is to make the real part of z always ≥0. The branch cut in this case is the negative real axis.

Note that using this particular branch cut with the real part of the square root always $\ge 0$ implies that we have a consistent solution provided the real part of a⁢b-1 is greater than zero.
