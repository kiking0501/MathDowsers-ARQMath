Kronecker's Theorem - what can be deduced if f is reducible?

I am happy with the statement of Kroncker's Theorem as follows. Let f∈K⁢[X]. Then there exists a simple field extension L=K⁢(α) of K with f⁢(α)=0. If f is irreducible over K, then the extension L is unique in sense that the natural map K⁢[X]/f⁢K⁢[X]→L,g+f⁢K⁢[X]→g⁢(α) constitutes an isomorphism.

However, I am trying to prove something at present and need to understand what can be said if f is actually reducible. If f is reducible, then it does not generate a field at all. For example, if f=X2 then there is no polynomial g such that X⁢g=1 modulo f. This generalises to other f and does not depend on the field K.

However, the irreducible factors of f do generate a field.
