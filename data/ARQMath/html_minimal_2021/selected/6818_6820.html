Here is a handwaving argument that the series diverges for all k∈(0,2): Given k, there will be a certain density of n for which k+c⁢o⁢s⁢n<1. A "random sample" of the harmonic series will diverge by the same argument that shows the harmonic series diverges. Our series will be greater, term by term, than the harmonic series that has numerator 0 if k+c⁢o⁢s⁢n>1 and numerator 1 if k+c⁢o⁢s⁢n<1

Is every positive-density sub-series of the harmonic series divergent?
I think so, at least for a density bounded away from zero. What you need for an easy proof is positive density in terms (2^n+1,2^(n+1)). For a given positive density e, there must be an N such that the number of elements less than n in the series is greater than n*e/2 for n>N. So once we get big enough, there are 2^n*e/2 terms by term 2^n, each >1/2^n. Then there are 2^n*e/2 more by 2^(n+1), which contribute e/2 and so on.
