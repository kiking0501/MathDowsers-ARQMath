Why is right-continuity important in the martingale convergence theorem

Let (Xt) be a right-continuous super-martingale such that supt⁡E⁢[Xt-]<∞. Then limt→∞⁡Xt=X∞ a.s. where X∞ is integrable. I am trying to prove this.

I know the proof for the countable case, i.e. if (Xn) is a super-martingale and if supn⁡E⁢[Xn-]<∞ then limn→∞⁡Xn=X∞ a.s. where X∞ is integrable. (Here n∈ℕ).

On the one hand I understand that without right-continuity the limit limt→∞⁡Xt might not be measurable. On the other, I am unable to understand the flaw with the following line of thought.

Choose tn↑∞. Then Xtn is a super-martingale and hence limn→∞⁡Xtn=X∞t exists a.s. Similarly, let sn↑∞. Then Xsn is a super-martingale and hence limn→∞⁡Xsn=X∞s exists a.s. But, if we form an increasing sequence hn from the union {tn}∪{sn} and call the corresponding limit X∞h. Then X∞h=X∞t=X∞t a.s. as tn,sn are sub-sequences of hn

Therefore, the limit of any sub-sequence of Xt is the same and hence the limit limt→∞⁡Xt exists a.s. irrespective of right continuity. I am sure this must be a wrong proof but I cannot understand where the mistake is.

Using your argumentation, we get an exceptional null set N=N⁢((sn),(tn)) such that X∞s⁢(ω)=X∞t⁢(ω)  for all⁢ω∈Ω\N for any two sequences (sn)n and (tn)n. The problem is the following: There exist uncountably many sequences (sn) and (tn) such that sn→∞ and tn→∞. This means that we have to deal with uncountably many null sets N⁢(s,t) - and an uncountable union of null sets is in general not a null set; therefore we cannot use this argumentation to conclude that the limit X∞ exists.
