Extended Euclidean algorithm gives the answer.
To really get into Galois field inverses you would need to replace x8+1 with an irreducible degree eight polynomial. See for example [this thread](https://math.stackexchange.com/q/529156/11619) for a well worked out example about finding the inverse of an element in a degree eight extension field.
+1 for having a correct example. Your other degree seven polynomial is x7+x6+x5+x4+x3+x2+x+1=(x+1)7. Basically because all the binomial coefficients (7k),k=0,1,…,7 are odd. The six term polynomial is divisible by x+1, so the product of those two degree seven polynomials is divisible by (x+1)8=x8+1, and hence equal to zero in the quotient ring.

I'm afraid the element you described has no inverse in the quotient ring ℤ2⁢[x]/⟨x8+1⟩.

This is because your degree seven polynomial f⁢(x)=x7+x5+x4+x2+x+1 shares a factor x+1 with g⁢(x)=x8+1. The easiest way of seeing this is to observe that f⁢(1)=0=g⁢(1) so they are both divisible by x-1=x+1.

You can tell it at a glance because both polynomials have an even number of terms.

The quotient ring ℤ2⁢[x]/⟨g⁢(x)⟩ is a field if and only if g⁢(x) is irreducible. When g⁢(x) is irreducible it obviously cannot have common factors with lower degree polynomials, and extended Euclid works like charm and finds the inverse.

A popular choice for a degree 8 irreducible polynomial is g⁢(x)=x8+x4+x3+x+1 specified in Rijndael/AES crypto standard<.

If h⁢(x) is any polynomial we still have h⁢(1)⁢f⁢(1)=0 and this makes it impossible for h⁢(x)⁢f⁢(x) to be congruent to 1 modulo g⁢(x).
