Every invertible sheaf on a variety has an isomorphic invertible fractional ideal

Let X be an irreducible variety, and let ℒ be a locally-coherent sheaf of rank 1. How do I prove that there exists a coherent subsheaf ℐ of the constant sheaf k⁢(X) such that ℐ≅ℒ?

Kempf gives a proof that I do not understand:

Fix an open dense subset V;

let σ∈ℒ⁢(V), and define ℐ⁢(U)={f∈k⁢(X)|f⋅σ⁢ comes from an element of ⁢ℒ⁢(U)}.

(a) Why not just take X itself as V, if we just want an open dense subset?

(b) What is the definition of ℐ⁢(U) exactly, i.e. what's the meaning of "comes from"?

(c) Why is this sheaf isomorphic to the sheaf ℒ?

I assume the coherence of ℐ is automatic because it is isomorphic to ℒ.

For future reference, this seems to be lemma 5.5.1 in Kempf's _Algebraic varieties_.

(a) Because ℒ might not have any global sections, i.e. ℒ⁢(X) might be empty.

(b) Taking the stalk at the generic point, you can view σ as an element of Frac⁢(ℒ⁢(U))=k⁢(X). In particular, it makes sense to ask if f⋅σ∈ℒ⁢(U).

(c) One way is to write down an isomorphism of sheaves G*=G where on each open U, we map ℐ⁢(U)→ℒ⁢(U) by sending f↦f⁢σ. By definition of ℐ⁢(U), this is well-defined is an isomorphism.
