Product of elements of a finite abelian group

Suppose G={a1,…,an} is a finite abelian group, and let x=a1⁢a2⁢⋯⁢an. Prove that if there is more than one element of order 2 then x=e.

What I've done so far: (#1 is just for illustration, There should be at least 3 such elements)

  1. G has an even number of elements with order &⁢g⁢t;2 (They are paired so: (a,a-1) ). And then there are e and c≤x<b. Also, if G has at least one element of order 2 then |G| is even (Lagrange). Thus |{b|o⁢(b)=2}| must be odd.

  2. If there is exactly one element b of order 2 then x=b. That's because G is abelian, and we can write: x=e⋅b⋅(a1⁢a1-1⁢⋯⁢an⁢an-1)=b⋅(e⁢⋯⁢e)=b.

  3. If there are exactly 3 such elements a,b,c∈G, then, as shown above, x=a⁢b⁢c and thus x=e (That's because (a⁢b⁢c)2=a2⁢b2⁢c2=e which means a⁢b⁢c∈{a,b,c,e}. If a⁢b⁢c=a then b=c-1=c which is false, same goes for a⁢b⁢c=b,a⁢b⁢c=c.)

I haven't managed to find a reason why the claim must hold for 5,7,… (i.e. All other odd integers). Does anyone have an idea?

Thanks in advance. Please excuse my English.

So you seem to have reduced this to noting that x is the product of the elements of order 2. These form a subgroup isomorphic to (ℤ/2⁢ℤ)m for some m. Consider what happens when you add all the elements of such a group (see how many 1's you add in each coordinate).
@TobiasKildetoft, or alternatively how many zeroes ;-) (ℤ/2⁢ℤ)m is a vector space of dimension 2 over ℤ/2, so the elements with zero in the first coordinate, say, are the solution of the homogeneous linear equation x1=0, so they form a subspace of codimension 1.
@TobiasKildetoft Could you please elaborate on how the elements of order 2 form a subgroup isomorphic to (ℤ/2⁢ℤ)m?
@Py42 - The sum or difference of elements of order 2 is an element of order 2 by distribution. The elements of order 2 are then a vector space over the field ℤ/2⁢ℤ. Since the group is finite, this vector space is finite dimensional, hence the claim that Tobias made.
This is [Wilson's theorem for abelian groups](http://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilson's_theorem#Gauss.27s_generalization). See also http://math.stackexchange.com/a/28918/589.
