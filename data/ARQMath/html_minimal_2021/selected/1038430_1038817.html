Euler characteristic, genus and cohomology: a deep connection?

For a smooth projective curve V over the complex numbers, the algebraic genus, defined as the dimension of the linear system L⁢(ω), where ω is the canonical divisor, coincides with the topological genus of the curve, found by counting holes via homology/cohomology groups or by triangulating the curve and computing the Euler characteristic. According to Hulek's 'Elementary Algebraic Geometry', this is

a deep fact.

Here is another deep fact: given a complex quasi-projective variety, the simplicial (we may substitute singular) cohomology coincides with the de Rham cohomology on the Riemann surface induced by the variety. Since the algebraic version of genus involves divisors given by differentials (measured by the de Rham cohomology) and the topological version can be read off from the singular cohomology groups (or found by counting faces, edges and vertices in a suitablevsimplicial complex), it is very tempting to jump to the conclusion that these two deep facts are closely related. Are they?

Follow-up: Can one derive interesting alternative versions of the genus by considering sheaf cohomology, Čech cohomology (induced by either the Zariski or the classical topology) and other cohomology theories on algebraic varieties?

I think, first and foremost, you have a bit of confusion regarding the two uses of the word 'differential' here.

On one hand, when you speak of the differentials as g=dim⁡H0⁢(X,ΩX/ℂ1) you're speaking of algebraic differentials on X. It follows from the general philosophy of the GAGA principle (although there is a simpler proof in this special case) that if Xan denotes the analylitification of X (i.e. the Riemann surface associated to X), then H0⁢(X,ΩX/ℂ1) coincides with H0⁢(Xan,ΩXan,hol1). In other words, the algebraic differentials on X correspond to the holomorphic differentials on Xan.

On the other hand, when you speak of de Rham cohomology, you are thinking of the smooth differentials on Xan (thought of as just a smooth real manifold here). Indeed, the statement

Hsingi⁢(Xan,ℝ)≅HdRi⁢(Xan) (known as de Rham's Theorem) involves purely the smooth (real) manifold structure of Xan, not its complex structure.

Now, that's not to say that you can't compute the singular cohomology of Xan from the holomorphic differentials, it's just more difficult. Namely, what is the proof of de Rham's theorem? Well, let's fix some smooth manifold M. Then,

0→ℝ¯→ΩM0→ΩM1→⋯→ΩMn→0

is a resolution of sheaves (here ℝ¯ denotes the constant sheaf). But, since smooth manifolds have partitions of unity, any x2+1/x2=23-module (x2+1/x2=23 is the sheaf of smooth functions) is a so-called 'soft' sheaf, and thus acyclic. So, the ΩMi's (being x2+1/x2=23-modules) are acyclic. So, the above tells us that we can compute the (sheaf) cohomology of ℝ¯ as the cohomology of the following chain complex (of abelian groups):

0→ΩM0⁢(M)→ΩM1⁢(M)→⋯→ΩMn⁢(M)→0 But, this is precisely the de Rham cohomology of M (by definition), and so we obtain the isomorphism

Hi⁢(M,ℝ¯)≅HdRi⁢(M) The, we can use the general fact that on a locally contractible space, (sheaf) cohomology of the constant sheaf agrees with singular cohomology to get

Hsingi⁢(M,ℝ)≅Hi⁢(M,ℝ¯)≅HdRi⁢(M) which is what we wanted.

You could try to run this argument through on some complex manifold 𝒳, replacing smooth differentials with with holomorphic differentials, in a hope to prove something like Hsingi⁢(𝒳,ℂ) is equal to the cohomology of the following chain of abelian groups

0→Ωhol1⁢(𝒳)→⋯→Ωholn⁢(𝒳)→0 (*)

Of course, something is fishy here. I used n to denote the index of termination both here, and in the sequence of differentials on the smooth manifold above. But, they're different n! If you view 𝒳 as a smooth manifold, the 'n' that shows up in the smooth de Rham sequence is the real dimension of 𝒳, and the n that shows up in this holomorphic de Rham sequence is the complex dimension of 𝒳. So, of course it can't be true that Hsingi⁢(𝒳,ℂ) is the cohomology of this chain of abelian groups coming from holomorphic differentials, else the singular cohomology of a smooth manifold would be concentrated in degrees 0 to n, of the (real) 2⁢n-dimensional smooth manifold 𝒳. This certainly isn't the case in general (think of ℙn).

What goes wrong? Well, it's still true that

0→ℂ¯→Ωhol1→⋯→Ωholn→0

is a resolution of sheaves. But, complex manifolds do NOT have holomorphic partitions of unity. So, there is no reason that the sheaves Ωholi should be acyclic, and, in general, they aren't (think about ℙ1 with Ωhol1=𝒪⁢(-2)). So, you can't compute

Hsingi⁢(𝒳,ℂ)≅Hi⁢(𝒳,ℂ¯)

from the cohomology of chain of abelian groups (∗).

That said, there is a way you could try and fix this. You can try and compute sheaf cohomology from any resolution of a sheaf, but you have to be more clever if the resolution isn't acyclic. In particular, you need the fancy notion of hypercohomology of a complex of sheaves. Roughly, this takes your complex of sheaves ℱ∙ (which could certainly not be acyclic) resolves each ℱ∙ by acyclics ℐℱ,∙ and then takes the cohomology of the total complex Tot⁢(ℐℱ,∙). In fancier language, this is just identifying your complex ℱ∙ with its associated object in the derived category of sheaves of abelian groups on X. It then chooses within its derived class an equivalent (i.e. quasi-isomorphic) complex of acyclics, and computes the (normal) cohomology of that complex (the cohomology of its sequence of global sections).

The amazing thing is that if ℍi⁢(ℱ∙) denotes the ith hypercohomology of the complex ℱ∙, and 0→ℱ→ℱ∙ is some resolution, then

Hi⁢(𝒳,ℱ)≅ℍi⁢(ℱ∙)

even though the ℱ∙ may not be a complex of acyclics.

So, what is true, is that while Hsingi⁢(𝒳,ℂ) may not just be the cohomology of (∗), it is true that Hsingi⁢(𝒳,ℂ)≅ℍi⁢(Ωhol∙), which is the closest one can get to a "holomorphic de Rham's theorem".

A truly amazing theorem of Grothendieck's (called the comparison theorem for algebraic de Rham cohomology) says that if now X/ℂ is some smooth quasiprojective scheme (variety, if you'd prefer) then

ℍi⁢(ΩXi)≅ℍi⁢(ΩXan,holi)

In words, the hypercohomology of the de Rham sequence of algebraic differentials is isomorphic the hypercohomology of the de Rham sequence of holomorphic differentials on Xan. But, by what we said above,

ℍi⁢(ΩXan,holi)≅Hsingi⁢(X,ℂ)

and so we see that

Hsingi⁢(Xan,ℂ)≅ℍi⁢(ΩXi)

So, we can, in fact, compute the singular cohomology of the analytification of X purely from the algebraic information of X's algebraic differentials, it's just slightly more complicated.

Remark: I mentioned the comparison theorem here for general flavor, but, as you'll see below, you need much, much less in this specific instance of its use. In particular, we are implicitly proving a special case of this theorem below.

There are many other important/really nice applications of Grothendieck's comparison theorem (beyond the obvious reality check that algebraic de Rham cohomology does what we want), but that's best left to another post).

Now that I've said the difference in the various incarnations of the word 'differential' in your question, we can actually begin to answer "are these two theorems related". These two theorems being that the genus of a smooth projective curve X/ℂ agrees with the topological genus of its associated Riemann surface Xan, and this holomorphic version of de Rham's theorem

The answer is, with this new understanding of differentials, yes! We can relate these two theorems.

There is something called the Hodge to de Rham spectral sequence that reads as follows

E1p,q=Hq⁢(X,ΩXan,holp)⟹ℍp+q⁢(X,ΩXan,hol∙) this is purely coming from the definition of hypercohomology, and the spectral sequence for a total complex.

Now, it's a deep theorem (morally equivalent to the Hodge decomposition) that this degenerates sequence degenerates on the first page. But, in the case of curves it's much simpler. Namely, the first pages looks like the following

⋮a⁢m⁢p;⋮a⁢m⁢p;⋮a⁢m⁢p;⋮a⁢m⁢p;0a⁢m⁢p;0a⁢m⁢p;0a⁢m⁢p;0a⁢m⁢p;⋯H1⁢(Xan,𝒪Xan)a⁢m⁢p;→a⁢m⁢p;H1⁢(Xan,ΩXan⁢hol1)a⁢m⁢p;0a⁢m⁢p;⋯H0⁢(Xan,𝒪Xan)a⁢m⁢p;→a⁢m⁢p;H0⁢(Xan,ΩXan,hol1)a⁢m⁢p;0a⁢m⁢p;⋯

But, H0⁢(Xan,𝒪Xan)→H0⁢(X,ΩXan,hol1) is just differentiation of constants (so zero), and H1⁢(Xan,ΩXan,hol1)→H1⁢(Xan,𝒪Xan) is just the Serre dual to this map, and so also zero. So, we do have degeneration on the first page.

Thus, we see that

Hsing1⁢(Xan,ℂ)≅ℍ1⁢(ΩXan,hol∙)=H1⁢(Xan,𝒪Xan)⊕H0⁢(Xan,ΩXan,hol1) and so, finally using the GAGA principle, to say that

H1⁢(X,𝒪X)≅H1⁢(Xan,𝒪Xan)  H0⁢(X,ΩX1)≅H0⁢(Xan,ΩXan,hol1) we derive the isomorphism

Hsing1(Xan,ℂ)≅H1(X,𝒪X)⊕H0(X,ΩX1)  (**)

Why is this good? Well, Serre duality tells us that since we're on a smooth curve,

H1⁢(X,𝒪X)≅H0⁢(X,ΩX1)∨

Moreover, we know precisely what the first singular cohomology of Xan, it's just ℂ2⁢g⁢(Xan) (where g⁢(Xan) denotes the topological genus). Thus, comparing dimensions in (**) we see that

2⁢g⁢(Xan)=dim⁡(H1⁢(X,𝒪X)⊕H0⁢(X,ΩX1))=2⁢dim⁡H0⁢(X,ΩX1)=2⁢g⁢(X)

where g⁢(X) denotes the genus of X/ℂ as a smooth projective (algebraic) curve. So, we're done!
