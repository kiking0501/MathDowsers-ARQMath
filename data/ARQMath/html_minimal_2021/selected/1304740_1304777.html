He means first category or second category in the sense of Baire.

A set is of first category if it can be written as a countable union of sets whose closure has empty interior. (A set whose closure has empty interior is commonly called nowhere dense, but I won't use this term in the remainder of this answer.) Otherwise it is of second category. If you aren't already familiar, the closure of a set A is the smallest closed set which contains A; the interior of A is the largest open set which is contained in A. If you're not familiar with, for instance, how you can think of ℤ as a subset of ℝ vs. ℤ as a subset of itself with the topology inherited from ℝ, I would suggest you look up the term "subspace topology".

Let's look at the part about the integers. Purely in set theory (no topology here), we can write ℤ=⋃n=-∞∞{n}, which is a countable union.

In the topology of ℝ, the closure of {n} is {n} and the interior of {n} is ∅ (because {n} does not contain any intervals). So we've shown that ℤ is of first category as a subset of ℝ.

In the topology of ℤ, the closure of {n} is still {n}. However, in the topology of ℤ, sets with only one element are actually open as well as closed. You can see this because {n}={m∈ℤ:|m-n|<1/2}, which is the intersection of an open interval with ℤ. This means that the interior of {n} is {n}. That is, we have written ℤ as a countable union of sets, but the closures of these sets do not have empty interior. The Baire category theorem says that in fact you cannot write ℤ as a countable union of sets whose closure has empty interior, because ℤ is a complete metric space.

The situation is different for ℚ, which is not a complete metric space (its completion is ℝ). As a result the answer to 1b turns out to be different than the answer to 2b.
