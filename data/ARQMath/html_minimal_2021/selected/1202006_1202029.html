As mentioned in the comments and in the other answers, the issue with your answer is that although it proves that it does not diverge to infinity, it might still diverge.

As for a counterexample, It can be shown that ∑n=1∞1n2=π26>1

The summation ∑n=1∞(-1)n diverges since the sequence of partial sums alternates between -1 and 0, never stopping on a single repeated entry.

So: ∑n=1∞-1n2≤∑n=1K-1n2≤∑n=1K(-1)n≤∑n=1K1n2≤∑n=1∞1n2 for all K, and the series on the left and on the right both converge, however the one in the center does not.

In regards to your edit and recent comment: you add that you had noted on your exam that -1n2≤sin⁡(-5n2)n2≤1n2 for each n.

In that case, then that precisely the same thing as saying that |sin⁡(-5n2)n2|≤1n2 for each n, which is what you wanted to do. In doing so, you can continue by noting that ∑n=1K|sin⁡(-5n2)n2|≤∑n=1K1n2 for each K (since term-by-term the summands are comparable).

Noting that the series with absolute values has strictly positive entries, it follows that ∑n=1∞|sin⁡(-5n2)n2|≤∑n=1∞1n2=π26 and so ∑n=1∞|sin⁡(-5n2)n2| converges. (This follows from theorem since for any series where all summands are positive, the sequence of partial sums is monotonically increasing. Any monotonic bounded sequence is convergent)

By theorem, any absolutely convergent series will also be convergent (see a proof here). Therefore ∑n=1∞sin⁡(-5n2)n2 converges as well.

In fact, for any f⁢(n) such that -1n2≤f⁢(n)≤1n2 for all n the proof is identical and will also have ∑n=1∞f⁢(n) be convergent.

Even so, if I were the one grading, I would be expecting to hear the statements of the theorems used (that positive-term series bounded above by a convergent series is convergent, and that absolutely convergent series are convergent).

You could forgo some of the trouble of this by noting that sin⁡(-5n2) is always negative and so -1⋅∑n=1∞sin⁡(-5n2)n2=∑n=1∞-sin⁡(-5n2)n2≤∑n=1∞1n2=π26 since -sin⁡(-5n2)n2 is always positive, skipping the necessity of using the linked theorem.
