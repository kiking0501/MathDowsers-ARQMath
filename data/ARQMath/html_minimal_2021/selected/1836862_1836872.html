Importance of the homogeneity assumption in definition of linear map

Let V and W be vector spaces over field F. A function f:V→W is said to be linear if for any two vectors x and y in V and any scalar α∈F, the following two conditions are satisfied:

  1. f⁢(x+y)=f⁢(x)+f⁢(y)
  2. f⁢(α⁢x)=α⁢f⁢(x)

Let F be a field of real numbers. Is it possible to construct f such that the first condition is satified but not the second one?

The axiom is there for a reason. You can show that assuming (1), f has to be homogeneous with respect to elements on ℚ. I would pick V=W=ℝ and play around with this fact.
There might be also some connections with max-plus algebras and topical functions.

Relevant: [Overview of basic facts about Cauchy functional equation](http://math.stackexchange.com/questions/423492/overview-of-basic-facts-about-cauchy-functional-equation?lq=1)

A function f:ℝ→ℝ such that f⁢(x+y)=f⁢(x)+f⁢(y) is known as a Cauchy additive function. This would be a special case example when V=W=ℝ. Assuming the axiom of choice, there do exist such functions without the property that f⁢(α⁢x)=α⁢f⁢(x), but such functions are incredibly ugly, for example they aren't continuous anywhere.

The proof that such functions exist is non-constructive (because it uses the axiom of choice). This means that you can't actually construct an example of one of them.
