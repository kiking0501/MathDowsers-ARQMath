A different approach: integrate "a la Feynman". Let F⁢(t)=∫0∞ln⁡(1+t2⁢x2)1+x2⁢𝑑x. Then, differentiating inside the integral, \begin{align*} F'(t)&=\int_0^\infty\frac{2\,t\,x^2}{(1+t^2\,x^2)(1+x^2)}\,dx\\ &=\frac{2\,t}{1-t^2}\int_0^\infty\Bigl(\frac{1}{1+t^2\,x^2}-\frac{1}{1+x^2}\Bigr)\,dx\\ &=\frac{2\,t}{1-t^2}\Bigl(\frac1t\arctan(t\,x)-\arctan x\Bigl)\Bigr|_0^\infty\\ &=\frac{2\,t}{1-t^2}\Bigl(\frac{\pi}{2\,t}-\frac\pi2\Bigr)\\ &=\frac{\pi}{1+t}. \end{align*} Integrating F⁢(t)=π⁢log⁡(1+t)+C. Since limt→0+⁡F⁢(t)=F⁢(0)=0, C=0 and ∫0∞log⁡(1+t2⁢x2)1+x2⁢𝑑x=π⁢log⁡(1+t). Of course, differentiating under the integral sign and taking the limit as t→0+ must be justified.
