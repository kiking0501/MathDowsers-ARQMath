Note that this is a weak result. You maximize the function by solving quartics etc and getting x≈0.6879 giving 0.5487<916, so they are probably looking for some trick to come up with the weaker result.
Since the maximum of sin⁡(x)⋅sin⁡(3⁢x) is 916 (still quite lengthy to calculate) and sin⁡(2⁢x) has a maximum value of 1, you are left to proof, that there is no x0 where sin⁡(2⁢x0)=1 and sin⁡(x0)⋅sin⁡(3⁢x0)=916, which would mean the expression is never ≥916.

@Coodey This approach at first glance ignores the fact that the factors might be negative. The max of -sin⁡x⁢sin⁡3⁢x is 1 (at x=π/2, for example)

We have the following equality:

sin⁡(x)⁢sin⁡(3⁢x)=cos⁡(3⁢x-x)-cos⁡(3⁢x+x)2=cos⁡(2⁢x)-cos⁡(4⁢x)2=c-2⁢c2+12

where c=cos⁡(2⁢x). The function f⁢(x)=-2⁢x2+x+1 attains its global maximum at x=1/4, which means that we have

sin⁡(x)⁢sin⁡(2⁢x)⁢sin⁡(3⁢x)≤sin⁡(x)⁢sin⁡(3⁢x)=f⁢(cos⁡(2⁢x))2≤f⁢(1/4)2=916

if at most one of sin⁡(x),sin⁡(2⁢x),sin⁡(3⁢x) are negative.

Note: To show that the inequality is strict, it suffices to observe that sin⁡(2⁢x)<1 when cos⁡(2⁢x)=1/4.
