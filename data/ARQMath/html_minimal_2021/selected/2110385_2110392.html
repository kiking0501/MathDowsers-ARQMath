Technique in the demonstration

Let's say I want to prove that:

A⇒B or C.

Assume that B is false and use this and the assumptions (A) to arrive at C.

I think it's fundamentally important that you add parentheses to this expression.
A⟹(B⁢or⁢C)?
See here: https://www.physicsforums.com/threads/proving-an-or-statement.366427/
You should probably proceed along the lines, "If B is true, then (B or C) is true, so the implication A⟹(B or C) is true. If B is false, then ...", arriving at the desired implication in either case. You should explicitly show the implication holds whether B is true or false.
See the post [conditionals involving disjunctions](http://math.stackexchange.com/questions/2104937/conditionals-involving-disjunctions).

Yes, that is precisely the right approach. If either of your assumptions don't hold, then the statement is true by assumption, since F⇒_____ is always true, independent of the ____, and T⇒(T∨ ____) is always true, independent of the ____, since it simplifies to T⇒T. Therefore the case you specify is the only case that needs to be proven to prove that the theorem is true.
