One may adopt the approach as in Pranav Arora's comment. But this approach involves a double integral whose calculation seems painful. So here is an indirect approach that makes calculation slightly easier (at least to me):

Let us consider the following integral: for α,β∈ℂ∖(-∞,0] and 0<s<1,

I=I⁢(s,α,β):=∫0∞log⁡(1+α⁢x)⁢log⁡(1+β⁢x)x2+s⁢𝑑x,

Differentiating w.r.t. α and β, we have

∂2⁡I∂⁡α⁢∂⁡β=∫0∞d⁢xxs⁢(1+α⁢x)⁢(1+β⁢x).

Using standard complex analysis technique (you man use keyhole contour), it follows that

∂2⁡I∂⁡α⁢∂⁡β=πsin⁡π⁢s⁢βs-αsβ-α ⟹ I=πsin⁡π⁢s⁢∫0α∫0βxs-ysx-y⁢𝑑x⁢𝑑y.

Replace β by i⁢β (with β>0). Then (1) yields

2⁢I⁢(s,α,i⁢β)=2⁢πsin⁡π⁢s⁢∫0α∫0βis⁢xs-ysx+i⁢y⁢𝑑x⁢𝑑y.

Now assume that α,β>0. Taking real parts of the identity above and taking s→1-, it follows that

\begin{align*} \tilde{I}(\alpha, \beta) &:= \int_{0}^{\infty} \frac{\log(1+\alpha x)\log(1+\beta^{2}x^{2})}{x^{3}} \, dx \\ &= \int_{0}^{\alpha}\int_{0}^{\beta} \frac{2xy \log(y/x) + \pi x^{2}}{x^{2}+y^{2}} \, dxdy. \tag{2} \end{align*}

In particular, when β=α, by symmetry we retrieve the following formula

I~⁢(α,α)=π⁢∫0α∫0αx2x2+y2⁢𝑑x⁢𝑑y=π2⁢∫0α∫0α𝑑x⁢𝑑y=π2⁢α2.

which also follows from the formula in OP's posting. In general, using polar coordinates shows that we have

I~⁢(α,β)=β2⁢J⁢(α/β)-α2⁢J⁢(β/α)+π⁢α⁢β2+π2⁢β24-π⁢(α2+β2)2⁢arctan⁡(β/α),

where J is defined by

J⁢(x)=∫0xt⁢log⁡t1+t2⁢𝑑t.

This function can be written in terms of elementary functions and dilogarithm.

Remark. Though we have derived this formula for positive α,β, by the principle of analytic continuation (3) continues to hold on the region containing (0,∞)2 where both sides of (3) are holomorphic.
