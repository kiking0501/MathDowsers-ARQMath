Writing an alternating series as two non-alternating series?

How does one calculate ∑n=1∞(-1)n+1n2

if given this information: ∑n=1∞1n2=π26.

How does one account for the (-1)n+1 in the first series?

Note: in this earlier post, user David Hodden (thanks) pointed out that the following simplification can be made:

∑n=1∞(-1)n+1⁢1n2=∑n=1∞1n2-2⋅14⁢∑n=1∞1n2=π212

What I do not understand is where this simplification came from. Why can the alternating series be broken up into two non-alternating series?

The information given tells you (in addition to the value) that the alternating series is *absolutely* convergent. This gives you greater freedom to manipulate it.

There are two kind of terms in series ∑n=1∞(-1)n+1n2: terms with sign + and terms with sign -. Note that:

∑n=1∞(-1)n+1n2=112-122+132-142+⋯

So + occurs with terms for odd n and -: for even terms. So let's divide this sum into two sums: for even and odd n:

∑n=1∞(-1)n+1n2=∑n=1⁢n is odd∞(-1)n+1n2+∑n=1⁢n is even∞(-1)n+1n2

You know that - occurs for odd n and + for even, so you can write down this equality in different way:

∑n=1∞(-1)n+1n2=-∑n=1⁢n is odd∞1n2+∑n=1⁢n is even∞1n2

Next you can write down ∑n=1⁢n is even∞1n2 in different way:

∑n=1⁢n is even∞1n2=∑n=1∞1(2⁢n)2=14⁢∑n=1∞1n2=π224

On the other hand:

∑n=1⁢n is odd∞1n2=∑n=1∞1n2-∑n=1⁢n is even∞1n2=π26-14⁢∑n=1∞1n2=π26-π224
