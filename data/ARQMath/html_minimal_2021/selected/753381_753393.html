Limit x2⁢yx4+y2 is found using polar coordinates but it is not supposed to exist.

Consider the following 2-variable function:

f⁢(x,y)=x2⁢yx4+y2

I would like to find the limit of this function as (x,y)→(0,0).

I used polar coordinates instead of solving explicitly in ℝ2, and it went as the following:

x=r⁢cos⁡θ,y=r⁢sin⁡θ

Hence,

lim(x,y)→(0,0)⁡x2⁢yx4+y2=limr→0⁡r2⁢cos2⁡θ⁢(r⁢sin⁡θ)r4⁢cos4⁡θ+r2⁢sin2⁡θ

This simplifies to,

limr→0⁡r3⁢cos2⁡θ⁢sin⁡θr2⁢(r2⁢cos4⁡θ+sin2⁡θ)

Simplifying r3/r2, we finally get;

limr→0⁡r⁢(cos2⁡θ⁢sin⁡θ)r2⁢cos4⁡θ+sin2⁡θ

Now from the above, we find that as r→0 the limit is 0.

I wanted to verify this answer so I checked on Wolfram Alpha. Explicitly without changing to polar coordinates, it said that the limit does not exist at (0,0) and rightly so. Then how is it that with polar coordinates, the limit exists and is 0? Am I doing something wrong in this method?

Also, what should I do in this situation, and when should I NOT use polar coordinates to find limits of multi-variable functions?

You haven't taken into account what happens if θ is variable as a function of r when r goes to 0. Choose θ so that sin⁡θ=r, i.e. θ is approximately r and you will get cos⁡θ is about 1 for small r, and then the limit will not be zero, so the limit doesn't exist.

If you want to use polar coordinates to show that a limit exists, particularly in the case where you want to show the limit is 0 as r→0, then if you factor out a positive power of r then you need to bound the remaining factor by either a constant or a multiple of a negative power of r that is lower than the positive power you factored out. In your case you can't do this because when sin⁡θ=r you can't produce such a bound for the expression after you factor out r. If you had something like r/(cos4⁡θ+sin4⁡θ) then you could bound 1/(cos4⁡θ+sin4⁡θ) by a constant for all θ and so you would then get that the limit is indeed 0 as r→0.
