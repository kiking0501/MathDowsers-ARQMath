Shortest distance between an ellipse and a hyperbola.

Suppose we have a hyperbola x⁢y=c (c≠0) and an ellipse x2/a2+y2/b2=1 (a>b>0) that do not intersect. What is a quick way to calculate the shortest distance between these two curves?

I thought of setting a point (a⁢cos⁡θ,b⁢sin⁡θ) on the ellipse and a point (t,c/t) on the hyperbola, then the distance is given by (a⁢cos⁡θ-t)2+(b⁢sin⁡θ-ct)2, whose minimum may be found by calculus methods. But the computations look tedious and difficult to carry out. What are some easier ways to do this?

There doesn't seem to be a nice closed-form solution to this, but the following might be helpful..

For an ellipse x2a2+y2a2=1, the equation of the normal at point P⁢(a⁢cos⁡θ,b⁢sin⁡θ) on the ellipse is

a⁢sin⁡θb⁢cos⁡θ⁢x-y=a⁢(a2-b2a⁢b)⁢sin⁡θ

A hyperbola which just touches or does not intersect at all with the ellipse above has the equation x⁢y=m⁢a⁢b2 where m≥1. The equation of the normal at point Q⁢(v,m⁢a⁢b2⁢v) on the hyperbola is 2⁢v2m⁢a⁢b⁢x-y=v⁢(2⁢v2m⁢a⁢b-m⁢a⁢b2⁢v2)

The minimum distance between the ellipse and hyperbola is the distance P⁢Q when (1)=(2), i.e. both normals are the same line.

As the coefficients of y are the same in both (1),(2), equating coefficients of x in (1),(2) gives v=a⁢m⁢tan⁡θ2 This relationship ensures that the tangents and normals at P,Q are parallel to each other respectively (but the normals are not necessarily the same line).

Putting (3) in (2) gives

(ab⁢tan⁡θ)⁢x-y=a⁢m⁢tan⁡θ2⁢(ab⁢tan⁡θ-ba⁢cot⁡θ)

To ensure that both normals are the same line, we need to equate RHS of (1),(4). This gives (a2-b2a⁢b)⁢sin⁡θ=m⁢tan⁡θ2⁢(ab⁢tan⁡θ-ba⁢cot⁡θ) which is equivalent to (a2-b2)⁢sin⁡θ=m⁢tan⁡θ2⁢(a2⁢sin2⁡θ-b2⁢cos2⁡θsin⁡θ⁢cos⁡θ)

Solve numerically θ in (4), find corresponding value v in (3), then calculate P⁢Q. This should give the minimum distance between the ellipse and hyperbola.

In the trivial case where a=b (i.e. ellipse is a circle), then θ=π4 and v=a⁢m2 . This gives P=(a2,a2) and Q=(a⁢m2,a⁢m2) and the distance P⁢Q=a⁢(m-1).
