An alternate way to understand what is happening here is to note that 1=e0⁢i When we take the square root, we have 1=e0⁢i=e-π⁢i×eπ⁢i Notice that e-π⁢i=eπ⁢i=-1.

Now, since we are working in polar form, we can evaluate the square roots consistently, arriving at 1=e-π⁢i/2×eπ⁢i/2=-i×i=1

Essentially, the problem lies in the "branch cut" that occurs with the square root operation - you must be careful with the evaluation.

To put it another way, 1=e2⁢n⁢π⁢i for all integer n, and the square root function has to respect its specific value (of n), as it can take multiple different values depending on that n. To get 1=-1 as in the question, one must simultaneously use 1=e0⁢i and 1=e2⁢π⁢i.
