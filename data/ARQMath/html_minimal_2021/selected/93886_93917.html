Limit does not exists, if you consider two paths of approach y=0 and x=y.
Try converting to polar coordinates.
Would you be able to calculate the limit if you assume that y=x2? Would you be able to do the same if you assume y=x? If these limits are different, it means that you can obtain different values if you approach (0,0) along different curves and, consequently, the limit in two variables does not exist.
In general, to show that the limit does not exist at a point, you may use "Two Path Test" which @sos440 used above.
See also http://math.stackexchange.com/questions/174190/prove-that-lim-limits-x-y-to-0-0-fracxy2x2-y4-0
More general solution : https://math.stackexchange.com/questions/66226/multivariable-limit-proof-lim-x-y-rightarrow-0-0-frac-leftx-righta

Another approach is to consider the change of variables, x2=r⁢cos⁡θ and y=r⁢sin⁡θ then 2⁢x2⁢yx4+y2=2⁢r2⁢cos⁡θ⁢sin⁡θr2=2⁢cos⁡θ⁢sin⁡θ which depends on θ and hence the limit doesn't exist since it's not unique.
