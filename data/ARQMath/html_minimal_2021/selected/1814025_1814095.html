This integral and many similar integrals have been popularized in Ovidiu Furdui's interesting book (you can find it in some sample free pages in a link at the bottom): http://www.springer.com/us/book/9781461467618?token=prtst0416p
frac⁢(⋯) and floor⁢(⋯) are usually written as {⋯} and ⌊⋯⌋, respectively.

Making the variable change, u=1-x, we have

∫1/21{1x}⁢{11-x}⁢𝑑x=∫01/2{11-u}⁢{1u}⁢𝑑u.

Hence,

∫01{1x}⁢{11-x}⁢𝑑x=2⁢∫01/2{1x}⁢{11-x}⁢𝑑x=2⁢limn→∞⁡∑k=2n∫1/(k+1)1/k{1x}⁢{11-x}⁢𝑑x.

For 1/(k+1)<x<1/k we have k<1/x<k+1 and 1+1/k<1/(1-x)<1+1/(k-1).

Hence, for k⁢\geqslant⁢2,

{1x}=1x-k,{11-x}=11-x-1,

and

∫01{1x}⁢{11-x}⁢𝑑x=2⁢limn→∞⁡∑k=2n∫1/(k+1)1/k(1x-k)⁢(11-x-1)⁢𝑑x.

Proceeding,

∫1/(k+1)1/k(1x-k)⁢(11-x-1)⁢𝑑xamp;=∫1/(k+1)1/k(1-k⁢x1-x)dxamp;=∫1/(k+1)1/k((1-k)+k⁢(1-x)1-x)dxamp;=[(k-1)log(1-x)+kx]1/(k+1)1/kamp;=(k-1)logk-1k-(k-1)logkk+1+1k+1amp;=klogk+1k-(k-1)logkk-1-[log(k+1)-logk]+1k+1

Summing, we get

∑k=2n∫1/(k+1)1/k(1x-k)⁢(11-x-1)⁢𝑑xamp;=∑k=2n1k+1-log(n+1)+nlogn+1namp;=-32+∑k=1n+11k-log(n+1)+log[(1+1n)n].

Taking the limit as n→∞ we find

limn→∞⁡∑k=2n∫1/(k+1)1/k(1x-k)⁢(11-x-1)⁢𝑑x=-12+γ,

We see the integral is symmetric by using the substitution u=1-x.
We write it as a sum over intervals [1/(k+1),1/k] because we can identify the fractional part of 1/x and 1/(1-x) easily as I show. Note that on the integration interval [0,1/2] we also know that 1/(1-x) is between 1 and 2 so the fractional part is just 1/(1-x)-1.
