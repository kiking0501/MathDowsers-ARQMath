  1. Any branch of the inverse cosine will work as long as the same choice is used consistently in all the later formulas.

  2. There isn't any arbitrary choice of sign for the trigonometric function, just consistency in maintaining whatever sign convention was specified as part of the meaning of the original x integral. Defining the x integrand involves a choice of sign for the square root function in 1-x2. Whatever choice is made, has to be kept when rewriting that function as 1-cos2‚Å°Œ±. Suppose the positive square root was intended; then 1-cos2=|sin|, no matter what angle appears "inside" the sine function. So this is a separate issue from the choice of Œ±.

  3. Yes, sin‚Å°(arccos‚Å°x) is not uniquely determined (for instance, using -Œ± or 2‚Å¢œÄ-Œ± as an alternative choice of arccos will reverse the sign of sin‚Å°arccos‚Å°x). But a careful calculation maintaining consistent choices as in points 1-2 above, would utilize only |sin‚Å°(Œ±)| or other quantities that are functions of cos‚Å°(Œ±) (that is, x) alone, so this apparent ambiguity is an illusion. After all, the process started from a function of x only. Whatever replacements of x quantities by Œ± quantities are performed, if traced back to x step by step, are ultimately calculable from cos‚Å°Œ± and thus do not depend on the particular choice of Œ±.

These comments apply to any trigonometric (or other) substitutions that involve choices or an apparent dependence of the post-substitution calculations on the choices. If all the choices are made explicit and performed consistently there should be no ambiguities.

Also, there is no forward or reverse direction in substitution, except in a psychological sense that one integral is given and the other is thought of as a simplification. The relation cos‚Å°(Œ±)=x translates integrals in Œ± to integrals in x, and vice versa. Which variable is considered as a replacement of the other is immaterial and does not affect calculations.
