There is no point applying formulas if you don't know what they mean. Formulas [7] and [8] are both correct but they are the answers to different problems. You need to read carefully further back in your textbook to see what all these formulas are calculating.
I would sort of recreate the formula each time. Imagine a little chunk of length d⁢s near (x,y). Rotate this chunk about the y-axis. The **radius** is x, so the surface area swept out is ≈2⁢π⁢x⁢d⁢s. Now "add up" (integrate).
Note that ∫2⁢π⁢x2⁢𝑑s cannot be a surface, because the dimension of x2 is an *area* and of d⁢s is a *length*. So, dimensionally, ∫2⁢π⁢x2⁢𝑑s is a *volume*.

I rather dislike the way a lot of textbooks present the material on surfaces and solids of revolution because they will at some point present a table of formulas without having clarified the underlying reasoning. Students are confronted with several rather similar-looking equations and wonder how they are going to memorize them.

What the surface area integral for a figure of revolution does is similar to what is done for the "shell method" in volume computation. For surface area, we are taking an infinitesimal bit of the arclength d⁢s of the curve and revolving it about the rotation (symmetry) axis for the surface. A particular bit of the curve is at a distance (radius) r from that rotation axis, so when it is "swung around" the axis, it "sweeps out" a "belt" with an infinitesimal area d⁢A= 2⁢π⁢r⁢d⁢s, which is defined by the infinitesimal arclength being carried around the circumference of a circle. (This is the resemblance to the "cylindrical shell" method for volume.) The generic integral for surface area of a figure of revolution is then

S=∫ 2⁢π⁢r d⁢s .

The "radius arm" to the curve is always perpendicular to the axis of rotation. So when the rotation axis is the x- axis, the radius arm is "vertical", making the integral S=∫ 2⁢π⁢y d⁢s ,

and when the rotation axis is the y- axis, the radius arm is horizontal and the integral become S=∫ 2⁢π⁢x d⁢s .

As a separate matter, there is the choice of the "direction of integration", which affects what we write for d⁢s. Since we work with "ordinary" Euclidean space in these calculus courses, the basic representation for an "infinitesimal element" of arclength is d⁢s2=d⁢x2+d⁢y2 . (This is, in a sense, the basis of the "distance formula" and the Pythagorean Theorem.) So in the surface area integral, we are always writing d⁢s=d⁢x2+d⁢y2 , but will need to extract a "differential factor" in accordance with our direction of integration.

So for integration in the x- direction, the differential to be used is d⁢x and the infinitesimal arclength element is written as

d⁢s=d⁢x2d⁢x2+d⁢y2d⁢x2 d⁢x = 1+(d⁢yd⁢x)2 d⁢x

and for integration in the y- direction, the differential to be used is d⁢y and the infinitesimal arclength element is written as

d⁢s=d⁢x2d⁢y2+d⁢y2d⁢y2 d⁢y = (d⁢xd⁢y)2+ 1 d⁢y .

[I'll mention, as an aside, that when you get to parametric curves described by coordinate functions x⁢(t),y⁢(t), the differential is d⁢t and we have d⁢s=(d⁢xd⁢t)2+(d⁢yd⁢t)2 d⁢t. ]

So we have four possibilities for surface area integrals, with two "choices" for rotation axis and two for direction of integration. I won't write all of them out, but the combinations you show as "Formulas (5) and (6)" are both for the x - axis being the rotation axis. For (5), the integration is along the x- direction, so y in the surface area integral needs to be replaced by f⁢(x) , and the integral should be written as

S=∫ 2πy ds =  2π∫abf(x)1+(d⁢yd⁢x)2 dx.

If we are taking the integration along the y- direction [Formula (6) ] because, say, we are working with a curve given by x=g⁢(y), then the radius arm y is left as is and we will need to find the derivative d⁢xd⁢y=d⁢gd⁢y. The surface area integral (still for the symmetry axis being the x- axis) is now

S=∫ 2πy ds =  2π∫cdy(d⁢xd⁢y)2+ 1 dy.

Perhaps it is clear from this how to construct the appropriate integrals for a surface produced by rotation about the y- axis.

For the "Example 2" that you show, the surface has the y- axis as the symmetry or rotation axis, so the basic integral is S=∫ 2⁢π⁢x d⁢s. Since the integration is being taken in the x- direction, that "radius arm" x is left as is and the d⁢x- form for d⁢s is used.

If the figure is produced by revolving about the y- axis, then the integral is ∫2⁢π⁢x⁢𝑑s (the radius is always perpendicular to the rotation axis). If you are integrating in the x- direction as well, then that factor "x" is left as such. If you are integrating in the y- direction, then y is the independent variable, so the factor "x" needs to be expressed as a function of y, for which you use the function for the curve.
