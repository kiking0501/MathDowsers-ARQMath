You want to show that A countable ⟹An countable. If A is countable there is f:A→ℕ injective. We can create a new injection g:An→ℕn for any n, defined (a1,a2,…,an)↦(f⁢(a1),f⁢(a2),…,f⁢(an)). To show An countable it will suffice to find an injection h:ℕn→ℕ; then the composition h∘g will give the desired map An→ℕ.

The famous injective example ℕ×ℕ→ℕ is sometimes called the Cantor pairing function. Rather than duplicating the explanation here I'd refer you to this Math Stack Exchange thread and this Wikipedia article. The idea is to represent ℕ×ℕ as a grid, and count the elements, starting at (0,0) and weaving along. (This is the basis for showing ℚ and ℕ are of the same cardinality.)

Now, if k:ℕ×ℕ→ℕ is our injection, consider k^:(ℕ×ℕ)×ℕ→ℕ defined ((n,m),l)↦k⁢(k⁢(n,m),l). The argument extends inductively.
