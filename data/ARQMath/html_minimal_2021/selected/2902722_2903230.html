Modus ponens as a rule of inference vs. tautology

When I look up the mechanics of a propositional logic system here we have a collection of both rules of inference (a⊢b) and axioms (⊢b), if I understand right.

One such rule of inference is modus ponens, p,(p→q)⊢q which on a metalogical level is "from p and p→q, infer q".

However I have also seen people "prove" it using truth tables or treating it as a tautology by stating it as (p∧(p→q))→q.

Why do I see it stated these two ways if the latter is not technically modus ponens? Is it totally and utterly wrong to even ask the question how to "prove modus ponens" because it's considered a rule of inference, i.e. it has no proof since it's a metalogical assumption? Why do I sometimes see it stated the second way, if so, and how do we know that's the correct interpretation as a tautological claim?

I know informally it makes sense as "if a then b" or "whenever a is true, b is true" but the whole ⊢ concept seems to be a little different from → (especially since ⊢ is metalogical) and so I'm trying to understand what's going on here.

To prove (p∧(p→q))→q. using truth tables, you have to use the truth table for material implication, which is usually presented simply as a definition. Each line of that truth table, however, can also be derived using well-known principles of logic including MP, direct proof (introducing →), proof by contradiction and double negation.
Modus ponens is usually the name of the *rule of inference* : "from P and P→Q, derive Q". But we may call *MP* also the tautology : ((P∧(P→Q))→Q).

"Why do I see it stated these two ways if the latter is not technically modus ponens?"

Because people don't think about this much or are confused.

" Is it totally and utterly wrong to even ask the question how to "prove modus ponens" because it's considered a rule of inference, i.e. it has no proof since it's a metalogical assumption?"

Given that "proof" means "formal proof" where a formal proof has every step being a well-formed formula, then yes. Why? Because {(p->q), p} ⊢ q (I note in passing that {p, q} and {q, p} are the same set, so I referred to the same rule of inference as you did) is not a well-formed formula in the same context where "formal proof" applies to for propositional calculus.

"Why do I sometimes see it stated the second way, if so, and how do we know that's the correct interpretation as a tautological claim?"

At the very least, the well-formed formula (one you would parenthesize it) is just one well-formed formula. The antecedent is a single well-formed conjunction also, and sometimes conjunction is not a primitive, and sometimes not even a defined connective, for a propositional calculus. On the other hand, {(p->q), p} consists of a set of two well-formed formulas.

⊢ is not a connective between propositions. If we where to write:

"If p, if (p->q) also, then q" we would have stepped outside of propositional calculus, since "if", "then", "also, and "," are not part of the language for well-formed formulas in propositional calculus. In other words, they are not part of the "formula language".

However, "⊢" doesn't happen inside of the formula language. If we wrote:

"If p, if (p>q) also, ⊢ q"

we haven't stepped outside of the meta-language where ⊢ comes as permissible, since there doesn't exist any formation rules prescribing the correct or incorrect use of ⊢ other than it gets used intelligibly. So, as long as the above comes as comprehensible to the reader, it qualifies as correct. That differs from the use of (well-formed) formulas, where every string used needs to follow the formation rules to stay in the language.
