Here is a sketch of another elementary solution based on a proof in Bromwich's Theory of Infinite Series.

Using sin⁡(2⁢k+1)⁢x-sin⁡(2⁢k-1)⁢x=2⁢cos⁡2⁢k⁢x⁢sin⁡x and summing from k=1 to k=n we have sin⁡(2⁢n+1)⁢x=sin⁡x⁢(1+2⁢∑k=1ncos⁡2⁢k⁢x),

and hence ∫0π/2sin⁡(2⁢n+1)⁢xsin⁡xdx=π/2.  (1)

Let y=(2⁢n+1)⁢x and this becomes ∫0(2⁢n+1)⁢π/2sin⁡y(2⁢n+1)⁢sin⁡(y/(2⁢n+1))⁢𝑑y=π/2.

and since limn→∞⁡(2⁢n+1)⁢sin⁡y2⁢n+1=y it suggests that there is a proof lurking in there somewhere.

So let's put Inamp;=∫0n⁢π/(2⁢n+1)sin⁡(2⁢n+1)⁢xsin⁡xdxamp;=∑k=0n-1∫k⁢π/(2⁢n+1)(k+1)⁢π/(2⁢n+1)sin⁡(2⁢n+1)⁢xsin⁡xdx.

Hence we have In=u0⁢–⁢u1+u2⁢⋯+(-1)n-1⁢un-1, where uk is a decreasing sequence of positive terms. We can see this from the shape of the curve y=sin⁡(2⁢n+1)⁢x/sin⁡x, which crosses the x-axis at π/(2⁢n+1),2⁢π/(2⁢n+1),…,n⁢π/(2⁢n+1). (I said that this is just a sketch, you have to check the details.)

Hence the sequence In converges, and by (1) it converges to π/2.

Now if we make the substitution y=(2⁢n+1)⁢x we see that uk=∫k⁢π(k+1)⁢πsin⁡y(2⁢n+1)⁢sin⁡(y/(2⁢n+1))⁢𝑑y,

and since In can be written as an alternating sequence of decreasing positive terms we can truncate the sequence wherever we like and the value of In lies between two successive partial summations. Hence

∫02⁢m⁢πsin⁡y(2⁢n+1)⁢sin⁡(y/(2⁢n+1))dy&lt;In&lt;∫0(2⁢m+1)⁢πsin⁡y(2⁢n+1)⁢sin⁡(y/(2⁢n+1))dy.  (2)

for any m such that 2⁢m+1≤n. (Take m=[n], say, n≥6.)

Now |sin⁡yy-sin⁡y(2⁢n+1)⁢sin⁡(y/(2⁢n+1))|<π2⁢(2⁢m+1)23⁢(2⁢n+1)2 and so this difference tends to zero uniformly in the interval 0≤y≤(2⁢m+1)⁢π and so by taking the limn→∞ in (2) we obtain ∫0∞sin⁡xx⁢𝑑x=π2.
