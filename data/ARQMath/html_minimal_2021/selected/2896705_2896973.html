I suspect it is unrealistic to expect middle school/high school algebra students to be able to solve the general form of this problem without it being multiple choice, even if n is known to be an integer. Consider a similar, simpler problem: xx=4. By inspection, x=2 solves the equation, but let's try to get there.

xx=4

l⁢o⁢g2⁢(xx)=l⁢o⁢g2⁢4

x⁢l⁢o⁢g2⁢x=2

Now what? Elementary algebra doesn't appear to have the right tools to solve it; however, elementary algebra does have the tools to check a solution.

Does x=2 solve xx=4?

Yes, 22=4

Back to the original problem. Using only elementary algebra, there needs to be some guesswork. If the question is multiple choice as this one is, the guesswork is mostly done. The following algebra could lead to the proper guesswork even without pre-determined choices for the students, but I didn't follow this line of reasoning until I found the answer with other methods.

let x=n11⟹n=x111

nn132=nx12=x(111)x12=xx1211=xx11⁢(x11)

By inspection x=11⟹x2=121=n22
