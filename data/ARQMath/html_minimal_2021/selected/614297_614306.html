Note that equivalence classes ARE NEVER EMPTY. If anything π∈[π]S. You can show that [x]S is always countable by construction a bijection between [x]S and ℚ.

The quotient set (also group, but you mentioned that you don't want algebraic arguments) is not made of rational numbers, but rather of an element from each equivalence class. By knowing that the equivalence classes are countable, you can easily prove that there must be uncountably of them, because their union is ℝ.
