Does a closed form exist for this summation?

How do I calculate ∑k=1∞k⁢sin⁡(k⁢x)1+k2 for 0<x<2⁢π? Wolfram alpha can't calculate it, but the sum surely converges.

calculus summation
First thing to see is that S(x) is the derivative of an other sum, T⁢(x)=∑k=1∞-cos⁡(k⁢x)1+k2
[Wolfram Functions](http://functions.wolfram.com/ElementaryFunctions/Sin/23/02/) says that ∑k=1∞k⁢sin⁡(k⁢x)a2+k2=π⁢sinh⁡(a⁢(π-x))2⁢sinh⁡(a⁢π). But how to prove it?
I think you can translate this sum in to a integral and then try to use the contour integral methods to derive this formula.

Using known Fourier series expansion sinh⁡(a⁢t)=2⁢sinh⁡π⁢aπ⁢∑n=1∞(-1)n+1⁢n⁢sin⁡n⁢tn2+a2,t∈(-π,π) (which can be easily proven simply by calculating the Fourier coefficients for this function) and noting that (-1)n+1⁢sin⁡n⁢t=sin⁡((π-t)⁢n) we get the series in question taking a=1 and t=π-x: ∑n=1∞n⁢sin⁡n⁢xn2+1=π⁢sinh⁡(π-x)2⁢sinh⁡π,x∈(0,2⁢π).
