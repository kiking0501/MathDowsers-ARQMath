Strengthening of a lemma in Ramsey Theory: R⁢(m,n)≤R⁢(m-1,n)+R⁢(m,n-1)-1 if both numbers are even

A cool lemma in Ramsey theory is the following:

R⁢(m,n)⁢≤⁢R⁢(m-1,n)+R⁢(m,n-1).

It is not hard to prove it. However, a friend of mine said that, if R⁢(m-1,n) and R⁢(m,n-1) are both even, then

R⁢(m,n)⁢≤⁢R⁢(m-1,n)+R⁢(m,n-1)-1.

How can I prove it?

inequality graph-theory ramsey-theory

Consider that you have a graph of R⁢(m-1,n)+R⁢(m,n-1)-1 points. And you want a clique or an independent set.

Now take any point if it has an edge with ≥R⁢(m-1,n) points then by induction you are done. If it has an edge with ≤R⁢(m-1,n)-2 points then it will have a non edge with ≥R⁢(m,n-1) points and again you are done. So one is left with the case where the chosen point has exactly R⁢(m-1,n)-1 edges.

In the language of graph theory it has degree R⁢(m-1,n)-1. Now you are able to chose this beginning point at will, thus if at least one point does not have degree R⁢(m-1,n)-1, you are done.

Finally the case where all points have degree R⁢(m-1,n)-1 is impossible since 2⁢(# of edges)=(# of vertices)⋅degree =(R⁢(m-1,n)+R⁢(m,n-1)-1)⁢(R⁢(m-1,n)-1) and under your assumptions this last number is odd so it cannot be 2⁢(# of edges) thus at leas one point does not have degree R⁢(m-1,n)-1.

An example of this is R⁢(3,4)=9.

But R⁢(2,4)+R⁢(3,3)=4+6=10.
