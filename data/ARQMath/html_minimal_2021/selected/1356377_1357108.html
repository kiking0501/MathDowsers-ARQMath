@Leandro, you can prove (by induction, for example) this inequality for integer a and after some manupulations extends it to all a.

Let xn=an/na and then we have xn+1xn=an+1(n+1)a⋅naan=a⁢(nn+1)a→a⁢ as ⁢n→∞ Now a>1 and hence we can choose a number k with 1<k<a and by the above limit there exists a positive integer m such that xn+1xn>k for all n≥m. Thus we can see that xm+nxm>kn for all positive integers n. Now we can see that k>1 and hence kn=(1+(k-1))n≥1+n⁢(k-1) so that kn→∞ as n→∞. It follows that xm+n>xm⁢kn and hence xm+n→∞ as n→∞. Thus xn→∞ as n→∞.

It is now obvious that after a certain value of n we will always have xn>1 and hence an>na after a certain value of n. There is no need to go for logarithms and complicated limits related to them. Just simple limits coupled with definition of limit is sufficient to handle the problem.
