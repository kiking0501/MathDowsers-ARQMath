Then (x+y)k+1=(x+y)⁢(x+y)k≥(x+y)⁢(x+k⁢y) by our inductive hypothesis and as (x+y)≥0. Now, "foiling,' we see that (x+y)⁢(x+k⁢y)=x2+k⁢x⁢y+x⁢y+k⁢y2≥x2+(k+1)⁢x⁢y. Thus, (x+y)n≥x+n⁢y for all positive natural n.

There is one step that is not (always) valid. The inequality

x2+(k+1)⁢x⁢y⁢\geqslant⁢1x⁢(x2+(k+1)⁢x⁢y)

only holds for x+(k+1)⁢y⁢\geqslant⁢0, if x>1. If x+(k+1)⁢y<0 and x>1, you have a strict inequality in the other direction,

x2+(k+1)⁢x⁢y<1x⁢(x2+(k+1)⁢x⁢y).

However, in that case, the desired inequality follows from

(x+y)k+1⁢\geqslant⁢0>x+(k+1)⁢y.

For x+k⁢y⁢\geqslant⁢0 (for x+k⁢y<0, we have the sharper (x+y)k⁢\geqslant⁢0>x+k⁢y), the Bernoulli inequality yields a sharper bound per

(x+y)k=xk⁢(1+xy)k⁢\geqslant⁢xk⁢(1+k⁢xy)=xk+k⁢xk-1⁢y=xk-1⁢(x+k⁢y),

which may explain why your inequality is not mentioned in the literature.
