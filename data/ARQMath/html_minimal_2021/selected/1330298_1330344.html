Find the sum of reciprocals of divisors given the sum of divisors

Let d1,d2,⋯⁢dk be all the factors of a positive integer 'n' including 1 and n. Suppose d1+d2+d3+⋯+dk=72. Then find the value of 1d1+1d2+⋯+1dk.

Attempt: Consider, d1,d2,⋯⁢dk are the factors of n ascending order. Then

d1⁢dk=n, d2⁢dk-1=n, ⋯, dk⁢d1=n ............... (1)

Assume, 1d1+1d2+⋯+1dk=P Then nd1+nd2+⋯+ndk=n⁢P i.e dk+dk-1+⋯+d2+d1=n⁢P (using 1) i.e 72=n⁢P, or P=72/n.

Problem: I have considered that number of divisors of n are even as every divisor d of n has a twin divisor n/d. But it is not true if n is a perfect square i.e n=d2 as in this case n has odd number of divisors (for example, 4=22, 4 has three divisors 1,2&a⁢m⁢p;4).

The amusing thing is that the OEIS listing stops at _n_ = 70, so of course _n_ = 71 isn't included.

Let p,q,r be prime.

n=p: ∑d=1+p, thus p=71, which is indeed prime.

n=pk: ∑d=1+p+p2+…+pn, thus p⁢(pk-1+…+p+1)=71, which is not possible since p,p+1>1 and 71 is prime.

n=p⁢q: ∑d=1+p+q+p⁢q, thus (p+1)⁢(q+1)=72.

  • p=2 gives q=23. n=46
  • p=3 gives q=17. n=51
  • p=5 gives q=11. n=55
  • p=7 gives nothing.

n=p2⁢q: ∑d=1+p+p2+q+p⁢q+p2⁢q, thus (p2+p+1)⁢(q+1)=72.

  • p=2 gives nothing.
  • p=3 gives nothing.
  • p=5 gives nothing.

n=pk⁢ql, k,l≥2: σ1⁢(n)≥(p2+p+1)⁢(q2+q+1)≥7⋅13>72.

If n=p⁢q⁢r, σ1⁢(n)≥σ1⁢(30)≥72, with first inequality equality iff n=30.

So the values of n for this holds are exactly:

n=30,46,51,55,71

And this gives the values for P as:

125,3623,2417,7255,7271

This shows that it could be bruteforced in a reasonable time (a quarter) but of course your argument is much more elegant.
