Smoothness of Fourier transform of 1|x|p

Consider the "function" (more precisely it is a tempered distribution) given by f:ℝn→ℝ, f⁢(x)=1|x|p, where 0<p<n. It can be calculated that the Fourier transform of f is given (upto a constant) by f^⁢(ξ)=1|ξ|n-p. Now, I am trying to prove that the Fourier transform of f is smooth eveywhere, except at the origin, which is clear from above. But I am unclear how to prove this without calculating the Fourier transform explicitly first. One should be able to exchange derivatives and decay, but the singularity at 0 is throwing me off. Any hints would be highly appreciated.

Let χ be a compactly supported smooth function that is equal to 1 near the origin. Write f1=f⁢χ and f2=f⁢(1-χ). Note that f1 is compactly supported, therefore its Fourier transform is real-analytic. The function f2 is smooth, but has heavy tail at infinity, which is the main issue.

Fix ξ0≠0. Let ϕ be a bump function that is equal to 1 near ξ0, and is zero in a neighborhood of the origin. The goal is to prove that f2^⁢ϕ is smooth. Equivalently, we must prove that f2*ϕˇ vanishes at infinity faster than any power of |x|.

Given a positive integer k, let ψ⁢(ξ)=|ξ|-2⁢k⁢ϕ⁢(ξ). Note that ψ is still smooth and compactly supported. Going back to space variable, observe that ϕˇ=Δk⁢ψˇ (Laplacian applied k times, ignoring some multiplicative constants.)

So, f2*ϕˇ=f2*Δk⁢ψˇ=(Δk⁢f2)*ψˇ. The benefit of all this juggling of Laplacians is that Δk⁢f2 decays at infinity faster than f2: namely, like |x|-p-2⁢k. Since ψˇ is a Schwartz function, it follows that (Δk⁢f2)*ψˇ is O⁢(|x|-p-2⁢k) as |x|→∞. This completes the proof.
