The convergence of ∫0∞sin⁡(x)x⁢𝑑x is well-known, and can be established by splitting the integral up into integrals of the form ∫2⁢k⁢π(2⁢k+2)⁢πsin⁡(x)x⁢𝑑x, with k a positive integer. Each of these integrals has a positive and negative component, and it is clear that |∫(2⁢k)⁢π(2⁢k+2)⁢πsin⁡(x)x⁢𝑑x|<2(2⁢k)⁢π-2(2⁢k+1)⁢π=1k⁢(2⁢k+1)⁢π. If the upper bound is not a multiple of 2⁢π, then there will be an additional contribution of ∫2⁢n⁢πR+2⁢n⁢πsin⁡(x)x⁢𝑑x, with n the last positive integer for which the upper limit is smaller than 2⁢π⁢n and R smaller than 2⁢π, but this integral is clearly less than R2⁢n⁢π. The integral ∫0R+2⁢n⁢πsin⁡(x)x⁢𝑑x is therefore smaller than 2⁢π+∑k=1n1k⁢(2⁢k+1)⁢π+R2⁢n⁢π, with the 2⁢π coming from the region from x=0 to x=2⁢π. Since the sum is convergent as n tends to ∞ (limit comparison to the p-series with p=2) and the additional piece tends to zero in this limit, we can say for sure that this integral converges. Showing that it is equal to π2 is a different matter, but it can be done in multiple ways (contour integration and relation to the Gamma function are the two that come to mind, but I am sure there are others).
