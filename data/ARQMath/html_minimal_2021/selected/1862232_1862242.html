Units in Euclidean domains

I'm studying basic Ring Theory. And in my textbook, the author states the definition of Euclidean domain:
An integral domain R is called to be a Euclidean domain precisely when there is a function f:R∖{0}→ℕ0, called degree function of R, such that:
(i) If a,b∈R∖{0} and there exists c∈R such that a⁢c=b then f⁢(a)≤f⁢(b).
(ii) a,b∈R with b≠0, then there exist q,r∈R such that a=b⁢q+r with r=0 or r≠0 and f⁢(r)⁢\lt⁢f⁢(b).

I know the fact: all units in R have smallest degree, and this question pops into my head:

I want to prove that all units have degree 0.

Unfortunately I have no idea for it. Can anyone has an answer for my question or give me a readable explanation about it? I really appreciate !

You can't prove that units have degree 0: if f:R∖{0}→ℕ0 is a degree function, also fk:R∖{0}→ℕ0,fk⁢(a)=f⁢(a)+k is a degree function as well, for every integer k≥-f⁢(1) (because f⁢(1) is the minimum value attained by f).

You can normalize your degree function, by defining f^⁢(a)=f⁢(a)-f⁢(1) and now units will have degree 0 with respect to f^.
