Linear Independence of different combinations of vectors

In a previously asked question I missed a key ingredient, which will be mentioned in this question, so please do not tag this "duplicate".

Let V be a finite dimensional vector space with dimension suitably large ( say > 7 ). Let α1,α2,α3,β1,β2,β3,γ∈V such that all of them are distinct. Now if,

(i) α1,α2,α3 are linearly independent

(ii) β1,β2,β3 are linearly independent.

(iii) α1,β1 are linearly independent.

(iv) α2,β2 are linearly independent.

(v) α3,β3 are linearly independent.

(vi) β1,β2,β3,γ are linearly independent.

Then prove or disprove (provide a counter example) whether α1,α2,α3,γ are linearly independent.

Moreover V in question can be any arbitrary (suitably) finite dimensional vector space. So I couldn't come up with anything.... Thanks in advance for help....

linear-algebra vector-spaces

Let α1,α2,α3,β1,β2,β3 be a linear independent list in V. Choose γ=α1+α2+α3. All of your points are satisfied, however α1,α2,α3,γ is obviously not linearly independent.
