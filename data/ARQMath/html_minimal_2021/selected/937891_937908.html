Solving Diophantine equations involving x,y,x2,y2

My father-in-law, who is 90 years old and emigrated from Russia, likes to challenge me with logic and math puzzles. He gave me this one:

Find integers x and y that satisfy both (1) and (2)

x+y2=8 x2+y=18

I found one solution by deriving this equation:

x⁢(x+1)+y⁢(y+1)=26

which means, I need to find two numbers that add up to 26, where each number is the product of two consecutive numbers. Through trial and error I found a solution. From college days I seem to remember that there is a more elegant way using the theory of Diophantine equations.

Can someone remind me or point to me to an easy to follow explanation of the method for solving problems like these?

Usually (as is the case with your problem) you need to try to 'factorise' - i.e. manipulate the equations to a form f(x,y)g(x,y)=c where c is some number. Try subtracting the two equations instead.
Yes, **Diophantine Analysis** is the right approach for this problem. However, sometimes it pays off to just look at the problem and think about it for a few seconds, test some small numbers as inputs, and try to get a "feeling" for what the right solution should look like. Here, I noted that if the solution is a pair of positive integers, then x will be larger than y. The pair x=4,y=2 solves this problem.
I don't think there's a more elegant way than you've suggested. Note that by using pronic (a.k.a. oblong) numbers x⁢(x+1) and y⁢(y+1), you are guaranteeing that your summands are positive, even if x or y is negative. Since |x|≥|y|, you need only consider (x⁢(x+1),y⁢(y+1)) from amongst the 14 pairs (26,0),(25,1),…⁢(13,13).

Plug in values of a and b obtained from (2) to get possible values for x and y (that are integers).
