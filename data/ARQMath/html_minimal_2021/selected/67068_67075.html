My solution is identical to acarchau's answer.

The second identity counts the number of nonempty sets with a distinguished representative; that is, all pairs (x,S) such that S⊆[n] with x∈S.

  1. You can pick a set consisting of k elements (in (nk) ways) and pick the representative in k ways for each choice of the set.
  2. You can pick the representative x∈[n] first, and then choose the other members of the set in 2n-1 ways.

Equating these two counts, we are done.
