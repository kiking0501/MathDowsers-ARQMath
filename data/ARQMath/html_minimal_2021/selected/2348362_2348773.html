This is perhaps best explained in terms of Pappus's Centroid Theorems:

Pappus's (1s⁢t) Centroid Theorem states that the surface area A of a surface of revolution generated by rotating a plane curve C about an axis external to Cand on the same plane is equal to the product of the arc length s of C and the distance d traveled by its geometric centroid (Pappus's centroid theorem). Simply put, S=2⁢π⁢R⁢L, where R is the normal distance of the centroid to the axis of revolution and L is curve length. The centroid of a curve is given by

𝐑=∫𝐫⁢𝑑s∫𝑑s=1L⁢∫𝐫⁢𝑑s

Pappus's (2n⁢d) Centroid Theorem says the volume of a planar area of revolution is the product of the area A and the length of the path traced by its centroid R, i.e., 2⁢π⁢R. The bottom line is that the volume is given simply by V=2⁢π⁢R⁢A. The centroid of a volume is given by

𝐑=∫A𝐫⁢𝑑A∫A𝑑A=1A⁢∫A𝐫⁢𝑑A

Thus we can say for your cases that

S=2⁢π⁢∫04y⁢𝑑s=2⁢π⁢∫04y⁢1+y′⁣2⁢𝑑x⁢V=π⁢∫04y2⁢𝑑x

This is just as stated in the OP. Notice that here, the length, L and the volume, V have cancelled out, thus disguising the true nature of the equations.

Now, clearly you can't have

V=π⁢∫04y2⁢𝑑x=π⁢∫04y2⁢1+y′⁣2⁢𝑑x

But more to the point, the volume pertains to the area under the curve, whereas, the term 1+y′⁣2 in the surface area equation refers to the area along the arc of d⁢s and it has nothing to do with the volume of revolution.
