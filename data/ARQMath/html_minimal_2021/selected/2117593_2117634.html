This is a type of ODE named "Clairaut's equation".

Suppose you have an equation in the form of:

y=x⁢y′+ψ⁢(y′)

Defining p=y′ we get

y=x⁢p+ψ⁢(p)

If you take the derivative of the equation you get:

p=1⋅p+x⋅d⁢pd⁢x+ψ′⁢(p)⋅d⁢pd⁢x

or

d⁢pd⁢x⁢(x+ψ′⁢(p))=0

So you get two solutions. Either d⁢pd⁢x=0 and p is constant (hence, you can see that y=a⁢x+ψ⁢(a)), or

x=-ψ′⁢(p) that's an algebraic equation that will give p⁢(x) and you can substitute it in the original equation:

y=x⁢y′+ψ⁢(y′)=-ψ′⁢(p)⋅p+ψ⁢(p) where p is a known function of x.

And if you have an equation of the form y=x⋅φ⁢(y′)+ψ⁢(y′) it's called "Lagrange's equation" (but φ⁢(y′)≠y′)
