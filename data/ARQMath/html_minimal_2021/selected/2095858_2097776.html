1st step:

First we suppose that cn=1n2.

In this case we have to prove the inequality:

∑j=1naj⁢bj+1≥2n⁢(∑j=1naj)⁢(∑j=1nbj)⁢(1)

We write:

1=∑j=1naj22+∑j=1nbj22=∑j=1n(aj2+bj2)2

and therefore it suffices to prove that:

∑j=1n(aj+bj)2≥4n⁢(∑j=1naj)⁢(∑j=1nbj)⁢(*)

But with the Cauchy-Schwarz inequality for the n-tuples (1,…,1), (a1+b1,…,an+bn) we get:

∑j=1n(aj+bj)2≥1n⁢(∑j=1n(aj+bj))2=1n⁢(∑j=1naj+∑j=1nbj)2

Since (x+y)2≥4⁢x⁢y,∀x,y∈ℝ for x=∑j=1naj and y=∑j=1nbj we finally prove (*), as we wanted.

2nd step:

Now, for the general case consider the numbers: xj=aj∑j=1naj2,yj=bj∑j=1nbj2,j∈{1,…,n}

Observe that ∑j=1nxj2=∑j=1nyj2=1. Thus we can apply the already proved inequality (1) for the x1,…,xn,y1,…,yn. Multiply both sides by ∑j=1naj2⁢∑j=1nbj2 and you are done.

For the equalities, you just have to check when equalities occur in the inequalities that I used. It is easy to do it and I leave it to you.

As the inequality is homogeneous in both ai and bi, the first step is enough +1.
