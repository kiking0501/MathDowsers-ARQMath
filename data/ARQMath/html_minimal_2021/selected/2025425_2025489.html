least-common-multiple

From max⁡{n1,n2}=3 we gather that either n1 or n2 must be 3, while the other must only be one of 0,1,2, or 3. From max⁡{n2,n3}=3 max⁡{n3,n1}=3 we find the same result must hold for the pairs n2,n3 and n3,n1. But how can this result hold for all 3 pairs? Clearly at least two of n1,n2,n3 must be 3 else the result would fail for one pair. The third ni in the trio may be any of 0,1,2,3. Now we must count how many ways there are to choose n1,n2,n3. There are (32) ways to choose two to be 3, and for the last we have 4 options. This gives 4⁢(32)=12 options for n1,n2,n3. Can you use similar logic to find m1,m2,m3?
