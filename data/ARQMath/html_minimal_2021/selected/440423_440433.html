You're given the polynomial f⁢(x)=-1+x+x2-x4+x6+x9-x10 You want to find polynomials f3 and j such that f⁢(x)⁢f3⁢(x)-(x11-1)⁢j⁢(x)≡1(mod3) So, you do the extended Euclidean algorithm on the polynomials f⁢(x) and x11-1, at every step doing all the computations modulo 3. This may be very messy: you start by dividing a degree 11 polynomial by a degree 10 polynomial, which could give you a degree 9 remainder; then you'll have to divide the degree 10 by that degree 9, which could give a degree 8 remainder; and so on, for quite a few steps; and after you're done with that, you have to build your way back up to f3⁢(x). It's no wonder wikipedia leaves out the work! It's probably best handled by a computer algebra system like Maple or Mathematica, rather than trying to do it by hand.

*This* example is certainly a candidate for a CAS, but I hope @datosh that you do take the time to try some smaller examples by hand so that you finally believe that "it works." Just pick a small polynomial for f⁢(x) and use x4-1 instead, and see what happens. If you pick unluckily you may find out you didn't start with a unit, but seeing the result should be educational.
