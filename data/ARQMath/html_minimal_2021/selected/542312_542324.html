Do there exist functions such that f⁢(f⁢(x))=-x?

I am wondering about this. A well-known class of functions are the "involutive functions" or "involutions", which have that f⁢(f⁢(x))=x, or, equivalently, f⁢(x)=f-1⁢(x) (with f bijective).

Now, consider the "anti-involution" equation f⁢(f⁢(x))=-x. It is possible for a function f:ℂ→ℂ to have f⁢(f⁢(z))=-z. Take f⁢(z)=i⁢z.

But what about this functional equation f⁢(f⁢(x))=-x for functions f:ℝ→ℝ, instead of f:ℂ→ℂ? Do such functions exist? If so, can any be described or constructed? What about in the more general case of functions on arbitrary groups G where f⁢(f⁢(x))=x-1 (or -x for abelian groups), f:G→G? Can we always get such f? If not, what conditions must there be on the group G for such f to exist?

There is no continuous solution. If f⁢(f⁢(x))=-x and f is continuous, then f is a continuous bijection ℝ→ℝ and therefore strictly monotone increasing or strictly monotone decreasing. But in either case f∘f must then be monotone increasing, contradiction.
