Is there a direct, elementary proof of n=∑k|nϕ⁢(k)?

If k is a positive natural number then ϕ⁢(k) denotes the number of natural numbers less than k which are prime to k. I have seen proofs that n=∑k|nϕ⁢(k) which basically partitions ℤ/n⁢ℤ into subsets of elements of order k (of which there are ϕ⁢(k)-many) as k ranges over divisors of n.

But everything we know about ℤ/n⁢ℤ comes from elementary number theory (division with remainder, bezout relations, divisibility), so the above relation should be provable without invoking the structure of the group ℤ/n⁢ℤ. Does anyone have a nice, clear, proof which avoids ℤ/n⁢ℤ?

I have a pretty cool inductive proof.

The Base Case is trivial.

Now, assume that for some positive integer m we had

∑m|nϕ⁢(m)=n

Now, I will show that for any prime power pd, we must have ∑m|pd⁢nϕ⁢(m)=pd⁢n

For the sake of convenience, we may assume that gcd⁡(p,n)=1. If this was not the case, just repeat the proof but instead of using n use nordp⁢(n) instead.

Now,

∑m|pd⁢nϕ⁢(m)amp;=∑m|nϕ(m)+∑p⁢|m|⁢p⁢nϕ(m)+…+∑pd⁢|m|⁢pd⁢nϕ(m)amp;=n+∑k|nϕ(p)ϕ(k)+∑k|nϕ(p2)ϕ(k)+…+∑k|nϕ(pd)ϕ(k)amp;=n+ϕ(p)*n+ϕ(p2)*n+…+ϕ(pd)*namp;=n(1+(p-1)+p(p-1)+p2(p-1)+…+pd-1(p-1))amp;=n(1+(p-1)pd-1p-1)amp;=n*pd

thus proving the inductive step thus completing the proof.

The base case consists of showing the equality holds for all primes n (I guess that's what you called trivial?) but you also need to prove that the inductive step does not just work for prime powers, but for any composite n=p⋅q.
