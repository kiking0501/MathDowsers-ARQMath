The answer to your first question is no. The answer to your second question is yes.

If N=1 then this is equivalent to asking whether b2/a<1 implies ∥[0-b/a0-b2/a]∥2<1, where b∈ℝ and a∈(0,∞).

But this seems false. Take a=1/2 and b=2/3. Then b2/a=8/9<1, however ∥[0-b/a0-b2/a]∥2=∥[0-4/30-8/9]∥2=4⁢139>1.

Probably this counterexample can be extended to all N∈ℕ by letting A=12⁢IN and B=23⁢IN.

On the other hand, for N=1 the above matrix will always have spectrum {-b2/a,0}. So the spectral radius will be b2/a<1.

The case for N>1 requires the block matrix property of determinants: det⁢(AB0D)=det⁢(A)⁢det⁢(D). Observe that the eigenvalues to your matrix are the solutions to 0=det⁢(-λ-A-1⁢B0-BT⁢A-1⁢B-λ)=det⁢(-λ)⁢det⁢(-BT⁢A-1⁢B-λ) which is true precisely when det⁢(-λ)=0 or else det⁢(-BT⁢A-1⁢B-λ)=0. The former implies λ=0, while the latter gives the negative eigenvalues to BT⁢A-1⁢B. It is well-known that the spectral radius is bounded above by the 2-norm. Hence ρ⁢(0-A-1⁢B0-BT⁢A-1⁢B)=ρ⁢(BT⁢A-1⁢B)≤∥BT⁢A-1⁢B∥2<1.
