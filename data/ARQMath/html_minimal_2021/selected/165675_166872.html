It's not possible for arbitrary F and n. For example, it's impossible if F is finite or ℝ, and n is at least 3.
Even where it's possible, I'm not sure if there's any general way (other than the trial-and-error method). Note that if f of degree n works over F, then it doesn't work over any extension of F that has one or more roots of f, so your construction is going to have to know a lot about F in order to work.

We will prove that there exists a finite Galois extension K/ℚ such that Sn = G⁢a⁢l⁢(K/ℚ) for every integer n≥1. We will follow mostly van der Waerden's book on algebra. You can also see his proof on Milne's course note on Galois theory. However, Milne refers to his book for a crucial theorem(Proposition 1 below) whose proof uses multivariate polynomials. Instead, we will use elementary commutative algebra to prove this theorem.

Notations

We denote by |S| the number of elements of a finite set S.

Let K be a field. We denote by K* the multiplicative group of K.

Let τ = (i1,…,im) be a cycle in Sn. The set {i1,…,im} is called the support of τ. Let σ∈Sn. Let σ = τ1⁢…⁢τr, where each τi is a cycle of length mi and they have mutually disjoint supports. Then we say σ is of type [m1,…,mr].

Definition 1 Let F be a field. Let f⁢(X) be a non-constant polynomial of degree n in F⁢[X]. Let K/F be a splitting field of f⁢(X). Suppose f⁢(X) has distinct n roots in K. Then f⁢(X) is called separable. Since the splitting fields of f⁢(X) over F are isomorphic to each other, this definition does not depend on a choice of a splitting field of f⁢(X).

Definition 2 Let F be a finite field. Let |F|=q. Let K/F be a finite extension of F. Let σ be a map: K→K defined by σ⁢(x)=xq for each x∈K. σ is an automorphism of K/F. This is called the Frobenius automorphism of K/F.

Definition 3 Let G be a permutation group on a set X. Let G′ be a permutation group on a set X′. Let f:X→X′ be a bijective map. Let λ:G→G′ be an isomophism. Suppose f⁢(g⁢x)=λ⁢(g).f⁢(x) for any g∈G and any x∈X. Then G and G' are said to be isomorphic as permutation groups.

Lemma 1 Let F be a field. Let f⁢(X) be a separable polynomial of degree n in F⁢[X]. Let K/F be a splitting field of f⁢(X). Let G=G⁢a⁢l⁢(K/F). Let S be the set of roots of f⁢(X) in K. Then G acts transitively on S if and only if f⁢(X) is irreducible in F⁢[X].

Proof: If f⁢(X) is irreducible, clearly G acts transitively on S.

Conversely, suppose f⁢(X) is not irreducible. Let f⁢(X)=g⁢(X)⁢h⁢(X), where g and h are non-constant polynomials in F⁢[X]. Let T be the set of roots of g⁢(X) in K. Since G acts on T and S≠T, S is not transitive. QED

Lemma 2 Let F be a field. Let f⁢(X) be a separable polynomial in F[X]. Let f⁢(X)=f1⁢(X)⁢…⁢fr⁢(X), where f1⁢(X),…,fr⁢(X) are distinct irreducible polynomials in F⁢[X]. Let K/F be a splitting field of f⁢(X). Let G=G⁢a⁢l⁢(K/F). Let S be the set of roots of f⁢(X) in K. Let Si be the set of roots of fi⁢(X) in K for each i. Then S=∪Si is a disjoint union and each Si is a G-orbit.

Proof: This follows immediately from Lemma 1.

Lemma 3 Let F be a finite field. Let K/F be a finite extension of F. Then K/F is a Galois extension and G⁢a⁢l⁢(K/F) is a cyclic group generated by the Frobenius automorphism σ.

Proof: Let |F|=q. Let n=(K:F). Since |K*|=qn-1, xqn-1=1 for each x∈K*. Hence xqn=x for each x∈K. Hence σn=1.

Let m be an integer such that 1≤m<n. Since the polynomial Xqm-X has at most qm roots in K, σm≠1. Hence σ generates a subgroup G of order n of A⁢u⁢t⁢(K/F). Since n=(K:F), G=A⁢u⁢t⁢(K/F). Since |A⁢u⁢t⁢(K/F)|=n, K/F is a Galois extension. QED

Lemma 4 Let F be a finite field. Let f⁢(X) be an irreducible polynomial of degree n in F⁢[X]. Let K/F be a splitting field of f⁢(X). Let σ be the Frobenius automorphism of K/F. Then G⁢a⁢l⁢(K/F) is a cyclic group of order n generated by σ.

Proof: Let α be a root of f⁢(X) in K. By Lemma 3, F⁢(α)/F is a Galois extension. Hence K=F⁢(α)/F
By Lemma 3, G⁢a⁢l⁢(F⁢(α)/F) is a cyclic group of order n generated by σ. QED

Lemma 5 Let F be a finite field. Let f⁢(X) be an irreducible polynomial of degree n in F⁢[X]. Let K/F be a splitting field of f⁢(X). Let G=G⁢a⁢l⁢(K/F). Let σ be the Frobenius automorphism of K/F. Let S be the set of roots of f⁢(X). We regard G as a permutation group on S. Then σ is an n-cycle.

Proof: This follows immediately from Lemma 4.

Lemma 6 Let F be a finite field. Let f⁢(X) be a separable polynomial in F[X]. Let f⁢(X)=f1⁢(X)⁢…⁢fr⁢(X), where f1⁢(X),…,fr⁢(X) are distinct irreducible polynomials in F⁢[X]. Let mi = deg fi⁢(X) for each i. Let K/F be a splitting field of f⁢(X). Let G=G⁢a⁢l⁢(K/F). Let σ be the Frobenius automorphism of K/F. Let S be the set of roots of f⁢(X). We regard G as a permutation group on S. Then σ is a permutation of type [m1,…,mr].

Proof: This follows immediately from Lemma 2, Lemma 3 and Lemma 5.

Lemma 7 Sn is generated by (k,n)⁢、⁢k=1⁢、⁢．⁢．⁢．⁢、⁢n-1.

Proof: Let (a,b) be a transpose on {1,…,n}. If a≠n and b≠n, then (a,b)=(a,n)⁢(b,n)⁢(a,n). Since Sn is generated by transposes, we are done. QED

Lemma 8 Let G be a transitive permutation group on a finite set X. Let n=|X|. Suppose G contains a transpose and a (n-1)-cycle. Then G is a symmetric group on X.

Proof: Without loss of generality, we can assume that X = {1,…,n} and G contains a cycle τ = (1,…,n-1) and transpose (i,j). Since G acts transitively on X, there exists σ∈G such that σ⁢(j) = n. Let k = σ⁢(i). Then σ⁢(i,j)⁢σ-1 = (k,n)∈G. Taking conjugates of (k,n) by powers of τ, we get (m,n),m=1,…,n-1. Hence, by Lemma 7, G=Sn. QED

Lemma 9 Let F be a finite field. Let n≥1 be an integer. Then there exists an irreducible polynomial of degree n in F⁢[X].

Proof: Let |F|=q. Let K/F be a splitting field of the polynomial Xqn-X in F⁢[X]. Let S be the set of roots of Xqn-X in K. It's easy to see that S is a subfield of K containing F. Hence S=K. Since Xqn-X is separable, |S|=qn. Hence (K:F)=n. Since K* is a cyclic group, K* has a generator α. Let f⁢(X) be the minimal polynomial of α over F. Since K=F⁢(α), the degree of f⁢(X) is n. QED

Lemma 10 Let f⁢(X)∈ℤ⁢[X] be a monic polynomial. Let p be a prime number. Suppose f⁢(X) mod p is separable in ℤ/p⁢ℤ⁢[X]. Then f⁢(X) is separable in ℚ.

Proof: Suppose f⁢(X) is not separable in ℚ. Since ℚ is perfect, there exists a monic irreducible g⁢(X)∈ℤ⁢[X] such that f⁢(X) is divisible by g⁢(X)2. Then f⁢(X) (mod p) is divisible by g⁢(X)2 (mod p). This is a contradiction. QED

Proposition 1 Let A be an integrally closed domain and let P be a prime ideal of A. Let K be the field of fractions of A. Let K~ be the field of fractions of A/P. Let f⁢(X)⁢∈⁢A⁢[X] be a monic polynomial without multiple roots. Let f~⁢(X)∈(A/P)⁢[X] be the reduction of f⁢(X) mod P. Suppose f~⁢(X) is also wihout multiple roots. Let L be the splitting field of f⁢(X) over K. Let G be the Galois group of L/K. Let S be the set of roots of f⁢(X) in L. We regard G as a permutation group on S. Let L~ be the splitting field of f~⁢(X) over K~. Let G~ be the Galois group of L~/K~. Let S~ be the set of roots of f~⁢(X) in L~. We regard G~ as a permutation group on S~.

Then there exists a subgroup H of G such that H and G~ are isomorphic as permutation groups.

Proof: See my answer here.

Corollary Let f⁢(X)∈ℤ⁢[X] be a monic polynomial of degree m. Let p be a prime number. Suppose f⁢(X) mod p is separable in ℤ/p⁢ℤ⁢[X]. Suppose f≡f1⁢…⁢fr (mod p), where each fi is monic and irreducible of degree mi in ℤ/p⁢ℤ⁢[X]. Let K/ℚ be a splitting field of f⁢(X). Let M be the set of roots of f⁢(X). G=G⁢a⁢l⁢(K/ℚ) can be regarded as a permutation group on M. Then G contains an element of type [m1,…,mr].

Proof: By Lemma 10, f⁢(X) is separable in ℚ⁢[X]. Let Fp = ℤ/p⁢ℤ⁢[X]. Let f~⁢(X)∈Fp⁢[X] be the reduction of f⁢(X) mod p. Let K~/Fp be a splitting field of f~⁢(X). Let G~ be the Galois group of K~/Fp. Let τ be the Frobenius automorphism of K~/Fp. Let M~ be the set of roots of f~⁢(X). We regard G~ as a permutation group on M~. By Lemma 6, τ is a permutation of type [m1,…,mr]. Hence the assertion follows by Proposition 1. QED

Theorem There exists a finite Galois extension K/ℚ such that Sn = G⁢a⁢l⁢(K/ℚ) for every integer n≥1.

Proof(van der Waerden): By Lemma 9, we can find the following irreducible polynomials.

Let f1 be a monic irreducible polynomial of degree n in ℤ/2⁢ℤ⁢[X].

Let g0 be a monic polynomial of degree 1 in ℤ/3⁢ℤ⁢[X]. Let g1 be a monic irreducible polynomial of degree n-1 in ℤ/3⁢ℤ⁢[X]. Let f2=g0⁢g1. If n-1=1, we choose g1 such that g0≠g1. Hence f2 is separable.

Let h0 be a monic irreducible polynomial of degree 2 in ℤ/5⁢ℤ⁢[X]. If n-2 is odd, Let h1 be a monic irreducible polynomial of degree n-2 in ℤ/5⁢ℤ⁢[X]. Let f3=h0⁢h1. Since h0≠h1, f3 is separable.

If n-2 is even, n-2=1+a for some odd integer a. Let h1 and h2 be monic irreducible polynomials of degree 1 and a respectively in ℤ/5⁢ℤ⁢[X]. Let f3=h0⁢h1⁢h2. If a = 1, we choose h2 such that h1≠h2. Hence f3 is separable.

Let f=-15⁢f1+10⁢f2+6⁢f3. Since each of f1,f2,f3 is a monic of degree n, f is a monic of degree n.

Then,

f≡f1 (mod 2)

f≡f2 (mod 3)

f≡f3 (mod 5)

Since f≡f1 (mod 2), f is irreducible. Let K/ℚ be the splitting field of f. Let G=G⁢a⁢l⁢(K/ℚ). Let M be the set of roots in K. We regard G as a permutation group on M.

Since f is irreducible, G acts transitively on M.

Since f≡f2 (mod 3), G contains a (n-1)-cycle by Corollary of Proposition 1.

Similarly, since f≡f3 (mod 5), G contains a permutation τ of type [2,a] or [2,1,a], where a is odd. Then τa is a transpose. Hence G contains a transpose.

Hence, G is a symmetric group on M by Lemma 8. QED
