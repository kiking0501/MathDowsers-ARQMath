If you know a bit about complex numbers and roots of unity, here is another approach:

The nth roots of unity are vertices of a regular n-gon. Let z=1 denote A1 and w=e2⁢π⁢ιn denote A2 in the complex plane. Then w2 denotes A3 and w3 denotes A4. Now, the given equation can be written as

1|1-w|=1|1-w2|+1|1-w3| Multiplying with |1-w| throughout, we get 1=1|1+w|+1|1+w+w2|

Now since |w|=1, pulling out |w| from the denominator of the second term in the R.H.S, 1=1|1+w|+1|1+w+w¯| Notice that w+w¯ is purely real and can be written w+1w, hence 1-w1+w+w2=1+w21+w+w2=1|1+w| Squaring both sides and using |z|2=z⁢z¯, (1+w21+w+w2)2=1(1+w)⋅1(1+1w)=w(1+w)2 Cross multiply, (1+w+w2+w3)2=w⁢(1+w+w2)2 and simplify to get 1+w+w2+w3+w4+w5+w6=0 Therefore, 1-w7=0 and w≠1, so w must be a 7th root of unity. Argue that n=7.
