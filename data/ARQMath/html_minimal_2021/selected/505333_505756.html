You can just use the constants from the equivalence of the vector 1- and 2-norms plus the definition of the operator norm. And yes, you can find for both bounds a matrix which makes one of the inequality to become equality.

You can use simply the fact that ∥x∥2≤∥x∥1≤n⁢∥x∥2 to get (upper bound in the numerator, lower bound in the denominator): ∥A∥1=maxx≠0⁡∥A⁢x∥1∥x∥1≤maxx≠0⁡m⁢∥A⁢x∥2∥x∥2=m⁢∥A∥2. Similarly for the other: ∥A∥2=maxx≠0⁡∥A⁢x∥2∥x∥2≤maxx≠0⁡∥A⁢x∥1(n)-1⁢∥x∥1=n⁢∥A∥1. Hence 1n⁢∥A∥2≤∥A∥1≤m⁢∥A∥2.

The constants α=1n and β=m are best in the sense that they are the tightest constants such that the bounds hold for all matrices, that is, there exist matrices Aα and Aβ for which one of the bounds is attained: 1n⁢∥Aα∥2=∥Aα∥1 and ∥Aβ∥1=m⁢∥Aβ∥2. These matrices can be, e.g.: Aα=[11⋯100⋯0⋮⋮⋮⋮00⋯0],Aβ=[10⋯010⋯0⋮⋮⋮⋮10⋯0].
