The defining property of a prime number is that p∣a⁢b⟹p⁢∣a⁢ or ⁢p∣⁢b

Thus, if p∣a2 then p∣a.

ADD I am guessing you define a number p to be prime if the only divisors of p are 1 and p itself. Euclid's lemma then gives the "true" definition (1) of a prime number. To get this result, you can use the well ordering principle, that any nonempty subset of the positive integers has a least element. Define first

DEF Let a,b be integers. Then the greatest common divisor of a,b is the unique positive integer d=(a,b) such that d∣a,b and whenever f∣a,b then f∣d.

Bezout's Lemma then proves both the existence and the uniqueness of d=(a,b), as follows:

PROP Let a,b be integers. Then (a,b) exists, and is unique.

P Consider the set ℤ⁢a+ℤ⁢b={x⁢a+y⁢b:x,y∈ℤ}. By considering possible cases of the sign of a,b, it is seen the set of positive elements of ℤ⁢a+ℤ⁢b is nonempty (for example, if a>0,b<0 then a-b>0 is in the set). Let d be the least positive element of ℤ⁢a+ℤ⁢b.

First, we show d is a common divisor. Indeed, write a=q⁢d+r and b=q′⁢d+r′ with either r=0,r<d and r′=0,r′<d. Then a-d⁢q,b-q′⁢d are elements of ℤ⁢a+ℤ⁢b (check they have the form x⁢a+y⁢b). Since r<d and r′<d is impossible by the definition of d, r=r′=0 so that d divides both a,b.

Now, we show d divides every element of ℤ⁢a+ℤ⁢b. Indeed, pick an element m in the set. Then m=q⁢d+r with r<d or r=0, by the division algorithm, and again m-q⁢d=r is in ℤ⁢a+ℤ⁢b so we must have r=0. We have shown thus that ℤ⁢a+ℤ⁢b={d⁢z:z∈ℤ}=ℤ⁢d. If f∣a,b then f∣a⁢x+b⁢y for any x,y. Since d is of this form by construction, f∣d. Thus d is a greatest common divisor. But if d′ is another one, by definition d∣d′ and d′∣d for they both greatest common divisors. Thus d=±d′; and since they are both positive, by definition, d=d′. ▲.

OBS The above (very, very important) result can be stated as

ℤ⁢a+ℤ⁢b=ℤ⁢(a,b)

Now Euclid's lemma comes in easily

LEMMA Suppose that (a,b)=1 and a∣b⁢c. Then a∣c.

P By Bezout's lemma, we can write a⁢x+b⁢y=1 for some integers x,y. Then c⁢a⁢x+b⁢c⁢y=c. Since a∣a⁢c and a∣b⁢c, a∣c⁢a⁢x+b⁢c⁢y=c.

Then, you move onto

LEMMA Let p be a prime. Then (p,a)=1⇔p∤a.

P Suppose p∤a. Since the only divisors of p are 1,p, then (p,a) can be either 1 or p. Since p∤a, p is not a common divisor of a and p; thus (a,p)=1. If p∣a then (p,a)=p≠1.

THM (Defining property of prime numbers) Let p>1 be a positive integer. Then p is prime if and only if for any a,b integers, p∣a⁢b⟹p∣a or p∣b.

P Suppose that p is a prime, and assume p∣a⁢b. If p∣a, there is nothing to prove. Assume thus that p∤a. The (p,a)=1, so Euclid's lemma says p∣b. By symmetry the claim follows by replacing b with a. Now suppose p is not prime. Then p has a divisor 1<a<p, and thus p=a⁢b with b=p/a. Then p∣a⁢b, but p∤a and p∤b.
