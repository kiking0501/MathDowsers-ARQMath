Let γ:[a,b]→ℝ2 be a path from (a,f⁢(a)) to (b,f⁢(b)). First of all, I will show we may take WLOG γ to be injective. Consider a point α=(p,q)∈ℝ2. Consider the set S={x∈[a,b];γ(x)=α}, which we suppose non-empty. Let c=inf⁡S, c′=sup⁡S. By the continuity of γ, we have γ⁢(c)=γ⁢(c′)=α. We may now consider the equivalence relation ∼ on [a,b] which sets x∼x and z∼y for z,y∈[c,c′]. The quotient space [a,b]/∼ is homeomorphic to an interval (since a<c≤c′<b, by the continuity of γ), and the induced map γ¯:[a,b]/∼→ℝ2 may be taken as a path which passes through α only once.

Since [a,b] is compact and ℝ2 is Hausdorff, γ is a homeomorphism onto its image. Clearly γ⁢([a,b])⊂G⁢(f). We must now show this is an equality, which is done via the intermediate value theorem. Consider π1:G⁢(f)→ℝ to be the projection onto the first coordinate, which is continuous. Consider ϕ=π1∘γ:[a,b]→[a,b], which also is continuous. Since ϕ⁢(a)=a,ϕ⁢(b)=b, ϕ⁢([a,b])=[a,b], and therefore γ⁢([a,b])=G⁢(f). Thus f is a function whose graph is homeomorphic to its domain. We will show in the next paragraph that this implies the continuity of f.

Indeed, suppose g:[a,b]→ℝ has a homeomorphism H:[a,b]→G⁢(g). This induces a map T:[a,b]→[a,b] such that H(x)=(T(x),g(T(x)). We note T is continuous since T=π1∘H. It is clearly seen T is bijective, and thus a homeomorphism since [a, b] is compact and Hausdorff. Since g∘T is continuous, g∘T∘T-1=g also is.

Actually, there is a problem when you state that you can take γ to be injective. Defining ∼ the way you did, the quotient space is \textbf{not} homeomorphic to a segment. You have to define it this way : ∀x∈[a,b], x∼x and ∀x,y∈[c,c′], x∼y. And this is not enough : this process removes only one of the loops "contained" in γ.
