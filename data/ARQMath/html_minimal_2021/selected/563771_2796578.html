The proof referred to in @lhf's comment shows that if g is differentiable on [a,b] then the derivative g′ takes **all** values in the interval [g⁢(a),g⁢(b)] (or the other way around, as necessary). Suppose f=g′, then this shows that f cannot 'skip' any values, hence cannot have a jump. The proof is straightforward.

Just trying to add some clarity to the tricky part of the proofs presented above.

Let f:[a,b]→ℝ be differentiable in (a,b) and c and interior point of the interval, c∈(a,b). We'll prove that limx→c+⁡f′⁢(x)=Alimx→c-⁡f′⁢(x)=B}⇒A=B=f′⁢(c) For each x∈(c,b), f is continuous in [c,x] and differentiable in (c,x) so we can apply the mean value theorem to f on the interval [c,x]. Thus, for each interval [c,x], there exists a d⁢(x)∈(c,x) such that f′⁢(d⁢(x))=f⁢(x)-f⁢(c)x-c Since f is differentiable at c, making x→c+ on the RHS of the expression gives: limx→c+⁡f⁢(x)-f⁢(c)x-c=f′⁢(c) The LHS of the equality is a bit trickier, we would like to say that limx→c+⁡f′⁢(d⁢(x))=limx→c+⁡f′⁢(x)=A but that is not obvious, since we can't assume f′ to be continuous (on the right) at c. However we know that limx→c+⁡f′⁢(x)=A, we'll use ϵ-δ definition of limit. Let ϵ>0, ∃δ>0: if ⁢0⁢<x-c⁢<δ⁢ then |⁢f′⁢(x)-A|<ϵ (notice that we write 0<x-c<δ instead of 0<|x-c|<δ because we're taking the right hand limit, that is x>c). The MVT guarantees that d⁢(x)∈(c,x), that is c<d⁢(x)<x⇒ 0<d⁢(x)-c<x-c But this means 0<x-c<δ⇒ 0<d⁢(x)-c<δ⇒|f′⁢(d⁢(x))-A|<ϵ thus limx→c+⁡f′⁢(d⁢(x))=A as desired. We've seen f′⁢(d⁢(x))=f⁢(x)-f⁢(c)x-c→x→c+A=f′⁢(c) Similarly, we can prove B=f′⁢(c) and we're done.

The answer to your question follows directly from this result.

Let f be differentiable in (a,c)∪(c,b). Assume f′ has a jump discontinuity at c: limx→c+⁡f′⁢(x)=A≠B=limx→c-⁡f′⁢(x) (to say we have a jump we require the limits to \textbf{exist} and to be different!). By the result, if f was also differentiable at c, we'd have A=B, that contradicts the hypothesis of having a jump: A≠B. Therefore f can't be differentiable at c.
