Counterexample(?) of the theorem An Orthonormal basis of a vector space X is a Hamel basis if and only if X is finite dimensional.

I found somewhere the following theorem.

An Orthonormal basis of a vector space X is a Hamel basis if and only if X is finite dimensional.

For Hilbert spaces it is quite easy to prove but for pre-Hilbert spaces I'm not really sure that this is true.

As a counterexample, the basis (ei) where each sequence ei is defined as ei(k)=δk⁢i is an orthonormal-Hamel basis in c00 with the inner product ||⋅||2, but the space c00 is not finite dimensional.

Where am I wrong? I know in some books they define Orthonormal basis in Hilbert spaces, but in my definition they can also exist in pre-Hilbert spaces. Is that the problem?

Yes, one needs completeness for the conclusion. In every Hilbert space H, the subspace spanned by an ONB is one where the ONB is also a Hamel basis. But that subspace is closed if and only if H is finite-dimensional.
Assuming c00 is the space of sequences that are eventually zero you're not wrong, this is all correct. If you really read that theorem stated for _vector spaces_ X then now you know you can't believe everything you read.

If X is infinite dimensional, then it possesses an orthonormal basis ℬ, which is clearly infinite dimensional (Zorn's Lemma is required to prove it).

We shall show that ℬ is NOT a Hamel basis.

Let {en}n∈ℕ⊂ℬ be an orthonormal sequence and w=∑k=1∞2-k⁢ek. It can be readily shown that the w is perpendicular (and hence linearly independent) to the elements of ℬ∖{en}n∈ℕ and linearly independent with the elements of {en}n∈ℕ.

In the same way one can construct 2ℵ0 such elements.
