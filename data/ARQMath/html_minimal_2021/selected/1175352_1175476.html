Is there a typo in statement B? You have n inside the absolute value indexed by n+1 and x outside the absolute value.

We claim that the first two conditions do not imply the convergence of the sequence, while the latter two do imply convergence.

For A and B, simply take ∀n:xn:=ln⁡n.

For C and D, let us first introduce a Cauchy sequence:

{xn}n is Cauchy if ∀ϵ′,∃N,∀n,m≥N:|xn-xm|<ϵ′.

There is another practical way to say this (I'll leave it to you to prove that they are equivalent):

{xn}n is Cauchy if ∀ϵ′,∃N,∀n≥N,∀p:|xn+p-xn|<ϵ′.

Next, it's practical to keep in mind that ℝ is complete.

Now D says precisely that the sequence is Cauchy.

Finally let us see that C implies that the sequence is Cauchy using the alternative statement of Cauchyness. To do this we also need to recall that the p-series converges for p>1 (see wikipedia). Set M:=∑1/n2.

Let ϵ′>0. Pick ϵ:=ϵ′M>0 in C, and let n0 be the corresponding index. Set N:=n0. Let n≥N and p∈ℕ. Then we have |xn+p-xn|≤|xn+p-xn+p-1|+…+|xn+1-xn|<ϵ(n+p)2+…+ϵ(n+1)2=ϵ′M⁢(1(n+p)2+…+1(n+1)2)<ϵ′. Observe that the last inequality follows from that (1(n+p)2+…+1(n+1)2) is nothing but a part of the 2-series (to be precise it is the difference of two partial sums).
