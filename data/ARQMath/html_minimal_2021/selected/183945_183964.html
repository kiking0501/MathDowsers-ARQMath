Why does a time-homogeneous Markov process possess the Markov property?

Klenke defines (Definition 17.3, p. 346) a time-homogeneous Markov process independently, rather than as a special case of a stochastic process that possesses the Markov property (Definition 17.1, p. 345) and in addition satisfies certain further constraints. He then goes on to claim that "a time-homogeneous Markov process is simply a stochastic process with the Markov property and for which the transition probabilities are time-homogeneous" (Remark 17.4, p. 346).

How is the Markov property implied by the definition of a time-homogeneous Markov process?

Relevant Definitions

Setting Let I⊆[0,∞) and (Xt)t∈I be an ℝn-valued stochastic process vis-a-vis (ℱt)t∈I, the natural filtration generated by (Xt)t∈I.

Definition 17.1 We say that X has the Markov property (MP) iff for every A∈ℬ⁢(ℝn) and all s,t∈I with s≤t, P[Xt∈A|ℱs]=P[Xt∈A|Xs]

Definition 17.3 Let I be closed under addition and assume 0∈I. A stochastic process X=(Xt)t∈I is called a time-homogeneous Markov process with distributions (Px)x∈ℝn on the space (Ω,𝒜) iff:

  1. For every x∈ℝn, X is a stochastic process on the probability space (Ω,𝒜,Px) with Px[X0=x]=1.
  2. The map κ:ℝn×ℬ⁢(ℝn)⊗I→[0,1], (x,B)↦Px[X∈B] is a stochastic kernel.
  3. X has the time-homogeneous Markov property: For every A∈ℬ⁢(ℝn), every x∈ℝn and all s,t∈I, we have Px[Xt+s∈A|ℱs]=κt(Xs,A)Px-a.s. Here, for every t∈I, the transition kernel κt:ℝn×ℬ⁢(ℝn)→[0,1] is the stochastic kernel defined for x∈ℝn and A∈ℬ⁢(ℝn) by κt(x,A):=κ(x,{y∈ℝI:y(t)∈A})=Px[Xt∈A]

The family (κt⁢(x,A),t∈I,x∈ℝn,A∈ℬ⁢(ℝn)) is also called the family of transition probabilities of X.
