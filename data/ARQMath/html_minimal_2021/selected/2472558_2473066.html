So long as the limit limx→c⁡f⁢(x)=0 and g⁢(x) is bounded within the interval (c-ϵ,c+ϵ) for some ϵ>0 then limx→c⁡(f⁢(x)⁢g⁢(x))=0. Similarly if limx→∞⁡f⁢(x)=0 and g⁢(x) is bounded to the right, then again limx→∞⁡(f⁢(x)⁢g⁢(x))=0. By bounded to the right, I mean there exists some real X and some real N such that for all x>X you have |g⁢(x)|≤N. If g is unbounded, then more care needs to be taken.

The usual variant of product rule of limits is as follows:

Product rule of limits: If both limx→a⁡f⁢(x) and limx→a⁡g⁢(x) exist then limx→a⁡f⁢(x)⁢g⁢(x) also exists and limx→a⁡f⁢(x)⁢g⁢(x)=limx→a⁡f⁢(x)⋅limx→a⁡g⁢(x) The above statement holds if a is replaced by symbols ∞,-∞.

The main limitation of the rule is that it does not say anything about the cases when one or both of the limits limx→a⁡f⁢(x),limx→a⁡g⁢(x) do not exist. Most limit questions tend to involve such cases.

A slight but very useful improvement over the above is that the equation (1) holds provided one of the limits of f,g exists and is non-zero. If the limit of one of the functions is 0 then one can not proceed without the knowledge of the limit of the other function.

From your wording of the question it appears that you also want to deal with the case when f⁢(x)=0 identically. In this case the whole product is 0 and the limit is also 0.
