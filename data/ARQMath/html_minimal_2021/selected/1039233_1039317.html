Trying to bring back old memories of my teacher's very good elementary proof ... I'll try to give a sketch and hopefully some intuition for 1. (2. is trivial as per @Jason's comment):

First note that your integral can be written as ∫-ππf(t)cos(nt)dt=1n∑k=-nn-1∫k⁢π(k+1)⁢πf(u/n)cos(u)du==1n∑k(-1)k∫0πf(v+k⁢πn)cos(v)dv

Now when n→∞, v↦f⁢(v+k⁢πn) becomes very "flat" over [0,π] while over the same interval ∫0πcos⁡(v)⁢𝑑v=0. So intuitively you would like to see something like ∫0πf⁢(v+k⁢πn)⁢cos⁡(v)⁢𝑑v∼∫0πf⁢(k⁢πn)⁢cos⁡(v)⁢𝑑v=0

To get to that, take ϵ>0. Since f is continuous on [0,π], it is also uniformly continuous (from the Heine-Cantor theorem), so there is a η=ηϵ>0 so that |x-y|<η⟹|f⁢(x)-f⁢(y)|<ϵ.

Now let's take an integer n>Nϵ=πϵ. You would have for all k's: ∀v∈[0,π],|f⁢(v+k⁢πn)-f⁢(k⁢πn)|<ϵ and thus (oh what a mouthful of LaTeX ...):

|∫0πf⁢(v+k⁢πn)⁢cos⁡(v)⁢𝑑v|=|∫0πf⁢(v+k⁢πn)⁢cos⁡(v)⁢𝑑v-∫0πf⁢(k⁢πn)⁢cos⁡(v)⁢𝑑v|≤∫0π|f⁢(v+k⁢πn)-f⁢(k⁢πn)|⁢|cos⁡(v)|⁢𝑑v≤ϵ⁢π

Now inject that in (1) and you get: n≥Nϵ⟹|∫-ππf⁢(t)⁢cos⁡(n⁢t)⁢𝑑t|≤2⁢π⁢ϵ

And you conclude limn→∞⁡∫-ππf⁢(t)⁢cos⁡(n⁢t)⁢𝑑t=0

Now I hope I haven't ridiculed myself with some major mistake...

The good thing with this proof is that you actually see what topological and functional properties are required to get the result:

  • the interval [0,π] is compact in ℝ
  • f is continuous and thus uniformly continuous
  • cos verifies ∫ϕ=0 and ∫|ϕ|<π on each interval [k⁢π,(k+1)⁢π]

It's probably easy to find examples that show that relaxing any of these conditions would void the result.
