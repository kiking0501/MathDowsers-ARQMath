Divisor of the cardinality of a group

Let G be an abelian group. Show that, if G is not cyclic, then for all x∈G, there is a divisor d of n=|G| which is strictly smaller than n satisfying xd=1.

I'm guessing that this is a consequence of Lagrange's Theorem. We can have that G is a disjoint union of left cosets that all have the same cardinality. So |H|<|G| since G is composed with more than just one left coset. By Lagrange's Theorem, we have that |H|=d and then d divides n. However the "if G is not cyclic" part is bothering me. Does the fact that G is not cyclic put a restriction?

The "not cyclic" assertion is for the "strictly smaller" condition.
If it were cyclic, you cannot guarantee such a thing. For example, ℤ7.
Then is the solution I provided correct? Oh, is it correct to say that the non cyclic part makes it that the cardinality of the group is not prime?

  • Consider E={e∈ℤ:xe=1⁢ for all ⁢x∈G}, the set of exponents of G.
    Then E is a subgroup of ℤ and so E=m⁢ℤ.

  • By Lagrange's Theorem, n∈E and so m divides n.

  • m is the lcm of the orders of all elements of G.

  • If a,b∈G with r=o⁢r⁢d⁢(a) and s=o⁢r⁢d⁢(b) and gcd⁡(r,s)=1, then o⁢r⁢d⁢(a⁢b)=r⁢s=l⁢c⁢m⁢(r,s).

The last two facts imply that there is an element of G having order m.

Thus, either G is cyclic or has exponent a proper divisor of n.
